### <b><i style="color:red;">I. Definitions and General Principles</i></b>

* <font color="red">"Plastikos"</font> is the Greek origin of the word plastic, meaning "to mold," referring to the ability to modify an object's shape without destruction.
* <font color="red">Plastic Surgery</font> is a subspecialty focused on techniques to modify tissues to restore both function and form via reconstructive and aesthetic methods.
* <font color="red">Reconstructive Surgery</font> primarily aims to restore function in congenital or acquired defects, with the restoration of form as a vital but secondary goal.
* <font color="red">Aesthetic (Cosmetic) Surgery</font> involves reshaping normal body structures to improve appearance and self-esteem, where patient satisfaction is the ultimate measure of success.

### <b><i style="color:red;">II. Reconstructive Surgery Scope</i></b>

| **Category** | **Pediatric Reconstruction** | **Adult Reconstruction** |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Common Etiology** | Congenital anomalies (e.g., Clefts, Synostoses). | Trauma (MVC, falls), tumor extirpation, chronic disease. |
| **Key Conditions** | <b><font color="red">Cleft Lip and Palate</font></b>, <b><font color="red">Craniosynostosis</font></b>, <b><font color="red">Romberg Syndrome</font></b>. | Maxillofacial fractures, Breast reconstruction, Pressure sores. |
| **Unique Finding** | <b><font color="red">Macrostomia</font></b>: "Joker-like" appearance where the lip extends to the lateral ear tragus. | <b><font color="red">Breast Reconstruction</font></b>: Should be offered concurrently with mastectomy for all cancer patients. |

* <font color="red">Craniosynostosis</font> is the abnormal fusion of skull suture lines leading to patterns such as frontal bossing or a cone-shaped head.
* <font color="red">Chronic Wounds</font> in adults, particularly in the Philippines, are often related to diabetes (foot ulcers) or cerebrovascular disease (venous/arterial ulcers).

### <b><i style="color:red;">III. Cleft Lip and Palate (CLP)</i></b>

* <font color="red">Clefting</font> is multifactorial and occurs during the **first trimester** (organogenesis), influenced by genetics, folate deficiency, toxins, or drugs (e.g., Valium, Dilantin).
* <font color="red">Simonart’s Band</font> is a bridge of soft tissue and skin that is pathognomonic for an <b><font color="red">Incomplete Cleft Lip</font></b>.
* <font color="red">Primary Palate</font> refers to structures anterior to the incisive foramen; the <b><font color="red">Secondary Palate</font></b> is posterior to the incisive foramen.
* <font color="red">Veau Classification</font> for cleft palate morphlogy: 
    1. Veau I: Midline, limited to the soft palate. 
    2. Veau II: Extends anteriorly to the posterior hard (secondary) palate. 
    3. Veau III: Complete unilateral cleft. 
    4. Veau IV: Complete bilateral cleft.
* <font color="red">Submucous Cleft Palate</font> presents as a hallmark triad: bifid uvula, a translucent midline (zona pellucida), and a palpable notch in the posterior hard palate.
* <font color="red">Levator Palatini</font> is arranged vertically in cleft cases; its failure to push the palate against the throat prevents the pronunciation of plosive sounds like /p/, /d/, and /b/.
* <font color="red">Hypernasality (Ngongo)</font> occurs when a <b><font color="red">Submucous Cleft Palate</font></b> or late repair causes levator muscle dysfunction.

### <b><i style="color:red;">IV. CLP Treatment Timeline</i></b>

| **Procedure** | **Ideal Timing** | **Key Goals/Concepts** |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Cleft Lip Repair** | 3 - 6 Months | **Rule of 10s**; Restore <b><font color="red">Orbicularis Oris</font></b> function for suckling. |
| **Cleft Palate Repair** | 10 - 12 Months | Achieving normal speech; avoids <b><font color="red">Maxillary Retrusion</font></b>. |
| **VPD Surgery** | 4 - 7 Years | Lengthening the palate to treat <b><font color="red">Velopharyngeal Insufficiency</font></b>. |
| **Alveolar Bone Graft** | 7 - 11 Years | Uses <b><font color="red">Iliac Crest Bone</font></b>; coincides with first permanent tooth eruption. |
| **Orthognathic Surgery** | Skeletal Maturity | Females: 18+; Males: 21+; Jaw surgery must precede rhinoplasty. |

* <font color="red">The Rule of 10s</font> for lip repair requires: 10 weeks of age, 10 lbs weight, and 10 g/dL Hemoglobin.
* <font color="red">Maxillary Retrusion (Yakmo)</font> is a risk if palate repair is done too early (before 10 months), while speech hypernasality is a risk if done too late (after 12 months).

### <b><i style="color:red;">V. The Reconstructive Ladder</i></b>

* <font color="red">Secondary Intention</font> is the simplest form of wound closure, where the wound is left open to heal naturally via scar tissue filling the gap.
* <font color="red">Primary Closure</font> involves surgically opposing wound edges using sutures, staples, or tissue adhesives.
* <font color="red">Skin Grafting</font> is the transfer of devascularized tissue from a donor to a recipient site, dependent on the recipient's blood supply for survival.
* <font color="red">Local Tissue Rearrangement (Flap)</font> involves moving uninjured skin immediately surrounding the wound; it has better cosmetic outcomes and lower contracture rates.
* <font color="red">Free Tissue Transfer</font> (Microvascular surgery) is the most complex level, involving the harvest of tissue with its blood supply (artery/vein) for anastomosis at a distant site.

### <b><i style="color:red;">VI. Wound Healing Phases</i></b>

| **Phase** | **Timeline** | **Primary Cell/Mechanism** |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Hemostasis & Inflammation** | First 48 - 72 hours | <b><font color="red">Platelets</font></b> (dominant); Neutrophils/Macrophages (scavengers). |
| **Proliferation** | Day 4 - 21 | <b><font color="red">Fibroblasts</font></b> (prominent); Type III Collagen deposition. |
| **Tissue Remodeling** | Day 21 - 1 Year | Type III replaced by <b><font color="red">Type I Collagen</font></b>; Scar flattens/matures. |

* <font color="red">Chronic Wounds</font> are defined as persisting for >6 weeks, often caused by <b><font color="red">Infection</font></b> with an organism count >10^5/gm of tissue.
* <font color="red">Myofibroblasts</font> are responsible for wound contraction during the proliferation phase, moving at a rate of 0.75–1 mm/day.

### <b><i style="color:red;">VII. Scar Formation & Management</i></b>

* <font color="red">Hypertrophic Scars</font> stay within the original borders of the injury and usually flatten/improve after 6 months.
* <font color="red">Keloids</font> grow beyond the original injury borders, are genetically predisposed, and are more common in pigmented races (Blacks, Southeast Asians).
* <font color="red">Silicone Gel Treatment</font> (sheets or sticks) is the conservative first-line management for excessive scar formation for at least 2 months.
* <font color="red">Intralesional Steroid Injections</font> are invasive treatments for stubborn or burn-related scars to soften and flatten them.

### <b><i style="color:red;">VIII. Primary Closure Factors & Suture Techniques</i></b>

* <font color="red">Relaxed Skin Tension Lines (RSTL)</font> are natural wrinkle lines; scars parallel to RSTL are inconspicuous, while perpendicular scars are always visible.
* <font color="red">Smoking</font> should be stopped 1 month before and after elective surgery because nicotine causes <b><font color="red">peripheral vasoconstriction</font></b> and poor blood supply.
* <font color="red">Simple Interrupted Suture</font> is the gold standard; it should result in slight <b><font color="red">wound edge eversion</font></b> to ensure the scar is level once healed.
* <font color="red">Vertical Mattress Suture</font> is excellent for wound edge eversion but must be removed early to prevent cross-hatching marks.
* <font color="red">Horizontal Mattress Suture</font> is the strongest suture, providing good hemostasis; it is ideal for thick skin on the feet and hands.
* <font color="red">Subcuticular Suture</font> is buried within the dermal layer, leaving only the entrance and exit visible, thus avoiding external suture marks.
* <font color="red">Face Suture Removal</font> should occur between 3-5 days post-op (as early as 2 days for eyelids) to prevent permanent hatch marks.

### <b><i style="color:red;">IX. Skin Grafting Principles</i></b>

| **Feature** | **Split-Thickness (STSG)** | **Full-Thickness (FTSG)** |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Composition** | Epidermis + Partial Dermis. | Epidermis + Complete Dermis. |
| **Contraction** | High <b><font color="red">Secondary Contraction</font></b> (shinks as it heals). | High <b><font color="red">Primary Contraction</font></b> (shinks immediately). |
| **Engraftment** | Higher success rate; low metabolic demand. | Lower success rate; high metabolic demand. |
| **Aesthetics** | Poor; risk of pigmentation. | **Best outcome**; more durable; less pigmentation. |

* <font color="red">Plasmatic Imbibition (Serum Inhibition)</font> is the first phase of graft take (24–48 hours) where the graft survives via nutrition diffusion from the wound bed.
* <font color="red">Inosculation</font> is the second phase (after 48 hours) where capillary buds from the bed and graft "kiss" and form loose anastomoses.
* <font color="red">Revascularization</font> is the final phase (day 5–10) where firm vascular anastomoses allow perfusion; dressings are usually not removed until this phase.

### <b><i style="color:red;">X. Wound Bed Preparation & Dressings</i></b>

* <font color="red">The TIME Concept</font> framework for wound assessment: 
    - **T**issue Management (Debridement)
    - **I**nfection/Inflammation Control
    - **M**oisture Balance
    - **E**dge of Wound (Epithelial advancement).
* <font color="red">Sharp Debridement</font> is the gold standard for removing necrotic tissue (T in TIME).
* <font color="red">Hydrogels</font> provide moisture to dry wounds or exposed bone/tendon to prevent desiccation.
* <font color="red">Hydrocolloids</font> gradually liquefy to allow <b><font color="red">autolytic debridement</font></b> of moderately exudative wounds (e.g., abrasions).
* <font color="red">NPWT (Negative Pressure Wound Therapy)</font> uses a suction system to promote angiogenesis, formation of granulation tissue, and shortening of the proliferation phase.

### <b><i style="color:red;">XI. Surgical Flaps and Blood Supply</i></b>

* <font color="red">Surgical Flaps</font> differ from grafts because they are transferred with their native vascular supply intact.
* <font color="red">Axial Pattern Flaps</font> are based on a named, identified artery (e.g., Median Forehead Flap based on the <b><font color="red">Supratrochlear Artery</font></b>).
* <font color="red">Random Pattern Flaps</font> rely on an unidentified blood supply from the subdermal plexus.
* <font color="red">Mathes and Nahai</font> classification for Musculocutaneous Flaps:
    - **Type 1:** One dominant pedicle (Tensor fascia lata).
    - **Type 2:** One dominant and minor supplemental supply (Gracilis).
    - **Type 3:** Two dominant pedicles; either can sustain the flap (Gluteus maximus).
    - **Type 4:** Multiple segmental vessels (Sartorius).
    - **Type 5:** One dominant and multiple segmental vessels (Latissimus dorsi).
* <font color="red">TRAM Flap</font> (Transverse Rectus Abdominis Myocutaneous) is a regional flap for breast reconstruction based on the <b><font color="red">Superior Epigastric Artery</font></b>.
* <font color="red">Angiosomes</font> are discrete tissue units supplied by a single artery, as discovered by Ian Taylor.

### <b><i style="color:red;">XII. High-Yield Comparisons for Exams</i></b>

* <font color="red">Reconstructive vs. Aesthetic Surgery</font>: Reconstructive deals with pathologic/congenital deformity; Aesthetic deals with normal tissue enhancement.
* <font color="red">STSG vs. FTSG Contraction</font>: <b><font color="red">STSG</font></b> undergoes more secondary contraction (shrinking as it heals); <b><font color="red">FTSG</font></b> undergoes more primary contraction (shrinking immediately upon harvest).
* <font color="red">Hypertrophic Scar vs. Keloid</font>: <b><font color="red">Hypertrophic scars</font></b> stay within wound borders and stabilize; <b><font color="red">Keloids</font></b> grow beyond borders and continue to enlarge indefinitely.
* <font color="red">Graft vs. Flap</font>: A <b><font color="red">Graft</font></b> is devascularized and relies on the recipient bed for blood; a <b><font color="red">Flap</font></b> maintains its own blood supply.
* <font color="red">Thin STSG vs. Thick STSG</font>: <b><font color="red">Thin grafts</font></b> have higher success/take rates but are less durable; <b><font color="red">Thick grafts</font></b> are more durable but have higher metabolic demands.
* <font color="red">Veau Class I vs. Class IV</font>: <b><font color="red">Veau I</font></b> involves only the soft palate; <b><font color="red">Veau IV</font></b> involves bilateral complete clefts of both primary and secondary palates.
* <font color="red">RSTL vs. Langer’s Lines</font>: <b><font color="red">RSTL</font></b> are observed in living patients (best for scar planning); <b><font color="red">Langer’s lines</font></b> were discovered in cadaveric skin.
* <font color="red">Imbibition vs. Inosculation</font>: <b><font color="red">Imbibition</font></b> is initial nutrition via diffusion; <b><font color="red">Inosculation</font></b> is the alignment and "kissing" of capillary buds.
* <font color="red">Primary vs. Tertiary Intention</font>: <b><font color="red">Primary intention</font></b> closes wounds immediately (fastest healing); <b><font color="red">Tertiary intention</font></b> (delayed primary closure) involves debriding and observing before grafting.
* <font color="red">Medial vs. Lateral CLP Repair</font>: In cheiloplasty, the <b><font color="red">Medial segment</font></b> is rotated downward while the <b><font color="red">Lateral segment</font></b> is advanced medially.
* <font color="red">Mathes/Nahai Type 3 vs. Type 5</font>: <b><font color="red">Type 3</font></b> has two dominant pedicles; <b><font color="red">Type 5</font></b> has one dominant pedicle and multiple segmental ones.
* <font color="red">Maceration vs. Desiccation</font>: <b><font color="red">Maceration</font></b> is tissue breakdown from too much moisture; <b><font color="red">Desiccation</font></b> is tissue death from a wound bed that is too dry.
* <font color="red">Alginate vs. Hydrogel</font>: <b><font color="red">Alginates</font></b> absorb heavy exudate; <b><font color="red">Hydrogels</font></b> add moisture to dry beds.
* <font color="red">Type I vs. Type III Collagen</font>: <b><font color="red">Type III</font></b> is laid down first during proliferation; <b><font color="red">Type I</font></b> is the stronger collagen used in the remodeling/scar maturation phase.

### <b><i style="color:red;">I. General Principles of Aesthetic Surgery</i></b>

*   The primary goal of <b><font color="red">Aesthetic Surgery</font></b> (also known as cosmetic surgery) is to reshape normal body structures to improve appearance and self-esteem.
*   The most important outcome parameter for <b><font color="red">Aesthetic Surgery</font></b> is patient satisfaction, rather than a strictly objective appearance, because beauty and self-esteem are subjective.
*   The guiding principles of <b><font color="red">Aesthetic Surgery</font></b> are based on the harmony of human proportions, the Vitruvian man, and the Golden ratio.
*   Clinical photography for <b><font color="red">Aesthetic Surgery</font></b> must be documented before surgery, intraoperatively, immediately post-op, and during the remodeling phase to track the evolution of the patient's appearance.
*   In <b><font color="red">Aesthetic Surgery</font></b> facial analysis, horizontal thirds divide the face into the upper (hairline to eyebrows), middle (eyebrows to nasal base), and lower (nasal base to chin) segments, which should ideally be equal.
*   The vertical facial proportions of <b><font color="red">Aesthetic Surgery</font></b> use the medial epicanthus as a landmark; the flare of the nasal ala should ideally align with the medial canthus.

### <b><i style="color:red;">II. Minimally Invasive and Non-Invasive Procedures</i></b>

| Feature | <b><font color="red">Soft Tissue Fillers</font></b> | <b><font color="red">Neuromodulators (Botox)</font></b> |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Primary Target** | Static lines (nasolabial folds, permanent depressions) | Dynamic lines (appear during muscle contraction) |
| **Mechanism** | Fills in unwanted contour differences or depressions | Blocks Acetylcholine release at neuromuscular junction |
| **Common Areas** | Nasolabial folds, lips, cheeks, temples | Glabellar lines, crow's feet, forehead lines |
| **Material** | Hyaluronic Acid (most common), Calcium hydroxylapatite | Botulinum Toxin A |
| **Duration** | 6 - 15 months (variable) | 4 - 6 months |

*   <b><font color="red">Hyaluronic Acid Fillers</font></b> are characterized by "G prime" (hardness/viscosity); lower G' is smoother for lips, while higher G' is used for malar (cheek) prominences.
*   <b><font color="red">Calcium Hydroxylapatite (Radiesse)</font></b> is a filler that stimulates natural collagen growth and is used for lipodystrophy or creating a "chiseled" jawline in men.
*   <b><font color="red">Poly-L Lactic Acid (Sculptra)</font></b> is an FDA-approved filler specifically for HIV-associated lipodystrophy.
*   <b><font color="red">Ablative Lasers (CO2)</font></b> are considered the gold standard for improving facial wrinkles but require a longer downtime of 1–2 weeks and carry a risk of hyperpigmentation in Asians (Fitzpatrick type 3+).
*   <b><font color="red">Chemical Peels</font></b> are categorized into three levels: 1) Superficial (salicylic/glycolic acid), 2) Moderate (TCA), and 3) Deep (Hetter's/Baker-Gordon).
*   <b><font color="red">Dermabrasion</font></b> involves the mechanical removal of top skin layers to stimulate collagen in the dermal layer; it is used to resurface acne scars with a 7–10 day downtime.
*   <b><font color="red">Microdermabrasion</font></b> is a suction-based device (often called "diamond peel") that removes dead skin but does not reach the dermis to stimulate collagen.
*   <b><font color="red">Ultherapy and Thermage</font></b> are non-invasive treatments that use radiofrequency or ultrasound to tighten skin laxity.
*   <b><font color="red">Coolsculpting (Cryotherapy)</font></b> is a non-invasive procedure that uses focused cooling to target and reduce subcutaneous fat by up to 29%.

*   Injury to the <b><font color="red">Temporal branch of the Facial Nerve</font></b> within Facial Danger Zone 2 results in permanent paralysis of the frontalis muscle.
*   Sensory deficits in the face after surgery commonly involve injuries to <b><font color="red">Facial Danger Zones 1, 5, 6, and 7</font></b>.

### <b><i style="color:red;">IV. Facial Surgical Procedures</i></b>

*   <b><font color="red">Brow Ptosis</font></b> is the descent of the eyebrows due to aging; in females, the ideal brow peaks at the lateral third, while in males, it is straight at the supraorbital ridge.
*   The <b><font color="red">Endoscopic Brow Lift</font></b> is the current gold standard for brow rejuvenation because it uses small incisions hidden in the hair-bearing scalp.
*   <b><font color="red">Dermatochalasis</font></b> is the presence of excess upper eyelid skin that may disrupt peripheral vision and is a common indication for <b><font color="red">Blepharoplasty</font></b>.
*   Post-procedure <b><font color="red">Blepharoplasty</font></b> care involves cold compress for the first 48 hours to minimize vasodilation, followed by warm compress on day 3 to help absorb hematoma.
*   <b><font color="red">Facelift (Rhytidectomy)</font></b> techniques that only address subcutaneous skin are abandoned due to high recurrence; successful lifts must address the <b><font color="red">SMAS layer</font></b>.
*   <b><font color="red">Asian Rhinoplasty</font></b> typically focuses on augmentation of the nasal dorsum and tip using medical-grade implants (silicone/Gore-Tex) or autologous rib/ear cartilage.
*   A <b><font color="red">Columellar Strut</font></b> is a cartilage graft used in <b><font color="red">Rhinoplasty</font></b> to provide tripod support and achieve better nasal tip projection.

| Procedure | **Primary Goal** | **Indications/Key Features** |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| <b><font color="red">Mastopexy</font></b> | Breast Lift; volume is preserved | Corrects ptosis; nipple-area complex is repositioned |
| <b><font color="red">Augmentation Mammaplasty</font></b> | Increase breast size | USed for Micromastia or Poland Syndrome |
| <b><font color="red">Breast Reduction</font></b>| Decrease volume and skin | Treats symptoms of Macromastia/Gigantomastia |

*   Preoperative screening for <b><font color="red">Breast Aesthetic Surgery</font></b> requires a breast MRI if the patient is < 40 years old or a mammogram if ≥ 40 years old to rule out cancer.
*   <b><font color="red">Breast Reduction</font></b> is indicated for the symptomatic triad of: 1) Upper back pain, 2) Bra strap grooving, and 3) Rashes under the inframammary fold (IMF).
*   <b><font color="red">Poland Syndrome</font></b> is a congenital condition involving missing or underdeveloped pectoralis major muscles often requiring <b><font color="red">Breast Augmentation</font></b>.
*   <b><font color="red">Subglandular Implants</font></b> (under the gland) require a skin pinch test of at least 2 cm; if the skin is thinner, a <b><font color="red">Submuscular Implant</font></b> (under the muscle) is preferred.

### <b><i style="color:red;">VI. Body Contouring and Liposuction</i></b>

*   <b><font color="red">Liposuction (Suction Assisted Lipectomy)</font></b> is not a weight-loss procedure but a body contouring technique for patients with good skin elasticity.
*   The <b><font color="red">Tumescent Technique</font></b> in liposuction involves infiltrating dilute lidocaine and epinephrine to reduce blood loss and allow higher safe doses of lidocaine (up to 35 mg/kg).
*   An <b><font color="red">Abdominal Wall Pannus</font></b> is a redundancy of skin and fat hanging over the pubic area, often requiring a <b><font color="red">Panniculectomy</font></b>.
*   <b><font color="red">Abdominoplasty (Tummy Tuck)</font></b> involves the maximal removal of excess skin, tightening of the underlying abdominal fascia, and transposition of the umbilicus.
*   The <b><font color="red">Fleur-de-lis Abdominoplasty</font></b> is specifically designed for massive weight loss patients to correct both horizontal and vertical skin excess, resulting in a midline vertical scar.
*   <b><font color="red">Autologous Fat Grafting</font></b> (e.g., "Mommy Makeover") involves harvesting fat from one area via liposuction and transferring it to another, such as the buttocks.

### <b><i style="color:red;">VII. High-Yield Distinctions for Exams</i></b>

*   Contrast <b><font color="red">Static vs. Dynamic Lines</font></b>: Static lines are visible at rest (treated with fillers); Dynamic lines appear only with muscle movement (treated with Botox).
*   Contrast <b><font color="red">Dermabrasion vs. Microdermabrasion</font></b>: Dermabrasion penetrates to the dermis for scar resurfacing; Microdermabrasion only suctions dead superficial skin.
*   Contrast <b><font color="red">Mastopexy vs. Breast Reduction</font></b>: Mastopexy preserves breast volume while lifting; Breast Reduction removes glandular tissue and skin to reduce size.
*   Contrast <b><font color="red">Subglandular vs. Submuscular Implants</font></b>: Subglandular is easier but requires thick skin (>2cm pinch); Submuscular prevents "double bubble" deformity in patients with thin skin.
*   Contrast <b><font color="red">Inframammary vs. Periareolar Approach</font></b>: Inframammary has less risk of contamination; Periareolar carries a higher risk of abscess due to bacteria in milk ducts.
*   Contrast <b><font color="red">Standard vs. Fleur-de-lis Abdominoplasty</font></b>: Standard abdominoplasty uses a transverse incision for horizontal laxity; Fleur-de-lis adds a vertical incision for massive vertical skin excess.
*   Contrast <b><font color="red">Ablative vs. Non-Ablative Lasers</font></b>: Ablative (CO2) has superior results but 2-week downtime; Non-ablative (Nd:YAG) has less downtime (3-4 days) but subtle results.
*   Contrast <b><font color="red">Liposuction vs. Weight Loss Surgery</font></b>: Liposuction removes localized fat for contour in healthy-weight patients; Bariatric surgery is for systemic weight reduction in obese patients.
*   Contrast <b><font color="red">Reconstructive vs. Aesthetic Surgery</font></b>: Reconstructive surgery treats pathologic deformities (congenital/acquired); Aesthetic surgery improves appearance of normal structures.
*   Contrast <b><font color="red">Hyaluronic Acid vs. Poly-L Lactic Acid</font></b>: Hyaluronic Acid is a reversible, volume-adding gel; Poly-L Lactic Acid (Sculptra) is a bio-stimulatory filler for longer-term tissue development.
*   Distinguish <b><font color="red">Fitzpatrick Skin Type</font></b>: Patients with Skin Type 3 and above (common in Filipinos) must avoid sun for 2 weeks post-laser to prevent melasma and hyperpigmentation.
*   Distinguish <b><font color="red">Facelift Vectors</font></b>: SMAS tightening should follow an oblique/upward vector (toward cheekbones), not a horizontal/backward vector.
*   Distinguish <b><font color="red">Areola Size</font></b>: An aesthetically pleasing areola is 4–6 cm in diameter; larger areolas are corrected via specialized incisions during mastopexy.
*   Distinguish <b><font color="red">Hematoma Management</font></b>: Use COLD compress for the first 48 hours (vasoconstriction); use WARM compress after 72 hours (vasodilation to absorb fluid).

### <b><i style="color:red;">I. Fundamentals of Neuroradiology</i></b>

| Feature | <font color="red">CT Scan</font> | <font color="red">MRI</font> |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Primary Parameter** | **Density** | **Intensity** (Signal brightness) |
| **Core Principle** | X-ray attenuation (absorption) | Magnetic resonance signals |
| **Appearance of Bone** | Hyperdense (White); High HU | Signal Void (Dark) |
| **Best Utility** | Acute hemorrhage, fractures, calcifications | Soft tissue, cord pathology, tumors |
| **Surgical Value** | Quick acquisition, surgical planning (screws) | Detailed anatomy, ligamentous injury |

*   The <font color="red">Attenuation Coefficient</font> represents a constant value of how much X-ray radiation is absorbed by a specific tissue.
*   <font color="red">Hounsfield Units (HU)</font> are a quantitative scale used in CT scans to describe radiodensity relative to water (0 HU).
*   On a <font color="red">CT Scan</font>, lower HU values (e.g., -1000 for **Air**) appear darker/black, while higher HU values (e.g., +1000 for **Bone**) appear whiter/hyperdense.
*   On a <font color="red">CT Scan</font>, **Acute Blood (Hemorrhage)** is hyperdense (75-80 HU) while **CSF** is hypodense (approx. +5 HU).
*   <font color="red">CT Scan</font> is the first study of choice for suspected acute intracranial disease due to high sensitivity for fractures and acute hemorrhage.
*   The <font color="red">Axial Cut</font> of a CT scan provides a "top view" of the brain.
*   The <font color="red">Sagittal Cut</font> of a CT scan provides a "side view" of the brain.
*   The <font color="red">Coronal Cut</font> of a CT scan provides a "front view" of the brain.
*   <font color="red">Contrast-Enhanced CT</font> is specifically indicated for visualizing neoplastic (tumors) or infectious (abscess) processes.

### <b><i style="color:red;">II. Surgical Management of Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI)</i></b>

*   An <font color="red">Acute Epidural Hematoma (EDH)</font> requires surgical evacuation if the volume is > 30 cm³, regardless of the patient's GCS.
*   An <font color="red">Acute Epidural Hematoma (EDH)</font> in a patient with GCS < 9 and pupillary anisocoria is an indication for surgery.
*   The <font color="red">Management of EDH</font> can be observation if the volume is < 30 cm³, thickness is < 15 mm, and midline shift is < 5 mm in a conscious patient.
*   An <font color="red">Acute Epidural Hematoma (EDH)</font> classically appears as a biconvex/lentiform hyperdense shape that does not cross suture lines.
*   The most common source of bleeding in an <font color="red">Acute Epidural Hematoma (EDH)</font> is the **middle meningeal artery**.
*   <font color="red">Traumatic Parenchymal Lesions</font> require surgery if there is progressive neurologic deterioration, refractory ICP, or a clot volume > 50 cm³.
*   A <font color="red">Frontal or Temporal Contusion</font> requires surgery if the volume is > 20 cm³ AND there is a midline shift > 5 mm or SIS compression.
*   <font color="red">Delayed Traumatic Intracerebral Hemorrhage</font> most likely occurs within the first 24 hours post-trauma; repeat imaging at 24 hours is often necessary.
*   A <font color="red">Posterior Fossa Mass Lesion</font> requires surgical intervention if it causes brainstem compression or obstructive hydrocephalus (obliterated 4th ventricle).
*   A <font color="red">Depressed Skull Fracture</font> requires surgery if the depression exceeds the thickness of the adjacent skull or if it is an open fracture with dural penetration.

### <b><i style="color:red;">III. CNS Tumors and Histopathology</i></b>

| Tumor Type | Key Histopathology/Markers | Clinical Presentation/Location |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| <font color="red">Pilocytic Astrocytoma</font> | **Rosenthal fibers**; Eosinophilic granular bodies | Cyst with mural nodule; Posterior fossa; Children |
| <font color="red">Ependymoma</font> | **Rosettes & perivascular pseudorosettes** | 4th ventricle (children); Hydrocephalus |
| <font color="red">Oligodendroglioma</font> | **Fried egg appearance**; Chicken wire pattern | Supratentorial; Calcifications; **1p/19q codeletion** |
| <font color="red">Glioblastoma (GBM)</font> | **Necrosis & microvascular proliferation** | Adult; Temporal lobe; **Stupp Protocol** (TMZ + Rad) |
| <font color="red">Medulloblastoma</font> | **Homer Wright rosettes** | Cerebellum; Malignant (children); **CSF seeding** |
| <font color="red">Meningioma</font> | **Arachnoid cap cells**; Extra-axial | Dural-based; Usually benign; Brain compression |
| <font color="red">Vestibular Schwannoma</font> | Nerve sheath of CN VIII | Cerebellopontine angle; **Neurofibromatosis Type 2** |

*   <font color="red">Glioblastoma (GBM)</font> median survival is approximately 15 months, even with optimal treatment.
*   <font color="red">Hemangioblastoma</font> is associated with **Von Hippel-Lindau syndrome** when multiple lesions are present.
*   <font color="red">Pilocytic Astrocytoma</font> may be seen as part of **Neurofibromatosis Type 1**.
*   The hallmark of <font color="red">Neurofibromatosis Type 2</font> is the presence of bilateral vestibular schwannomas.
*   <font color="red">MGMT-methylation</font> is a biomarker in GBM that indicates an improved response to temozolomide chemotherapy.

### <b><i style="color:red;">IV. Traumatic Spinal Cord Injury (TSI) Management</i></b>

*   The <b>Airway Management</b> in <font color="red">TSI</font> must assume cervical spine injury; use the **Jaw Thrust** with caution and maintain rigid collar immobilization.
*   The primary goal in <b>Circulation</b> for <font color="red">TSI</font> is to keep **SBP > 90 mmHg** and maintain **MAP 85-90 mmHg** for the first 7 days.
*   <font color="red">Neurogenic Shock</font> in TSI is characterized by the triad of hypotension, bradycardia, and warm, dry skin due to loss of sympathetic tone.
*   The first line of treatment for <font color="red">Hypotension in TSI</font> is fluid resuscitation, followed by **Norepinephrine** as the preferred vasopressor.
*   The <font color="red">Canadian C-Spine Rule</font> mandates imaging for patients age ≥ 65 or those with a "dangerous mechanism" of injury.
*   The <font color="red">NEXUS Criteria</font> allow for clinical clearance of the C-spine only if there is no midline tenderness, no focal neuro deficit, and no distracting injuries.
*   <font color="red">Clinical Clearance</font> of the C-spine involves removing the collar and having the patient rotate their head 45 degrees to each side without pain.
*   <font color="red">Complete Cervical TSI (C1-C4)</font> requires early elective intubation due to the high risk of respiratory arrest and loss of diaphragmatic innervation.
*   <font color="red">Quad Breathing</font> is a sign of cervical TSI where the abdomen moves out sharply during inspiration, indicating the need for elective intubation.
*   High <font color="red">Cervical Spine Injuries (above C3)</font> are associated with the loss of diaphragmatic function (phrenic nerve C3, C4, C5).
*   The use of <font color="red">Corticosteroids</font> for acute traumatic spinal cord injury is no longer considered the standard of care.

### <b><i style="color:red;">V. Advanced Imaging in Neurosurgery</i></b>

*   <font color="red">T1-Weighted MRI + Gadolinium</font> is the gold standard for identifying blood-brain barrier breakdown in tumors and abscesses.
*   <font color="red">FLAIR (Fluid-Attenuated Inversion Recovery)</font> is a T2 MRI sequence that suppresses CSF signal to better visualize periventricular pathology like MS plaques.
*   <font color="red">Diffusion-Weighted Imaging (DWI)</font> is the gold standard for diagnosing **acute ischemic stroke** within minutes of onset.
*   <font color="red">STIR (Short Tau Inversion Recovery)</font> sequences are used in spine imaging to identify **bone marrow edema**, distinguishing acute from chronic fractures.
*   <font color="red">Gradient Echo (GRE)</font> MRI is essential for detecting micro-hemorrhages and diagnosing **Diffuse Axonal Injury (DAI)**.
*   Absolute <font color="red">Contraindications to MRI</font> include cardiac pacemakers, ICDs, and retained metallic foreign bodies in the orbits.

### <b><i style="color:red;">VI. ASIA Impairment Scale (AIS) for Spinal Cord Injury</i></b>

*   The <font color="red">ASIA Grade A</font> classification signifies a **Complete** injury with no sensory or motor function in sacral segments S4-S5.
*   The <font color="red">ASIA Grade B</font> classification signifies **Sensory Incomplete** (sensory preserved below level, but no motor function).
*   The <font color="red">ASIA Grade C</font> classification signifies **Motor Incomplete** where at least half of the key muscles below the injury level have a grade **LESS than 3**.
*   The <font color="red">ASIA Grade D</font> classification signifies **Motor Incomplete** where at least half of the key muscles below the injury level have a grade **GREATER than or equal to 3**.
*   The <font color="red">ASIA Grade E</font> classification signifies **Normal** motor and sensory function in a patient who previously had deficits.

### <b><i style="color:red;">VII. High-Yield Distinctions for Exams</i></b>

*   Contrast <font color="red">Neurogenic Shock vs. Spinal Shock</font>: Neurogenic shock is a **hemodynamic** state (hypotension/bradycardia); Spinal shock is a **neurologic** state (loss of reflexes).
*   Contrast <font color="red">CT vs. MRI for Blood</font>: CT is superior for **acute** hemorrhage (white/hyperdense); MRI sequences like GRE are superior for **chronic** or micro-hemorrhages.
*   Contrast <font color="red">Epidural (EDH) vs. Subdural (SDH) Hematoma</font>: EDH is biconvex and respects suture lines; SDH is crescent-shaped and crosses suture lines.
*   Contrast <font color="red">C1 vs. C2 Fractures</font>: Use the **Rule of Spence** (lat mass displacement >7mm) to identify transverse ligament rupture in Jefferson (C1) fractures.
*   Contrast <font color="red">T1 vs. T2 MRI</font>: T1 is best for **anatomical detail** (CSF is dark); T2 is best for **edema/water** detection (CSF is bright).
*   Contrast <font color="red">Canadian C-Spine vs. NEXUS</font>: Canadian C-Spine involves high-risk factors (age/mechanism); NEXUS focuses on the absence of "5 criteria" (tenderness, intoxication, neuro deficit, etc.).
*   Contrast <font color="red">Pilocytic Astrocytoma vs. Medulloblastoma</font>: Both are in the posterior fossa of children, but Pilocytic is **benign/cystic** while Medulloblastoma is **malignant/solid**.
*   Contrast <font color="red">Vasopressors in TSI</font>: Fluid resuscitation is 1st line; **Norepinephrine** is preferred over others to maintain MAP.
*   Contrast <font color="red">Dermabrasion vs. Microdermabrasion</font>: Dermabrasion reaches the dermis for acne scars; microdermabrasion only removes dead skin via suction.
*   Contrast <font color="red">Standard vs. Fleur-de-lis Abdominoplasty</font>: Fleur-de-lis adds a **vertical** incision to address massive weight loss skin excess.
