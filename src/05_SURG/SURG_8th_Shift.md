# Summary

| COMPARISON OF ACYANOTIC VS. CYANOTIC HEART DISEASE |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Feature** | **Details** |
| **Mechanism** | <span style="color:red">**Acyanotic Heart Disease**</span> involves simple/single lesions with left-to-right shunts or obstructive lesions. <span style="color:red">**Cyanotic Heart Disease**</span> involves complex lesions with right-to-left shunts, common mixing, or parallel circulations. |
| **Laboratory Finding** | <span style="color:red">**Cyanosis**</span> manifests clinically when capillary reduced hemoglobin is **>5gm %** (oxygen saturation usually <85%). |
| **Incidence** | <span style="color:red">**Congenital Heart Disease (CHD)**</span> occurs in 8 out of every 1000 live births. |


| SUMMARY OF COMMON CONGENITAL HEART DEFECTS |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Disease Entity** | **Key Pathophysiology** | **Diagnostic Findings** | **Management / Treatment** |
| <span style="color:red">**Atrial Septal Defect (ASD)**</span> | Left to right shunt; <span style="color:red">**Ostium secundum**</span> is the most common type (80%). | **Fixed splitting of S2**; RV heave; 2D Echo shows shunt. | Surgery indicated if **Qp:Qs > 1.5:1**; Transcatheter closure requires 5mm rim. |
| <span style="color:red">**Aortic Stenosis (AS)**</span> | LVOT obstruction; Neonatal critical AS is ductal-dependent. | **"Parvus et tardus"** pulse; Crescendo-decrescendo murmur; LVH on ECG. | **Ross procedure** (harvesting pulmonary valve) used for growing patients. |
| <span style="color:red">**Patent Ductus Arteriosus (PDA)**</span> | Left to right shunt; survival of other defects may depend on it. | **Continuous machinery murmur**; Wide pulse pressure; Bounding pulses. | Indomethacin/Ibuprofen for closure in preemies; Rashkind device or ligation. |
| <span style="color:red">**Coarctation of the Aorta (COA)**</span> | Luminal narrowing distal to Left Subclavian Artery. | **Rib notching** on CXR; Femoral pulse < Upper limb pulse; Secondary HTN. | Resection with end-to-end anastomosis; Stenting for older patients. |
| <span style="color:red">**Tetralogy of Fallot (TOF)**</span> | Infundibular septum malposition: VSD, Overriding aorta, RVOT obstruction, RVH. | Most common cyanotic disease; **Boot-shaped heart**; **"Tet spells"** (squatting help). | Primary cardiac repair (VSD closure + infundibulectomy); Palliative mBTS. |
| <span style="color:red">**Transposition of Great Arteries (TGA)**</span> | Parallel circulation; VA discordance; LV atrophies if not corrected early. | **"Egg-shaped"** heart on CXR; Cyanosis at birth. | **Arterial Switch (Jatene)** ideally within 2-3 weeks; Senning/Mustard (Atrial repair). |


| PALLIATION STAGES FOR SINGLE VENTRICLE PHYSIOLOGY |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Stage** | **Procedure Name** | **Key Mechanism/Timing** |
| **Stage 1** | <span style="color:red">**Norwood Procedure**</span> or <span style="color:red">**BTS**</span> | Establishes systemic/pulmonary flow in neonates (e.g., HLHS or Tricuspid Atresia). |
| **Stage 2** | <span style="color:red">**Glenn Shunt**</span> (Bidirectional) | Superior cavopulmonary anastomosis (SVC to RPA); done at ~6 months. |
| **Stage 3** | <span style="color:red">**Fontan Procedure**</span> | Total cavopulmonary connection (IVC/SVC to PA); done at 2-4 years. |


| ACQUIRED HEART DISEASE QUIZ KEY FACTS |
| :--- |
| • <span style="color:red">**Rheumatic Heart Disease**</span> is the most common cause of acquired mitral stenosis. |
| • <span style="color:red">**Heparin dosage**</span> of **300 to 400 U/kg** is required for adequate anticoagulation during cardiopulmonary bypass. |
| • <span style="color:red">**Carpentier Class IIIB**</span> refers to functional mitral regurgitation resulting from ischemic systolic restriction of leaflets. |
| • <span style="color:red">**Intra-aortic Balloon Pump (IABP)**</span> augments coronary perfusion by **inflating during diastole**. |
| • <span style="color:red">**Coronary Artery Disease (CAD)**</span> is the most common cause of heart failure. |


| CONGENITAL HEART DISEASE BULLET POINTS |
| :--- |
| • <span style="color:red">**Sinus venosus ASD**</span> is frequently associated with partial anomalous pulmonary venous drainage (PAPVD) (Context: ASD). |
| • <span style="color:red">**Eisenmenger Syndrome**</span> describes irreversible pulmonary hypertension resulting from long-standing left-to-right shunts (Context: ASD Pathophysiology). |
| • <span style="color:red">**Paradoxical Embolization**</span> in ASD patients can allow a venous embolus to enter the systemic circulation, causing a stroke (Context: ASD Indications for surgery). |
| • <span style="color:red">**Warden Procedure**</span> is the specific surgical repair used for the Sinus Venosus type of ASD (Context: ASD Treatment). |
| • <span style="color:red">**Williams Syndrome**</span> features include elfin facies, hypercalcemia, and supravalvular aortic stenosis (Context: Aortic Stenosis). |
| • <span style="color:red">**Ductal-dependent systemic blood flow**</span> occurs in critical Aortic Stenosis; circulatory collapse happens once the PDA closes (Context: Aortic Stenosis). |
| • <span style="color:red">**Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve**</span> injury during PDA ligation is a known complication that leads to **vocal cord paralysis** (Context: PDA Surgery). |
| • <span style="color:red">**Rib Notching**</span> in Coarctation of the Aorta is caused by the erosion of bone by dilated/enlarged intercostal arteries used as collaterals (Context: COA Diagnosis). |
| • <span style="color:red">**Truncus Arteriosus**</span> is a defect characterized by a single great artery supplying pulmonary, systemic, and coronary circulations and is associated with **DiGeorge Syndrome** (Context: Truncus Arteriosus). |
| • <span style="color:red">**Snowman Sign**</span> (or Figure-8) on CXR is a characteristic finding in Total Anomalous Pulmonary Venous Connection (TAPVC) (Context: TAPVC Diagnosis). |
| • <span style="color:red">**Equal saturations in all chambers**</span> is the hallmark physiological finding in Total Anomalous Pulmonary Venous Connection (Context: TAPVC Hallmark). |
| • <span style="color:red">**Cor Triatriatum**</span> is a rare CHD where a fibromuscular diaphragm divides the left atrium, mimicking the hemodynamics of Mitral Stenosis (Context: Cor Triatriatum). |
| • <span style="color:red">**Aortopulmonary Window (APW)**</span> presents with two separate valves, distinguishing it from Truncus Arteriosus which has one (Context: APW Differentiation). |
| • <span style="color:red">**Dysphagia Lusoria**</span> is difficulty swallowing caused by esophageal compression from vascular rings or an aberrant right subclavian artery (Context: Vascular Rings). |
| • <span style="color:red">**Tricuspid Atresia**</span> requires an obligatory **Right-to-Left shunt** via an ASD/PFO for the patient to survive (Context: Tricuspid Atresia). |
| • <span style="color:red">**Ebstein Anomaly**</span> is characterized by the displacement of the tricuspid valve towards the apex, resulting in an **"atrialized" right ventricle** (Context: Ebstein Anomaly). |
| • <span style="color:red">**Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) Syndrome**</span> is found in approximately 15% of patients with Ebstein Anomaly (Context: Ebstein Anomaly). |
| • <span style="color:red">**GOSE Ratio**</span> (RA area / RV area) is used as a predictor of mortality in Ebstein Anomaly; higher ratios correlate with higher mortality (Context: Ebstein Anomaly). |
| • <span style="color:red">**D-TGA**</span> features Atrio-Ventricular (AV) concordance but Ventriculo-Arterial (VA) discordance (Context: Transposition of Great Arteries). |
| • <span style="color:red">**Rashkind Septostomy**</span> is an emergency balloon procedure used to create or enlarge an ASD to improve mixing in TGA (Context: TGA Management). |
| • <span style="color:red">**Taussig-Bing Malformation**</span> is a type of Double Outlet Right Ventricle (DORV) with a subpulmonic VSD, presenting clinically like TGA (Context: DORV spectrum). |
| • <span style="color:red">**Tet Spells**</span> are paroxysmal cyanotic events in TOF where squatting or the knee-chest position helps by **increasing systemic vascular resistance (SVR)** to drive blood into the lungs (Context: TOF). |
| • <span style="color:red">**Ventricular Septal Defect (VSD)**</span> is the **most common** congenital heart disease overall, with the muscular type being the most frequent subtype (Context: VSD). |
| • <span style="color:red">**Partial AVSD**</span> consists of a primum ASD and a mitral valve cleft (Context: AVSD). |
| • <span style="color:red">**Type A Aortic Dissection**</span> involves the ascending aorta and is a surgical emergency due to risk of rupture, tamponade, or coronary compromise (Context: Aortic Dissection). |
| • <span style="color:red">**Thoracic Aneurysm Surgery**</span> is generally indicated when the diameter reaches **5.5 cm**, or **4.5 cm** if the patient has a bicuspid aortic valve (Context: Aneurysm Management). |


| COMPARATIVE DIFFERENTIATION FOR EXAMS |
| :--- |
| • <span style="color:red">**ASD vs. VSD**</span>: ASD typically remains asymptomatic until adulthood and presents with a fixed split S2, whereas VSD is the most common CHD overall and often presents with heart failure in infancy. |
| • <span style="color:red">**Truncus Arteriosus vs. AP Window**</span>: Truncus Arteriosus has a **single truncal valve**, while Aortopulmonary Window (APW) has **two separate valves** (Aortic and Pulmonary). |
| • <span style="color:red">**TOF vs. TGA**</span>: TOF is the most common cyanotic CHD and shows a **boot-shaped** heart with decreased pulmonary flow; TGA shows an **egg-shaped** heart and presents with severe cyanosis at birth due to parallel circulation. |
| • <span style="color:red">**Coarctation (COA) vs. Interrupted Aortic Arch (IAA)**</span>: COA is a narrowing (stenosis) usually distal to the Left Subclavian Artery; IAA is a complete **lack of luminal continuity** between the ascending and descending aorta. |
| • <span style="color:red">**Cyanotic Hb Level**</span>: Cyanosis is based on the **absolute amount of reduced hemoglobin (>5g%)**, not just the percentage of saturation. |
| • <span style="color:red">**BTS vs. Glenn vs. Fontan**</span>: BTS (Stage 1) uses a systemic-to-pulmonary shunt; Glenn (Stage 2) connects SVC to PA; Fontan (Stage 3) connects IVC to PA. |
| • <span style="color:red">**Ross Procedure vs. Rastelli Procedure**</span>: Ross involves moving the patient's own pulmonary valve to the aortic position; Rastelli involves using a valved conduit to connect the RV to the PA (often used in TGA/VSD/LVOT obstruction). |
| • <span style="color:red">**Type A vs. Type B Dissection**</span>: Type A (Ascending) requires **emergency surgery**; Type B (Descending) is primarily managed **medically** with BP and HR control. |
| • <span style="color:red">**Indomethacin vs. Prostaglandin**</span>: Indomethacin (or Ibuprofen) is used to **close** a PDA; Prostaglandin (PGE1) is used to **keep a PDA open** in ductal-dependent lesions. |
| • <span style="color:red">**Fixed Split S2 vs. Machinery Murmur**</span>: A fixed split S2 is the hallmark of **ASD**; a continuous machinery murmur is the hallmark of **PDA**. |
| • <span style="color:red">**Parvus et tardus vs. Bounding Pulse**</span>: Parvus et tardus (weak and late) is found in **Aortic Stenosis**; bounding pulses with wide pulse pressure are characteristic of **PDA** or **Aortic Regurgitation**. |
| • <span style="color:red">**D-TGA vs. L-TGA**</span>: D-TGA is the common cyanotic type (VA discordance); L-TGA is "physiologically corrected" (both AV and VA discordance) but prone to later RV failure. |

# 8.2 - Acquired Heart Diseases
## Summary

SURGERY 2: ACQUIRED HEART DISEASES

### GENERAL PRINCIPLES, CLASSIFICATION, AND PREOPERATIVE ASSESSMENT


| Topic | Key Features / Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| <span style="color:red">NYHA Classification</span> | Functional classification of heart failure based on physical activity limitation: Class I (No limitation) to Class IV (Symptoms at rest). |
| <span style="color:red">CCS Angina Classification</span> | Grading of angina severity: Class I (Strenuous exertion only) to Class IV (Angina at rest or with any activity). |
| <span style="color:red">MACE Risk Estimation</span> | Estimated using NSQIP or Revised Cardiac Risk Index (RCRI); Major Cardiac Events include ischemic heart disease, HF, and renal insufficiency. |
| <span style="color:red">Functional Capacity</span> | Measured in METs; Good functional capacity is ≥4 METs, while <4 METs is poor/unknown and may require stress testing. |
| <span style="color:red">PCI Stent Precautions</span> | Before elective surgery, delay 30 days for Bare Metal Stents (BMS) and 180–365 days for Drug-Eluting Stents (DES) to prevent thrombosis. |

• In <span style="color:red">NYHA Class I</span> heart failure classification, physical activity is not limited by fatigue, palpitations, or dyspnea.
• In <span style="color:red">NYHA Class III</span> heart failure classification, there is a marked limitation of physical activity, as the patient is comfortable only at rest.
• In <span style="color:red">CCS Angina Class II</span>, walking more than 2 blocks or climbing one flight of stairs causes angina.
• As part of the <span style="color:red">General Signs and Symptoms</span> of heart disease, dyspnea in female and elderly patients often serves as an "anginal equivalent" rather than classic chest pain.
• For <span style="color:red">Diabetic Patients</span> with CAD, "silent ischemia" may occur due to autonomic neuropathy, leading to a lack of classic chest pain.
• In <span style="color:red">Heart Failure</span> physical examination, clubbing and cyanosis are late-stage signs indicating chronic tissue hypoperfusion or oxygen desaturation.
• The <span style="color:red">Most Common Arrhythmia</span> mentioned in the context of palpitations and valvular disease is Atrial Fibrillation (AF).
• In the <span style="color:red">Physical Examination</span> of cardiac patients, frailty and dementia are significant predictors of operative and late mortality.
• During <span style="color:red">Preoperative Cardiovascular Risk Assessment</span>, non-emergent surgery should be delayed for intensive evaluation if major active cardiac conditions like ACS or decompensated HF are present.


### EXTRACORPOREAL PERFUSION AND MYOCARDIAL PROTECTION


| Topic | Components / Mechanism | Important Details |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| <span style="color:red">Cardiopulmonary Bypass (CPB)</span> | Venous cannulae, reservoir, oxygenator, and arterial cannula. | Bypasses heart and lungs to allow a bloodless, still surgical field. |
| <span style="color:red">Anticoagulation Strategy</span> | <span style="color:red">Heparin</span> (300-400 U/kg) | Maintain <span style="color:red">Activated Clotting Time (ACT)</span> at 450 seconds. |
| <span style="color:red">Antegrade Cardioplegia</span> | Infused into aortic root or coronary ostia. | Follows natural blood flow; requires a competent aortic valve. |
| <span style="color:red">Retrograde Cardioplegia</span> | Infused into the <span style="color:red">Coronary Sinus</span>. | Best for diffuse CAD or incompetent aortic valve; provides uniform distribution. |

• For <span style="color:red">Cardiopulmonary Bypass (CPB)</span> management, systemic hypothermia helps by providing a 50% reduction in oxygen consumption for every 10°C drop in temperature.
• The <span style="color:red">Protamine Reversal</span> protocol is used at the end of CPB to neutralize heparin and achieve hemostasis.
• A major complication of <span style="color:red">Cardiopulmonary Bypass (CPB)</span> is <span style="color:red">Systemic Inflammatory Response Syndrome (SIRS)</span>, caused by the interaction of blood with the artificial circuit.
• To achieve <span style="color:red">Myocardial Protection</span>, the heart is intentionally arrested in the <span style="color:red">diastolic phase</span> using a potassium-rich cardioplegia solution.
• The <span style="color:red">Centrifugal Pump</span> is a modern CPB component that uses a vortex principle to propel blood back to the patient.
• In <span style="color:red">Myocardial Protection</span>, hypothermia is used as a secondary strategy to further decrease metabolic demand and oxygen consumption.
• The <span style="color:red">Myocardium</span> is the only organ not perfused during CPB, which is why cardioplegia and cross-clamping are necessary to prevent infarction.


### CORONARY ARTERY DISEASE (CAD) AND BYPASS GRAFTING (CABG)


| Procedure | Indication / Anatomy | Conduit Selection / Result |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| <span style="color:red">CABG (On-Pump)</span> | Left Main disease, 3-vessel disease, DM, LV dysfunction. | Standard via median sternotomy; uses CPB. |
| <span style="color:red">OPCAB (Off-Pump)</span> | High-risk for CPB; limited lesions. | Done on a beating heart; avoids SIRS but long-term survival controversial. |
| <span style="color:red">MIDCAB</span> | Isolated <span style="color:red">LAD</span> disease. | Left mini-thoracotomy; beating heart; uses <span style="color:red">LIMA</span>. |
| <span style="color:red">TMR (Laser)</span> | End-stage CAD; refractory angina. | Creates channels; benefit is likely from laser-induced angiogenesis. |

• The <span style="color:red">Gold Standard Diagnosis</span> for CAD is the Coronary Angiogram, which involves catheterization of the sinuses of Valsalva.
• In <span style="color:red">CAD Etiology</span>, the ultimate progression is from plaque rupture to thrombosis, resulting in Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS).
• The <span style="color:red">Most Durable Graft</span> used in CABG is the <span style="color:red">Internal Mammary Artery (IMA)</span>, also known as the Internal Thoracic Artery.
• For <span style="color:red">Internal Mammary Artery (IMA)</span> grafts, the patency rate is approximately 98% at 5 years and 85–90% at 10 years.
• Before using the <span style="color:red">Radial Artery</span> as a bypass conduit, an <span style="color:red">Allen’s Test</span> must be performed to ensure intact palmar arch circulation via the ulnar artery.
• The <span style="color:red">Great Saphenous Vein (GSV)</span> is a common venous conduit because it provides long segments for multiple grafts, though its patency (86% at 5 years) is lower than radial artery grafts.
• According to the <span style="color:red">BARI Trial</span>, CABG is impressively superior to PCI for patients with both <span style="color:red">Diabetes and CAD</span>, showing lower 5-year cardiac mortality (5.8% vs. 20.6%).
• In <span style="color:red">Myocardial Viability Testing</span>, "hibernating myocardium" refers to myocytes that are viable but functionally depressed, which will benefit from revascularization.
• For <span style="color:red">CABG Indications</span>, Left Main (LM) disease and 3-vessel disease are considered Class I recommendations to improve survival.
• During <span style="color:red">Conventional CABG</span>, the <span style="color:red">LITA to LAD</span> graft is usually performed last to prevent kinking of the conduit.


### MITRAL VALVE DISEASES (STENOSIS AND REGURGITATION)


| Disease | Etiology | Hallmark Findings / Classification | Treatment / Management |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| <span style="color:red">Mitral Stenosis (MS)</span> | Almost always <span style="color:red">Rheumatic (RHD)</span>. | Wilkins Score (Echo) for morphology. | PMBC (Balloon) is first-line; valve replacement if morphology is poor. |
| <span style="color:red">Mitral Regurgitation (MR)</span> | Myxomatous (US); RHD (Global); IE; Ischemic. | <span style="color:red">Carpentier Classification</span> (Type I, II, III). | Surgery if symptomatic or Asymptomatic with <span style="color:red">EF ≤ 60%</span>. |

• In <span style="color:red">Mitral Stenosis (MS)</span> diagnosis, 2D Echocardiography is the tool of choice to evaluate valve area and gradients.
• The <span style="color:red">Wilkins Score</span> is used to evaluate MS for balloon valvotomy based on leaflet mobility, thickening, calcification, and chordae involvement.
• <span style="color:red">Percutaneous Mitral Balloon Commissurotomy (PMBC)</span> is contraindicated if the patient has more than moderate MR or a Left Atrial thrombus.
• In <span style="color:red">Mitral Regurgitation (MR)</span>, the most important cause in the United States is myxomatous degenerative disease.
• In the <span style="color:red">Carpentier Classification</span> for MR, Type II is defined as excessive leaflet motion, commonly seen in mitral valve prolapse or "flail" leaflets.
• For <span style="color:red">Carpentier Type IIIb</span> MR, the pathology is restricted closure during systole, usually due to ischemic or functional MR.
• In <span style="color:red">Mitral Valve Repair</span>, artificial chords are often made from <span style="color:red">Polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE)</span>, also known as Teflon.
• For <span style="color:red">Prosthetic Valve Selection</span>, <span style="color:red">Mechanical Valves</span> are preferred in younger patients for durability but require lifelong anticoagulation.
• For <span style="color:red">Prosthetic Valve Selection</span>, <span style="color:red">Bioprosthetic Valves</span> are preferred in older patients to avoid the risks of lifelong anticoagulation and bleeding.


### AORTIC VALVE DISEASES (STENOSIS AND INSUFFICIENCY)


| Disease                                                  | Most Common Cause                                   | Pathophysiology Path                     | Management / Procedure                                                                                  |
| :------------------------------------------------------- | :-------------------------------------------------- | :--------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| <span style="color:red">Aortic Stenosis (AS)</span>      | Degenerative / Calcific.                            | Pressure overload → Concentric LVH.      | <span style="color:red">SAVR</span> (Surgical) or <span style="color:red">TAVR</span> (Catheter-based). |
| <span style="color:red">Aortic Insufficiency (AI)</span> | <span style="color:red">Aortic Root Disease</span>. | Volume overload → Eccentric hypertrophy. | Valve replacement or Root Repair (Ross, David procedures).                                              |

• The classic physical finding of <span style="color:red">Aortic Stenosis (AS)</span> is <span style="color:red">Pulsus Parvus et Tardus</span>, characterized by a weak pulse and delayed systolic upstroke.
• For patients with <span style="color:red">Aortic Stenosis (AS)</span>, the onset of symptoms significantly shortens survival: Angina (1 yr), Syncope (2 yrs) without intervention.
• In <span style="color:red">Aortic Stenosis (AS)</span> severity grading, "Severe" is defined as a valve area <span style="color:red">&lt1.0 cm²</span> or a jet velocity <span style="color:red">>4.0 m/s</span>.
• A <span style="color:red">Bicuspid Aortic Valve</span> is a congenital lesion that is highly prone to early calcification and may present as either AS or AI.
• In <span style="color:red">Aortic Insufficiency (AI)</span>, volume overload leads to the development of a "bovine heart," which is a massively dilated myocardium due to eccentric hypertrophy.
• The <span style="color:red">Ross Procedure</span> involves using the patient's own pulmonary valve as an autograft to replace the diseased aortic valve.
• In <span style="color:red">Acute Severe AI</span>, the LV cannot compensate for sudden volume overload, quickly leading to cardiogenic shock and pulmonary edema.
• During <span style="color:red">Aortic Valve Surgery</span>, if the aorta measures <span style="color:red">>4.5 cm</span> in a patient with a bicuspid valve, replacement of the ascending aorta is recommended.
• <span style="color:red">Transcatheter Aortic Valve Replacement (TAVR)</span> is currently the preferred treatment for aortic stenosis in patients at high surgical risk.


### TRICUSPID VALVE, HEART FAILURE, AND PERICARDIAL DISEASE


• <span style="color:red">Tricuspid Stenosis (TS)</span> is almost always caused by Rheumatic Heart Disease (RHD).
• <span style="color:red">Tricuspid Regurgitation (TR)</span> is most often a functional disease caused by RV dilation secondary to pulmonary hypertension or left-sided mitral valve disease.
• In <span style="color:red">Tricuspid Valve Repair</span>, an incomplete ring is used for annuloplasty to avoid damage to the <span style="color:red">AV Node</span> conducting tissue.
• The <span style="color:red">Dor Procedure</span> is a surgical ventricular restoration technique used when the myocardium is scarred and balloons out (aneurysm) rather than contracting.
• The <span style="color:red">Intra-Aortic Balloon Pump (IABP)</span> works by inflating during <span style="color:red">diastole</span> (augmenting coronary perfusion) and deflating during <span style="color:red">systole</span> (reducing afterload).
• For severe heart failure, a <span style="color:red">Ventricular Assist Device (VAD)</span> can be used as a "bridge to transplant" or as "destination therapy" for life.
• The <span style="color:red">Maze IV Procedure</span> is a surgical treatment for Atrial Fibrillation (AF) that creates lines of ablation to interrupt macroreentrant circuits.
• <span style="color:red">Acute Pericarditis</span> is often characterized by pleuritic chest pain, a pericardial friction rub, and diffuse ST elevation with PR depression on EKG.
• In <span style="color:red">Chronic Constrictive Pericarditis</span>, the hallmark physical finding is elevated JVP with <span style="color:red">Kussmaul’s sign</span> (rise in JVP on inspiration).
• <span style="color:red">Cardiac Myxoma</span> is the most common primary benign cardiac tumor and often presents with symptoms mimicking mitral stenosis.
• In <span style="color:red">Chronic Constrictive Pericarditis</span>, calcification of the pericardium is seen in approximately 25% of patients on Chest X-ray.


### DIFFERENTIATION AND COMPARISON FOR EXAMS


• <span style="color:red">NYHA vs. CCS</span>: NYHA Classifies Heart Failure (I-IV) based on dyspnea/fatigue; CCS Classifies Angina (I-IV) based on chest pain during exertion.
• <span style="color:red">Antegrade vs. Retrograde Cardioplegia</span>: Antegrade goes through the aortic root (needs competent valve); Retrograde goes through the coronary sinus (better for incompetent valve or diffuse blockages).
• <span style="color:red">BMS vs. DES Delay</span>: Bare metal stents (BMS) require a 30-day delay for elective surgery; Drug-eluting stents (DES) require 180-365 days due to higher stent thrombosis risk.
• <span style="color:red">Mitral Stenosis vs. Mitral Regurgitation Etiology</span>: MS is almost strictly <span style="color:red">Rheumatic</span>; MR is more commonly <span style="color:red">Myxomatous/Degenerative</span> in developed nations.
• <span style="color:red">Concentric vs. Eccentric Hypertrophy</span>: Aortic Stenosis (Pressure Overload) causes <span style="color:red">Concentric</span> (thick walls); Aortic Regurgitation (Volume Overload) causes <span style="color:red">Eccentric</span> (dilated/large heart).
• <span style="color:red">Pulsus Parvus et Tardus vs. Pulsus Paradoxus</span>: Parvus et Tardus is the hallmark of <span style="color:red">Aortic Stenosis</span>; Pulsus Paradoxus is typically seen in <span style="color:red">Cardiac Tamponade</span>.
• <span style="color:red">Ross vs. David Procedure</span>: Ross Procedure uses a pulmonary autograft (living valve); David Procedure is a valve-sparing root replacement (keeping the native aortic valve).
• <span style="color:red">Mechanical vs. Bioprosthetic Valves</span>: Mechanical is durable but needs <span style="color:red">Warfarin/Anticoagulation</span> (Younger patients); Bioprosthetic is tissue-based and fails earlier but no lifelong thinners (Older patients).
• <span style="color:red">Acute vs. Chronic AI</span>: Acute AI presents with sudden circulatory collapse and <span style="color:red">pulmonary edema</span>; Chronic AI can be asymptomatic for years while the heart progressively dilates.
• <span style="color:red">IABP Timing</span>: The balloon <span style="color:red">inflates</span> at the start of diastole (dicrotic notch) and <span style="color:red">deflates</span> just before systole (R wave).
• <span style="color:red">Wilkins Score vs. Carpentier Classification</span>: Wilkins evaluates if an MS patient can have a <span style="color:red">balloon procedure</span>; Carpentier classifies the mechanism of <span style="color:red">leaflet motion in MR</span>.
• <span style="color:red">Hibernating vs. Infarcted Myocardium</span>: Hibernating tissue is alive but "sleeping" due to low flow (recovers after CABG); Infarcted tissue is <span style="color:red">dead/scarred</span> (does not recover function).
• <span style="color:red">Suture vs. Ring Annuloplasty</span>: Suture annuloplasty is simpler using pledgeted sutures; Ring annuloplasty uses a rigid/semi-rigid ring for more defined support.
• <span style="color:red">Primary vs. Secondary TR</span>: Primary TR is due to valve damage (IE/Trauma); Secondary (Functional) TR is due to <span style="color:red">annular dilation</span> from RV failure/Mitral disease.
• <span style="color:red">Surgical vs. Medical Pericarditis</span>: Most pericarditis is medical (NSAIDs/Colchicine); Surgery (Window/Pericardiectomy) is reserved for <span style="color:red">Tamponade or Constriction</span>.
• <span style="color:red">Rotor vs. Centrifugal Pump</span>: Rotor pumps sequentially compress tubing; Centrifugal pumps use a <span style="color:red">vortex principle</span> and are more modern.
• <span style="color:red">Classic Angina vs. Atypical equivalent</span>: Classic is chest pressure; Atypical equivalents (common in women/elderly) is <span style="color:red">Dyspnea</span>.
• <span style="color:red">Cardiac Myxoma vs. Thrombus</span>: On echo, Myxoma is a tumor (often moves through valves); Thrombus is a blood clot (requires <span style="color:red">anticoagulation</span> while tumor requires <span style="color:red">resection</span>).
• <span style="color:red">Anterior/Posterior vs. Septal Annulus</span>: Most tricuspid dilation occurs at the <span style="color:red">Anterior and Posterior</span> annulus; the Septal annulus is near the conduction system.
• <span style="color:red">STICH vs. BARI Trial</span>: STICH looked at CABG in <span style="color:red">Heart Failure/LVD</span>; BARI looked at CABG vs PCI in <span style="color:red">Diabetics</span>.

DIFFERENTIATION AND COMPARISON FOR EXAMS

107. Contrast <b><font color="red">Hibernating vs. Infarcted Myocardium</font></b>. | Hibernating: alive but "sleeping" (recovers); Infarcted: dead/scarred (no recovery).

109. Contrast <b><font color="red">Primary vs. Secondary TR</font></b>. | Primary: valve damage (IE/trauma); Secondary: annular dilation from RV failure.
110. Contrast <b><font color="red">Surgical vs. Medical Pericarditis</font></b> management. | Medical: NSAIDs/Colchicine; Surgical: Pericardiectomy for tamponade/constriction.

113. Contrast <b><font color="red">Cardiac Myxoma vs. Thrombus</font></b> treatment. | Myxoma: resection; Thrombus: anticoagulation.
114. Which parts of the <b><font color="red">Tricuspid Annulus</font></b> typically dilate? | Anterior and Posterior annulus.
115. Compare the <b><font color="red">STICH vs. BARI Trial</font></b> focus. | STICH: CABG in Heart Failure; BARI: CABG vs PCI in Diabetics.
