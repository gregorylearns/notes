### SPECIMEN COLLECTION AND EVALUATION
- For <span style="color:red"><b>Specimen Evaluation</b></span>, the patient should be instructed to collect a <b>midstream clean catch urine</b> sample of at least 10 mL.
- The <b>most concentrated</b> and ideal sample for <span style="color:red"><b>Routine Urinalysis</b></span> is the <b>first morning voided urine</b>.
- For <span style="color:red"><b>Bacteriologic Examination</b></span>, the preferred methods are <b>catheterized specimen or suprapubic aspiration</b>.
- <span style="color:red"><b>Timed Urine (12- or 24-hour)</b></span> is required for <b>quantitative measurements</b> of urine components.
- <span style="color:red"><b>Specimen Labeling</b></span> is a critical step; a lack of labels is considered a "mortal sin" in clinical microscopy.
- Regarding <span style="color:red"><b>Specimen Processing Time</b></span>, the urine sample should be read <b>within 30 minutes to 1 hour</b>; specimens older than 1 hour without refrigeration must be discarded.
- In <span style="color:red"><b>Specimen Evaluation</b></span>, refrigeration prevents cell lysis but has the drawback of <b>increasing the precipitation of various substances</b>.

### PHYSICAL AND GROSS EXAMINATION
- The <span style="color:red"><b>Urochrome</b></span> pigment is primarily responsible for the <b>characteristic yellow color</b> of urine.
- <span style="color:red"><b>Contributing Pigments</b></span> to urine color include <b>urobilin, uroerythrin, and mesobilifuscin</b> (a byproduct of heme synthesis).
- In <span style="color:red"><b>Diabetes Mellitus</b></span>, urine color may be surprisingly <b>pale despite having a high specific gravity</b>.
- <span style="color:red"><b>Colorless Urine</b></span> is typically associated with <b>Polyuria or Diabetes Insipidus</b>.
- In the context of <span style="color:red"><b>Urine Clarity</b></span>, <b>Phosphate, ammonium urate, and carbonate</b> precipitates will <b>redissolve with the addition of acetic acid</b>.
- For <b>Uric acid and urates</b> causing <span style="color:red"><b>Turbidity</b></span>, the precipitates will <b>redissolve upon warming the specimen to 60°C</b>.
- A <b>uniform opalescence</b> in <span style="color:red"><b>Urine Clarity</b></span> is typically indicative of a <b>bacterial infection</b>.
- The presence of <b>fecal material</b> in a <span style="color:red"><b>Gross Examination</b></span> may suggest a <b>fistulous connection</b>.
- In <span style="color:red"><b>Acute Tubular Necrosis (ATN)</b></span>, a notable physical finding is a complete <b>lack of urine odor</b>.
- Regarding <span style="color:red"><b>Urine Volume</b></span>, <b>Polyuria</b> is defined as an output <b>exceeding 2000 mL</b> per 24 hours.
- <span style="color:red"><b>Oliguria</b></span> is defined as a urine volume <b>less than 500 mL</b> per 24 hours.
- <span style="color:red"><b>Specific Gravity</b></span> measures the <b>relative proportions of dissolved solid components</b> (density) to the total volume.
- The <b>main contributors</b> to <span style="color:red"><b>Urinary Specific Gravity</b></span> are <b>Urea (20%) and Sodium Chloride (25%)</b>.
- <span style="color:red"><b>Isosthenuria</b></span> occurs when the specific gravity is <b>fixed at 1.010</b>, signifying <b>severe renal damage</b>.
- <span style="color:red"><b>Hyposthenuria</b></span> is defined as a specific gravity <b>less than 1.007</b>, commonly seen in <b>Diabetes Insipidus</b>.

### METABOLIC DISORDERS AND URINE ODOR
- <span style="color:red"><b>Ketoacidosis</b></span> is associated with a <b>sweet, fruity</b> urine odor.
- <span style="color:red"><b>Maple Syrup Urine Disease (MSUD)</b></span> results in urine that smells like <b>maple syrup</b>.
- <span style="color:red"><b>Phenylketonuria (PKU)</b></span> is characterized by a <b>mousy or musty</b> urine odor.
- <span style="color:red"><b>Tyrosinemia</b></span> produces a <b>rancid</b> urine odor.
- <span style="color:red"><b>Isovaleric Acidemia</b></span> and <b>Glutaric Acidemia</b> result in urine smelling like <b>sweaty feet</b>.
- <span style="color:red"><b>Cystinuria</b></span> is associated with a <b>rotten egg</b> urine odor.
- <span style="color:red"><b>Trimethylaminuria</b></span> results in an odor of <b>rotting fish</b>.
- <span style="color:red"><b>Methionine Malabsorption</b></span> causes urine to smell like <b>cabbage or hops</b>.
- <span style="color:red"><b>Hawkinsinuria</b></span> is associated with a <b>swimming pool (chlorine)</b> odor.

### CHEMICAL SCREENING (REAGENT STRIPS)
- <span style="color:red"><b>Reagent Strips</b></span> are the <b>primary method</b> used for the chemical examination of urine; they typically contain 10 parameters.
- The <span style="color:red"><b>Principle of Specific Gravity</b></span> on a reagent strip is the <b>pKa change of pretreated polyelectrolytes</b> (an indirect method).
- For <span style="color:red"><b>Chemical Screening</b></span>, <b>Vitamin C (Ascorbic acid)</b> can lead to <b>false-negative glucose</b> results.
- In <span style="color:red"><b>Chemical Screening</b></span>, ignoring the specific reading times (e.g., 30 sec, 60 sec) for different parameters may result in <b>false positives or negatives</b>.
- For <span style="color:red"><b>Reagent Strip Methodology</b></span>, "splitting" or cutting strips into two to save supplies is <b>highly discouraged</b> as it affects result accuracy.

### MICROSCOPIC EXAMINATION: CELLS
- As part of <span style="color:red"><b>Microscopic Evaluation</b></span>, cells and casts begin to <b>lyse within 2 hours</b> of collection.
- <span style="color:red"><b>Erythrocytes (RBCs)</b></span> in normal urine are <b>0–3 per HPF</b>; they appear as <b>pale biconcave disks</b> of ~7 µm.
- <span style="color:red"><b>Shadow Cells (Ghost Cells)</b></span> are <b>erythrocytes</b> in non-fresh specimens where the hemoglobin has dissolved out.
- For <span style="color:red"><b>Microscopic Urinalysis</b></span>, finding <b>more than 3 RBCs/HPF</b> is considered abnormal and can be caused by <b>glomerulonephritis, stones, or trauma</b>.
- <span style="color:red"><b>Dysmorphic RBCs</b></span> must be differentiated from <b>yeast and oil droplets</b>; yeast cells show <b>budding</b>, while oil droplets are highly refractile.
- <span style="color:red"><b>Leukocytes (WBCs)</b></span> in normal urine are <b>0–5 per HPF</b>, and are usually <b>neutrophils</b>.
- In <span style="color:red"><b>Pyuria</b></span>, finding <b>>30 neutrophils/HPF</b> with repeated <b>sterile cultures</b> is suggestive of <b>tuberculosis or nephritis</b>.
- <span style="color:red"><b>Squamous Epithelial Cells</b></span> are the <b>most frequent</b> cells seen in normal urine but are of <b>least clinical significance</b>.
- A high volume of <span style="color:red"><b>Squamous Epithelial Cells</b></span> in females usually indicates <b>improper collection (contamination)</b>.
- <span style="color:red"><b>Transitional Epithelial Cells</b></span> are round or pear-shaped with a <b>centrally located nucleus</b>; they are pathologic if found in <b>clumps or sheets sans instrumentation</b>.
- The presence of increased <span style="color:red"><b>Renal Tubular Epithelial Cells</b></span> indicates <b>tubular damage</b>.

### MICROSCOPIC EXAMINATION: CASTS AND CRYSTALS
- <span style="color:red"><b>Casts</b></span> originate <b>exclusively from the kidney</b> (specifically the renal tubules).
- <span style="color:red"><b>Tamm-Horsfall Protein</b></span> is the specific <b>glycoprotein</b> that forms the matrix of all urinary casts.
- <span style="color:red"><b>Cylindruria</b></span> refers to the <b>presence of casts</b> in the urine sediment.
- <span style="color:red"><b>Hyaline Casts</b></span> appear clear and transparent because they have <b>no cells attached</b> to the protein matrix yet.
- <span style="color:red"><b>Urinary pH</b></span> is the <b>most important factor</b> and <b>main determinant</b> in identifying which crystal will precipitate.
- <span style="color:red"><b>Ammonium Biurate Crystals</b></span> are known as <b>"thorny apples"</b> and appear <b>only in alkaline urine</b>.
- <span style="color:red"><b>Triple Phosphate (Struvite) Crystals</b></span> are characterized by a <b>"coffin lid"</b> appearance.
- <span style="color:red"><b>Calcium Oxalate Monohydrate</b></span> crystals typically present in a <b>dumbbell shape</b>.
- <span style="color:red"><b>Cystine Crystals</b></span> are <b>hexagonal plates</b> found in <b>acidic pH</b>.
- <span style="color:red"><b>Cholesterol Crystals</b></span> appear in the urine as <b>angular or rhomboid</b> shapes with notched corners.
- <span style="color:red"><b>Polarized Light Microscopy</b></span> is the <b>ideal method</b> for properly <b>examining and identifying crystals</b>.

### OTHER MICROSCOPIC FINDINGS
- <span style="color:red"><b>Herpes Simplex Virus</b></span> infections can present with <b>syncytial giant cells</b> and <b>eosinophilic intranuclear inclusions</b> in urine.
- <span style="color:red"><b>Cytomegalovirus (CMV)</b></span> produces <b>basophilic intranuclear inclusions</b> (owl-eye appearance).
- <span style="color:red"><b>Polyomavirus</b></span> creates dense basophilic inclusions that <b>completely fill the nucleus</b> (decoy cells).
- <span style="color:red"><b>Fungi</b></span> found in urine are most commonly <b>Candida albicans</b>.
- <span style="color:red"><b>Parasites</b></span> commonly found in urine include <b>Trichomonas vaginalis, Schistosoma haematobium, and Entamoeba histolytica</b>.

### REPORTING STANDARDS
- In <span style="color:red"><b>Urinalysis Reporting</b></span>, the following are reported per <b>Low Power Field (LPF)</b>: <b>Casts and Abnormal Crystals ("Cast CrAbs")</b>.
- In <span style="color:red"><b>Urinalysis Reporting</b></span>, the following are reported per <b>High Power Field (HPF)</b>: <b>Transitional Epithelia, Trichomonas, Bacteria, Yeast, and Normal Crystals ("TTBaYN")</b>.
- For <span style="color:red"><b>Quantitative Cells</b></span> (RBCs, WBCs, Renal Epithelia), results are reported as a <b>Range per HPF ("RaRe")</b>.

| CONFUSING ENTITIES COMPARED |
| :--- |
| 1. <span style="color:red"><b>Diabetes Insipidus</b></span> presents with <b>colorless urine</b> and <b>low SG (<1.007)</b>, whereas <span style="color:red"><b>Diabetes Mellitus</b></span> may have <b>pale urine</b> but a <b>high SG</b> due to dissolved glucose. |
| 2. <span style="color:red"><b>Amorphous Phosphates</b></span> cause turbidity in <b>alkaline urine</b> and dissolve in <b>acetic acid</b>, while <span style="color:red"><b>Amorphous Urates</b></span> cause turbidity in <b>acidic urine</b> and dissolve with <b>heat (60°C)</b>. |
| 3. <span style="color:red"><b>RBCs</b></span> in fresh urine appear as <b>biconcave disks</b>, but in non-fresh or dilute urine, they become <span style="color:red"><b>Shadow Cells</b></span> lacking hemoglobin. |
| 4. <span style="color:red"><b>Yeast</b></span> cells can be mistaken for RBCs but are distinguished by <b>budding</b> and their <b>resistance to acetic acid</b> (which lyses RBCs). |
| 5. <span style="color:red"><b>Squamous Epithelial Cells</b></span> are larger and flatter with small nuclei (insignificant), while <span style="color:red"><b>Renal Tubular Epithelial Cells</b></span> are smaller, rounder, and indicate <b>tubular necrosis</b>. |
| 6. <span style="color:red"><b>Isosthenuria</b></span> is a fixed <b>SG of 1.010</b> indicating loss of renal concentrating ability, while <span style="color:red"><b>Hyposthenuria</b></span> is a <b>SG <1.007</b>. |
| 7. <span style="color:red"><b>Triple Phosphate</b></span> crystals look like <b>"coffin lids"</b> and appear in <b>alkaline pH</b>, whereas <span style="color:red"><b>Calcium Oxalate</b></span> (dihydrate) looks like <b>"envelopes"</b> and can appear in any pH (usually acid/neutral). |
| 8. <span style="color:red"><b>Ammonium Biurate</b></span> are <b>"thorny apples"</b> found in <b>alkaline</b> urine, but <p style="color:red"><b>Uric Acid</b></p> crystals (varied shapes) are typical of <b>acidic</b> urine. |
| 9. <span style="color:red"><b>Polyuria</b></span> is a volume <b>>2000 mL/24hr</b>, while <span style="color:red"><b>Nocturia</b></span> is specifically excess urine <b>at night (>500 mL)</b> with a low SG. |
| 10. <span style="color:red"><b>Hyaline Casts</b></span> are colorless and composed only of <b>Tamm-Horsfall protein</b>, whereas <span style="color:red"><b>Cellular Casts</b></span> contain trapped <b>RBCs, WBCs, or epithelial cells</b> within the matrix. |
| 11. <span style="color:red"><b>Transitional Epithelial Cells</b></span> have a <b>central nucleus</b> and "umbrella" shape, unlike <span style="color:red"><b>Squamous Cells</b></span> which have a <b>small, centralized nucleus</b> but a massive cytoplasm-to-nucleus ratio. |
| 12. <span style="color:red"><b>Sterile Pyuria</b></span> (high WBCs but negative culture) is a hallmark for <b>Renal Tuberculosis</b> or <b>nephritis</b>. |
| 13. <span style="color:red"><b>Refractometer</b></span> uses the <b>refractive index</b> (indirect) to measure SG, whereas the <span style="color:red"><b>Urinometer</b></span> is a hydrometer (direct) requiring large volumes of urine. |
| 14. <span style="color:red"><b>Reagent Strip SG</b></span> uses <b>pKa change</b> (indirect) and is <b>not affected</b> by high molecular weight substances like radiopaque dye, unlike the refractometer. |
| 15. <span style="color:red"><b>Cystine Crystals</b></span> are <b>hexagonal</b> and signify a metabolic error, whereas <span style="color:red"><b>Uric Acid</b></span> can occasionally be hexagonal but is <b>strongly birefringent</b> under polarized light. |
| 16. <span style="color:red"><b>Brightfield Microscopy</b></span> is standard for general use, but <span style="color:red"><b>Polarized Microscopy</b></span> is required specifically for <b>crystals and lipids</b>. |
| 17. <span style="color:red"><b>Herpes inclusions</b></span> are <b>eosinophilic</b>, while <span style="color:red"><b>CMV and Polyomavirus inclusions</b></span> are typically <b>basophilic</b>. |
| 18. <span style="color:red"><b>Oliguria</b></span> is <b>low output (<500 mL/day)</b>, whereas <span style="color:red"><b>Anuria</b></span> is the <b>near-complete suppression</b> of urine formation. |
| 19. <span style="color:red"><b>Urobilinogen</b></span> and <span style="color:red"><b>Bilirubin</b></span> on dipsticks are often negative in normal urine; their presence usually indicates <b>liver disease or hemolysis</b>. |
| 20. <span style="color:red"><b>Calcium Oxalate Monohydrate</b></span> is <b>dumbbell-shaped</b>, while the <span style="color:red"><b>Dihydrate</b></span> form is <b>envelope-shaped</b>. |

### **BULLET POINTS**

**TOPIC: RENAL PHYSIOLOGY**
* (Context: Renal Physiology) <span style="color:red"><b>Renal Blood Flow (RBF)</b></span> consists of approximately 1200 mL/min, which is about 25% of the total cardiac output.
* (Context: Renal Physiology) <span style="color:red"><b>Renal Plasma Flow (RPF)</b></span> is measured at approximately 600–700 mL/min.
* (Context: Renal Physiology) <span style="color:red"><b>Glomerular Filtration Rate (GFR)</b></span> is approximately 120 mL/min.
* (Context: Renal Physiology) <span style="color:red"><b>Ultrafiltrate Specific Gravity</b></span> in the glomerulus is approximately 1.010.
* (Context: Renal Physiology) <span style="color:red"><b>Glucose renal threshold</b></span> is defined as 160–180 mg/dL, representing the concentration at which reabsorption capacity is exceeded.
* (Context: Renal Physiology) <span style="color:red"><b>Active Transport</b></span> during reabsorption requires energy and carrier proteins for substances like Glucose, Amino acids, Na+, and Cl-.
* (Context: Renal Physiology) <span style="color:red"><b>Proximal Convoluted Tubule (PCT)</b></span> is the major site for reabsorption of glucose, amino acids, and salts, and the major site for tubular secretion.
* (Context: Renal Physiology) <span style="color:red"><b>Ascending Loop of Henle</b></span> is impermeable to water but allows for the reabsorption of Chloride (Cl-).
* (Context: Renal Physiology) <span style="color:red"><b>Descending Loop of Henle</b></span> is permeable to water.
* (Context: Renal Physiology) <span style="color:red"><b>Water reabsorption</b></span> occurs throughout the nephron except in the ascending loop of Henle.
* (Context: Renal Physiology) <span style="color:red"><b>Albumin</b></span> is normally NOT filtered by the glomerulus due to the negative charge barrier.
* (Context: Renal Physiology) <span style="color:red"><b>Tubular Secretion</b></span>'s major functions include eliminating non-filtered substances and regulating acid–base balance via H+ ions.

**TOPIC: RENAL HANDLING MODELS (A-D)**
* (Context: Renal Handling) <span style="color:red"><b>Substance A (Filtration only)</b></span> is the best and ideal substance for testing Glomerular Filtration Rate (GFR).
* (Context: Renal Handling) <span style="color:red"><b>Substance B (Filtration and partial reabsorption)</b></span> describes how Creatinine is handled and is widely used for GFR calculation.
* (Context: Renal Handling) <span style="color:red"><b>Substance C (Filtration and complete reabsorption)</b></span> describes substances like glucose that are filtered but completely reabsorbed and not detected in urine.
* (Context: Renal Handling) <span style="color:red"><b>Substance D (Filtration and Secretion)</b></span> represents substances where almost everything is found in the urine because they are both filtered and secreted.

**TOPIC: CLEARANCE TESTS**
* (Context: Clearance Tests) <span style="color:red"><b>Clearance</b></span> is defined as the volume of plasma completely cleared of a substance per minute (mL/min).
* (Context: Clearance Tests) <span style="color:red"><b>Clearance Formula</b></span> is calculated as C = (U x V) / P, where U is urine concentration, V is urine volume per unit time, and P is plasma concentration.
* (Context: Clearance Tests) <span style="color:red"><b>Ideal clearance substance</b></span> must be freely filtered by the glomerulus, but NOT reabsorbed or secreted by the tubules.
* (Context: Clearance Tests) <span style="color:red"><b>Inulin clearance</b></span> is an exogenous procedure, meaning the substance must be injected into the patient.
* (Context: Clearance Tests) <span style="color:red"><b>Endogenous substances</b></span> used for clearance, such as creatinine, are already present in the blood and excreted at a constant rate.
* (Context: Clearance Tests) <span style="color:red"><b>Urea clearance</b></span> was an endogenous procedure widely used in the past but is less common now.

**TOPIC: CREATININE CLEARANCE & GFR**
* (Context: Creatinine Clearance) <span style="color:red"><b>Creatinine</b></span> is a waste product of muscle metabolism produced enzymatically by creatine phosphokinase from creatine.
* (Context: Creatinine Clearance) <span style="color:red"><b>Creatinine Clearance Disadvantages</b></span> include:
    1. Some creatinine is secreted by the tubules.
    2. Chromogens in plasma can interfere with chemical analysis.
    3. Medications (Gentamicin, Cephalosporins, Cimetidine) can inhibit tubular secretion or affect results.
    4. Bacteria can break down urinary creatinine if left at room temperature.
    5. Heavy meat consumption during the 24-hour collection period influences results.
    6. It is not reliable in muscle-wasting diseases, heavy exercise, or for athletes using creatine supplements.
    7. Results must be corrected for body surface area.
* (Context: GFR Clinical Significance) <span style="color:red"><b>Glomerular Filtration Rate (GFR)</b></span> value does not lie in the detection of early renal disease, but rather in determining the extent of known nephron damage.
* (Context: eGFR) <span style="color:red"><b>Estimated GFR (eGFR)</b></span> formulas like Cockroft-Gault and MDRD are used for routine screening because they require only serum creatinine and no urine collection.
* (Context: eGFR Formulas) <span style="color:red"><b>MDRD formula</b></span> is most widely used and incorporates serum creatinine, age, gender (0.742 multiplier for women), and ethnicity (1.212 multiplier for black patients).
* (Context: eGFR Formulas) <span style="color:red"><b>Schwartz formula</b></span> and the Counahan-Barrett formula are specifically used for estimating GFR in children.

**TOPIC: CYSTATIN C & BETA2-MICROGLOBULIN**
* (Context: Cystatin C) <span style="color:red"><b>Cystatin C</b></span> is a small protein produced at a constant rate by all nucleated cells, which is filtered by the glomerulus and reabsorbed/broken down by tubules.
* (Context: Cystatin C) <span style="color:red"><b>Advantage of Cystatin C</b></span> is that it is independent of muscle mass, making it ideal for pediatric, elderly, and critically ill patients.
* (Context: Beta2-microglobulin) <span style="color:red"><b>Beta2-microglobulin</b></span> dissociates from human leukocyte antigens (HLA) and is removed from plasma by filtration.
* (Context: Beta2-microglobulin) <span style="color:red"><b>Beta2-microglobulin sensitivity</b></span> is higher than creatinine for detecting GFR decreases, but it is not reliable in patients with immunologic disorders or malignancy.

**TOPIC: TUBULAR REABSORPTION (CONCENTRATION TESTS)**
* (Context: Concentration Tests) <span style="color:red"><b>Concentration tests</b></span> determine the ability of the tubules to reabsorb essential salts and water.
* (Context: Osmolality) <span style="color:red"><b>Osmolality</b></span> is a more accurate measure of renal concentrating ability than specific gravity because it measures only the number of particles.
* (Context: Osmometry) <span style="color:red"><b>Freezing Point Osmometer</b></span> measures the depression of the freezing point (1 mol of solute lowers the freezing point by 1.86°C).
* (Context: Osmometry) <span style="color:red"><b>Vapor Pressure Osmometer</b></span> measures the dew point temperature but cannot detect volatile substances like alcohol.
* (Context: Osmolality Reference Values) <span style="color:red"><b>Serum osmolality</b></span> normal range is 275–300 mOsm/kg.
* (Context: Osmolality Ratio) <span style="color:red"><b>Urine:Serum Osmolality Ratio</b></span> should be ≥ 3:1 after fluid restriction.
* (Context: Diabetes Insipidus) <span style="color:red"><b>ADH Challenge</b></span> is used to differentiate neurogenic diabetes insipidus (lack of ADH production) from nephrogenic diabetes insipidus (renal resistance to ADH).

**TOPIC: TUBULAR SECRETION AND RENAL BLOOD FLOW**
* (Context: PAH Test) <span style="color:red"><b>p-aminohippuric acid (PAH) Test</b></span> measures tubular secretion and renal blood flow because it is completely removed from the blood into the urine in one pass.
* (Context: PAH Test) <span style="color:red"><b>Proximal Convoluted Tubule (PCT)</b></span> is the specific site where PAH is secreted from the peritubular capillaries into the urine.

### **DIFFERENTIATING ENTITIES FOR EXAMS**

1. <span style="color:red"><b>Osmolality vs. Specific Gravity</b></span>: Osmolality counts ONLY the number of particles; Specific Gravity is influenced by BOTH the number and the density (size) of particles.
2. <span style="color:red"><b>Neurogenic vs. Nephrogenic Diabetes Insipidus</b></span>: Neurogenic DI responds to exogenous ADH (the kidneys can concentrate urine); Nephrogenic DI does NOT respond to ADH because the tubules are defective.
3. <span style="color:red"><b>Creatinine vs. Cystatin C</b></span>: Creatinine is affected by muscle mass and diet; Cystatin C is independent of muscle mass and is produced at a constant rate by all nucleated cells.
4. <span style="color:red"><b>Inulin vs. Creatinine</b></span>: Inulin is the "gold standard" exogenous substance (filtration only); Creatinine is the most common endogenous substance (filtration and some secretion).
5. <span style="color:red"><b>Beta2-Microglobulin vs. Creatinine Clearance</b></span>: Beta2-microglobulin is a more sensitive indicator of early GFR decrease but is useless in patients with malignancies or immune issues.
6. <span style="color:red"><b>Freezing Point vs. Vapor Pressure Osmometry</b></span>: Freezing point is the standard; Vapor pressure is faster and uses smaller samples but fails to detect volatile substances like Ethanol.
7. <span style="color:red"><b>Active vs. Passive Transport</b></span>: Active transport (e.g., Glucose in PCT) requires energy/carriers; Passive transport (e.g., Urea or Water) moves down a gradient.
8. <span style="color:red"><b>Adult vs. Pediatric eGFR</b></span>: Adults use Cockroft-Gault or MDRD; Children use Schwartz or Counahan-Barrett formulas.
9. <span style="color:red"><b>Afferent vs. Efferent Arteriole</b></span>: Afferent brings blood TO the glomerulus; Efferent carries blood AWAY from the glomerulus toward peritubular capillaries.
10. <span style="color:red"><b>Ascending vs. Descending Loop of Henle</b></span>: The Descending loop is water-permeable; the Ascending loop is water-impermeable (the "diluting segment").
11. <span style="color:red"><b>Substance handling Case C vs. Case D</b></span>: Case C is completely reabsorbed (Glucose); Case D is heavily secreted (PAH).
12. <span style="color:red"><b>Fishberg vs. Mosenthal</b></span>: Fishberg involves water deprivation; Mosenthal involves comparing volume/gravity of day and night samples.
13. <span style="color:red"><b>Creatinine vs. Creatine</b></span>: Creatine is the precursor; Creatine Phosphokinase converts it to Creatinine (the waste product).
14. <span style="color:red"><b>MDRD vs. MDRD-IDMS</b></span>: The constant in the formula changes from 186 to 175 to account for standardization of the creatinine assay.
15. <span style="color:red"><b>Proximal vs. Distal Tubule</b></span>: PCT is the major site of reabsorption and secretion; DCT is primarily for fine-tuning Na+ and acid-base (H+).
16. <span style="color:red"><b>Renal Blood Flow (RBF) vs. Renal Plasma Flow (RPF)</b></span>: RBF is the whole blood (1200 mL); RPF is only the plasma portion (600-700 mL).
17. <span style="color:red"><b>Titratable Acidity vs. Urinary Ammonia</b></span>: Both are tests for tubular secretion and acid-base function.
18. <span style="color:red"><b>Glucose in Urine vs. Blood</b></span>: If blood glucose <160 mg/dL, it shouldn't be in urine; if >180 mg/dL, it will appear in urine due to threshold saturation.
19. <span style="color:red"><b>Alcohol in Osmometry</b></span>: Ethanol will increase osmolality in Freezing Point osmometers but will NOT be detected in Vapor Pressure osmometers.
20. <span style="color:red"><b>Exogenous vs. Endogenous</b></span>: Exogenous markers (Inulin, PAH, Radioisotopes) are more accurate but require injection; Endogenous markers (Creatinine, Urea, Cystatin C) are naturally occurring.

| BASIC PRINCIPLES OF ACID-BASE BALANCE |
| :--- |
* The <font color="red">pH level</font> is the measurement of the balance between acid and base in the body via blood.
* A <font color="red">pH below 7.35</font> indicates the body is in an acidotic state (Acidosis).
* A <font color="red">pH above 7.45</font> indicates the body is in an alkalotic state (Alkalosis).
* <font color="red">Bicarbonate (HCO3-)</font> is considered the primary "Base" in ABG analysis (Memory trick: base = Bicarbonate = double B’s).
* <font color="red">Carbon Dioxide (CO2)</font> acts as an acid in the body (Memory trick: Carbon diACID).
* <font color="red">Hydrogen ions (H2)</font> are highly acidic ions found in stomach acids and urine; an excess leads to an acidotic state.
* <font color="red">Compensation</font> occurs when one organ adjusts its component (acid or base) to steady the pH level and return it to a normal range when another organ is out of balance.

| ROLE OF THE LUNGS AND KIDNEYS |
| :--- |
* The <font color="red">Lungs</font> control the acid balance by regulating CO2 levels.
* <font color="red">Exhaling</font> is the main method the body uses to get rid of CO2.
* A <font color="red">Decreased Respiratory Rate</font> leads to less CO2 being exhaled, resulting in CO2 retention and a more acidic body state (Respiratory Acidosis).
* An <font color="red">Increased Respiratory Rate</font>, such as hyperventilation, causes the body to lose too much CO2, making the body more alkalotic (Respiratory Alkalosis).
* The <font color="red">Kidneys</font> maintain balance by controlling the excretion or retention of Hydrogen ions (acid) and Bicarbonate (base).
* In an <font color="red">Acidotic State</font>, the kidneys will compensate by excreting more H2 ions into the urine and retaining more HCO3.
* In an <font color="red">Alkalotic State</font>, the kidneys will compensate by excreting more HCO3 and retaining more H2 ions.

| CLINICAL CAUSES: RESPIRATORY DISORDERS |
| :--- |
* <font color="red">Respiratory Acidosis</font> is characterized by a "low and slow" respiratory rate (Memory trick: RR SlOOOw = AcidOOOsis).
* <font color="red">Sleep apnea</font> is a cause of Respiratory Acidosis due to airway obstruction occurring at night.
* <font color="red">Head trauma</font> that results in being "knocked out" can cause Respiratory Acidosis due to decreased respiratory drive.
* <font color="red">Postoperative anesthesia</font> recovery causes a low respiratory rate leading to Respiratory Acidosis.
* <font color="red">CNS depressants</font> such as Opiates (Morphine, Hydromorphone), Benzos (Diazepam), and Alcohol intoxication cause Respiratory Acidosis.
* <font color="red">Impaired gas exchange</font> conditions like Pneumonia (thick mucus build-up), COPD, and Asthma attacks lead to CO2 retention and Respiratory Acidosis.
* <font color="red">Respiratory Alkalosis</font> is caused by a fast respiratory rate where the patient "blows off" too much CO2.
* <font color="red">Hyperventilation</font> from anxiety or panic attacks is the hallmark cause of Respiratory Alkalosis.

| CLINICAL CAUSES: METABOLIC DISORDERS |
| :--- |
* <font color="red">Metabolic Alkalosis</font> can be caused by the loss of stomach acid through vomiting.
* <font color="red">NG tube suctioning</font> can lead to Metabolic Alkalosis because acid is being removed from the body.
* <font color="red">Metabolic Acidosis</font> is caused by diarrhea because the intestines hold base, and pooping it all out leaves the body acidic.
* <font color="red">Renal Failure</font> results in Metabolic Acidosis because broken kidneys cannot excrete H2 ions, leading to urine and acid retention.
* <font color="red">Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)</font> is a state of Metabolic Acidosis where the body compensates with Kussmaul respirations.

| ABG PROCEDURE AND TECHINQUE |
| :--- |
* <font color="red">Arterial Blood Gas (ABG)</font> analysis is the best diagnostic test to evaluate oxygenation and ventilation after a traumatic brain injury.
* <font color="red">Abnormal blood gases (ABGs)</font> provide the most important objective data when determining if a client is hypoxic.
* The <font color="red">Allen’s Test</font> must be performed before an ABG procedure to determine the patency of the ulnar artery.
* In the <font color="red">Allen’s Test</font>, the radial and ulnar arteries are occluded while the client makes a fist; the ulnar artery is released, and color should return to the palm within 15 seconds.
* The <font color="red">Arterial Puncture sites</font> include the radial artery (preferred), brachial artery, and femoral artery.
* <font color="red">Skin preparation</font> for an arterial puncture involves cleaning the site with 70% aqueous isopropanol or iodine solution.
* <font color="red">Pressure</font> must be held firmly at the puncture site as the priority intervention immediately following an ABG draw.
* <font color="red">Heparin</font> is used as the anticoagulant in ABG syringes to prevent clotting.
* <font color="red">Excess heparin</font> is the most common preanalytic error in ABG collection, which can dilute the sample and alter results.
* <font color="red">Butterfly infusion sets</font> are not recommended for ABG collection.
* <font color="red">Needle gauge</font> for ABGs is typically between 19–25 gauge.

| ABG INTERPRETATION AND COMPENSATION |
| :--- |
* In the <font color="red">Marching Band Suit method</font> of solving ABGs, pH is primary (on top), CO2 is second, and HCO3 is third.
* To <font color="red">Label an ABG chart</font>, use "ABBA" (Acid-Base-Base-Acid) for pH and CO2 and "BAB" (Base-Acid-Base) for the right side for HCO3.
* <font color="red">Uncompensated ABGs</font> occur when the pH is not in the normal range and the compensating organ has not yet adjusted.
* <font color="red">Partially compensated ABGs</font> occur when the pH is still not in the normal range, but the opposite organ is working/shifting to balance the pH.
* <font color="red">Fully compensated ABGs</font> are identified when the pH has returned to the normal range (7.35-7.45) because of the other organ's adjustments.
* When evaluating <font color="red">Compensation for Metabolic Acidosis</font>, the nurse should expect an increased respiratory rate to blow off acidic CO2.
* If a patient has <font color="red">Respiratory Acidosis</font>, the kidneys are expected to compensate by retaining more Bicarbonate (HCO3).

| DIFFERENTIATING SIMILAR CONCEPTS FOR EXAMS |
| :--- |
* <font color="red">Respiratory Acidosis vs. Respiratory Alkalosis</font>: Acidosis is caused by "low and slow" breathing (CO2 retention), while Alkalosis is caused by "fast" breathing/hyperventilation (CO2 loss).
* <font color="red">Vomiting vs. Diarrhea</font>: Vomiting causes Metabolic Alkalosis (loss of stomach acid), whereas Diarrhea causes Metabolic Acidosis (loss of intestinal base).
* <font color="red">Opiate Overdose vs. Panic Attack</font>: Opiate overdose causes Respiratory Acidosis (bradypnea); a Panic attack causes Respiratory Alkalosis (tachypnea).
* <font color="red">Uncompensated vs. Partially Compensated</font>: In uncompensated, the pH is abnormal and the "helper" value (CO2 or HCO3) is normal; in partially compensated, the pH is abnormal but the "helper" value is also abnormal, showing it is trying to fix the pH.
* <font color="red">Partially Compensated vs. Fully Compensated</font>: In partially compensated, the pH is still outside the 7.35-7.45 range; in fully compensated, the pH is within the 7.35-7.45 range.
* <font color="red">CO2 vs. HCO3</font>: CO2 is the respiratory component (acid controlled by lungs); HCO3 is the metabolic component (base controlled by kidneys).
* <font color="red">Acidotic State Kidney Action vs. Alkalotic State Kidney Action</font>: In acidosis, kidneys excrete H2/retain HCO3; in alkalosis, kidneys excrete HCO3/retain H2.
* <font color="red">Lungs vs. Kidneys Compensation Speed</font>: Lungs compensate for metabolic issues quickly (changing RR); kidneys compensate for respiratory issues more slowly (altering ion excretion).
* <font color="red">Allen’s Test Procedure</font>: High-yield to remember you release the pressure on the ulnar artery specifically to check for collateral circulation.
* <font color="red">Kussmaul Respirations</font>: Specific to Metabolic Acidosis (like DKA) as a compensatory mechanism, not a primary respiratory disorder.
* <font color="red">NG Suctioning vs. Renal Failure</font>: Both are metabolic; however, NG suctioning removes acid (alkalosis), while Renal Failure retains acid (acidosis).
* <font color="red">COPD vs. Hyperventilation</font>: COPD is a chronic state of CO2 retention (Respiratory Acidosis); Hyperventilation is an acute state of CO2 depletion (Respiratory Alkalosis).
* <font color="red">pH 7.32 (Acid) vs. pH 7.37 (Normal, Leaning Acid)</font>: pH 7.32 indicates partial compensation or uncompensation; pH 7.37 (in the presence of abnormal CO2/HCO3) indicates full compensation.
* <font color="red">Radial Artery vs. Ulnar Artery</font>: The radial artery is the site of the ABG puncture; the ulnar artery is the artery tested for patency during the Allen's Test.
* <font color="red">Normal CO2 vs. Normal HCO3 ranges</font>: CO2 (35-45) is simply the pH digits (7.35-7.45) without the 7; HCO3 is slightly lower (22-26).
