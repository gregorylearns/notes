## Summary
The following educational materials are organized to help differentiate and master the concepts of brain death and coma in a clinical setting.

### **Flashcard Bullet Points**

text
* In the context of trauma, a <span style="color:red">GCS 3</span> is interpreted as a critical or comatose state with a very poor prognosis.
* For patients with <span style="color:red">GCS 6-7</span>, clinicians consider the patient to still have some compensatory potential ("kaya pa").
* A <span style="color:red">Subdural Hematoma</span> is clinically identified on CT scan by its crescent-shaped appearance that follows the contour of the brain.
* An <span style="color:red">Epidural Hematoma</span> is distinguished from other bleeds by its lens-shaped or convex appearance on CT.
* In traumatic brain injury, a <span style="color:red">Midline Shift</span> is the result of mass effect pushing structures from the center to the opposite side.
* For any patient presenting with <span style="color:red">GCS 3</span>, immediate intubation and ventilatory support are required.
* During the management of head trauma, <span style="color:red">Cervical Spine Injury</span> must be assumed and the head must not be moved until cleared; manipulation during intubation can worsen the case.
* <span style="color:red">Mannitol</span> is an osmotic diuretic used to decrease intracranial pressure (ICP); clinicians must monitor urine output, urine osmolality, and electrolytes.
* <span style="color:red">Hypertonic Saline</span> is used to reduce ICP similarly to Mannitol, necessitating close monitoring of Sodium levels.
* Temporary <span style="color:red">Hyperventilation</span> to manage ICP aims to maintain CO2 between 30-40 mmHg; CO2 must never drop below 20 mmHg as it can lead to dangerous vasodilation.
* <span style="color:red">Head Elevation</span> to 30 degrees is a non-medical decompression method used to facilitate venous drainage and decrease ICP.
* In the management of <span style="color:red">Cerebral Edema</span>, clinicians should prioritize euvolemia and renal function over aggressive fluid reduction to stabilize electrolyte imbalances.
* The <span style="color:red">Cerebral Perfusion Pressure (CPP)</span> is calculated by subtracting ICP from the Mean Arterial Pressure (MAP).
* The <span style="color:red">Mean Arterial Pressure (MAP)</span> formula is [Systolic BP + 2(Diastolic BP)] divided by 3.
* A hallmark physical exam finding in <span style="color:red">Uncal Herniation</span> is ipsilateral anisocoria (unequal pupil size on the same side as the lesion).
* The <span style="color:red">Brainstem Reflexes</span> assessed for brain death include the pupillary reflex (CN II, III), corneal reflex (CN V), and gag reflex (CN IX, X).
* A <span style="color:red">Negative Apnea Test</span>, defined as no spontaneous breathing after removal from the ventilator despite hypercapnia, is a critical finding for brain death.
* According to <span style="color:red">Republic Act No. 7170 (Organ Donation Act)</span>, death is defined as the irreversible cessation of cardiac, respiratory, and all brain functions, including the brainstem.
* <span style="color:red">Brain Death</span> is defined as the irreversible loss of all brain functions, including the brainstem, characterized by coma, absence of brainstem reflexes, and a positive apnea test. 
* <span style="color:red">Informed Consent</span> for a brain death declaration is not mandatory as it is a diagnostic process, but it is encouraged and desirable for legal protection in organ donations.
* For the <span style="color:red">Brain Death Observation Period</span>, hypothermic patients must be rewarmed to normal temperature before the examination can be validly performed.
* Prerequisites for a <span style="color:red">Brain Death Examination</span> include an established etiology, irreversibility of vital functions, temperature >38C, and systolic BP >100mmHg (MAP >60mmHg).
* During <span style="color:red">Brain Death Determination</span>, the two examiners may perform the exam independently, simultaneously, or consecutively.
* <span style="color:red">Sensorium</span> represents the level and stability of consciousness and is a function of the cerebral cortex and the Activating Reticular System (ARAS).
* The <span style="color:red">Vegetative State (VS/UWS)</span> is characterized by wakefulness without clinical signs of awareness, often including spontaneous breathing and open eyes.
* <span style="color:red">Minimally Conscious State (MCS)</span> is differentiated from VS by the presence of minimal, inconsistent but reproducible signs of consciousness, such as following simple commands.
* <span style="color:red">MCS Plus</span> is a subtype where patients can follow commands, produce intelligible words, or display intentional communication.
* <span style="color:red">MCS Minus</span> is a subtype where patients show voluntary behaviors like localization to pain or visual pursuit, but no language processing.
* <span style="color:red">Stupor</span> is a state where a patient can only be transiently awakened by vigorous, noxious stimuli, usually involving withdrawal from the stimulus.
* <span style="color:red">Hyperactive Delirium</span> is classified as a disorder of mental content/function without a reduced level of consciousness.
* <span style="color:red">Hypoactive Delirium</span> involves a mildly reduced level of consciousness and is classified as a disorder of arousal.
* <span style="color:red">Locked-in Syndrome</span> is a coma mimic where the patient is aware but paralyzed, often seen in specific brainstem lesions.
* In the <span style="color:red">Initial Management of Coma</span>, ABCs must be stabilized, maintaining MAP 60-70, CO2 <40 (but >20), and O2 Sat >90%.
* <span style="color:red">Flumazenil</span> is the specific reversal agent given to comatose patients suspected of benzodiazepine overdose.
* <span style="color:red">Raycoon's eyes</span> are a general examination finding suggestive of base of skull fracture in trauma cases.
* In <span style="color:red">Methanol Poisoning</span>, a fundus examination may reveal a congested and edematous retina with blurred disc margins.
* In <span style="color:red">Lead Poisoning</span>, greyish deposits may be seen around the optic disc during a fundus examination.
* <span style="color:red">Terson Syndrome</span> refers to subhyaloid hemorrhage seen in rapid ICP increases, such as Subarachnoid Hemorrhage.
* <span style="color:red">Papilledema</span> is a diagnostic sign of chronic increased ICP, hypertensive encephalopathy, or intracranial mass lesions.
* <span style="color:red">Cheyne-Stokes Respiration</span> typically indicates a lesion in the diencephalon, thalamus, or bilateral hemispheres.
* <span style="color:red">Ataxic Breathing</span>, characterized by an irregular and gasping pattern, localizes the lesion to the medulla.
* <span style="color:red">Metabolic Encephalopathy</span> is clinically distinguished by "pinpoint" pupils that remain reactive to light.
* <span style="color:red">Uncal Herniation</span> causes a dilated and fixed pupil that is unequal to the contralateral side (anisocoria).
* <span style="color:red">Conjugate Lateral Eye Deviation</span> often localizes to an ipsilateral lesion in the frontal eye fields.
* <span style="color:red">Ocular Bobbing</span>, involving rapid downward jerks followed by a slow return to mid-position, is specific for an acute pontine lesion.
* <span style="color:red">Ocular Dipping</span> (inverse ocular bobbing) involves an initial slow downward phase followed by a rapid return.
* Before performing the <span style="color:red">Oculocephalic Reflex (Doll’s Eye)</span> maneuver, cervical spine injury must be strictly excluded.
* During <span style="color:red">Caloric Testing (Vestibulo-ocular reflex)</span>, cool water produces a response in the opposite direction, while warm water produces a response in the same direction.
* <span style="color:red">Metabolic Encephalopathy</span> is associated with normal results on oculocephalic maneuvers and caloric stimulation.
* The <span style="color:red">FOUR Score</span> is preferred for non-traumatic coma or ventilated/sedated patients as it provides more neurological data than the GCS.
* Each 1-point improvement in the <span style="color:red">FOUR Score</span> correlates with a 15% decrease in mortality.
* <span style="color:red">Midazolam</span> infusion can cause pinpoint pupils due to its GABAergic effects.
* <span style="color:red">Propofol</span> typically constricts the pupil, but the pupil will dilate upon the application of pain.
* <span style="color:red">Dexmedetomidine</span> is an alpha-2 agonist that causes sedation and amnesia without respiratory depression, allowing patients to be easily aroused.
* For <span style="color:red">Apnea Test</span> completion, the pCO2 must reach >60 mmHg or show a 20 mmHg increase from baseline post-test.
* The <span style="color:red">Apnea Test</span> must be stopped immediately if the patient's blood pressure falls below 90 mmHg.
* An <span style="color:red">Empty Skull Sign</span> on FDG PET imaging is a unique finding that confirms brain death.
* <span style="color:red">EEG Silence</span> (isoelectric EEG) is a mandatory confirmatory test in many protocols to support clinical brain death diagnosis.
* The <span style="color:red">Dorsolateral Pons</span> is one of the two primary brainstem sites where a lesion can cause coma.

### **Distinguishing Similar Entities & Exam Tips**

1.  **<span style="color:red">Subdural vs. Epidural Hematoma</span>**: Subdural is crescent and follows the brain surface (venous); Epidural is lens-shaped and doesn't cross sutures (arterial).
2.  **<span style="color:red">VS vs. MCS</span>**: VS has eyes open but no "awareness" of environment; MCS shows "minimal" indicators like following a person's movement with eyes or squeezing a hand on command.
3.  **<span style="color:red">Decorticate vs. Decerebrate Posturing</span>**: Flexion (Decorticate) is "higher up" (Midbrain); Extension (Decerebrate) is "lower down" (Pons/Medulla) and much worse.
4.  **<span style="color:red">Pupils in Metabolic vs. Pontine Lesions</span>**: Both can be small, but Metabolic pupils usually "react" to light; Pontine pupils are often "pinpoint and fixed."
5.  **<span style="color:red">Ocular Bobbing vs. Ocular Dipping</span>**: Bobbing is "Fast Down, Slow Up" (Pontine); Dipping is "Slow Down, Fast Up."
6.  **<span style="color:red">GCS vs. FOUR Score</span>**: Use GCS for trauma; use FOUR Score for patients on ventilators or those who are heavily sedated.
7.  **<span style="color:red">Doll’s Eye vs. Caloric Test</span>**: Doll’s eye involves moving the head (unsafe if C-spine isn't cleared); Caloric test uses water in the ear and is safer in trauma once the eardrum is verified intact.
8.  **<span style="color:red">Hypoactive Delirium vs. Coma</span>**: Delirious patients have a disturbance in "attention"; comatose patients have a total loss of "arousability."
9.  **<span style="color:red">Apneustic vs. Ataxic Breathing</span>**: Apneustic is a pause at full inspiration (Pons); Ataxic is completely random and unpredictable (Medulla - terminal).
10. **<span style="color:red">Anisocoria in Uncal Herniation</span>**: The dilated pupil is on the **same side** (ipsilateral) as the mass lesion due to CN III compression.

### **General Neuromuscular Principles**
*   The <span style="color:red">lower motor neuron (LMN) unit</span> encompasses the anterior horn cell, the peripheral motor nerve, the neuromuscular junction, and the muscle fiber.
*   In <span style="color:red">Neuromuscular disorders</span>, the distribution of weakness, presence of sensory deficits, and status of deep tendon reflexes (DTRs) are the primary tools for localization.
*   <span style="color:red">Sensory deficits</span> combined with increased DTRs suggest a spinal cord lesion rather than an LMN disorder.
*   <span style="color:red">Bulbar and ocular muscle involvement</span> specifically suggests a Neuromuscular Junction (NMJ) disorder like Myasthenia Gravis.

### **Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (ALS)**
*   <span style="color:red">Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (ALS)</span> is identified as the **most common progressive motor neuron disease** and the **most devastating** neurodegenerative disorder.
*   The <span style="color:red">Pathologic hallmark of ALS</span> is the simultaneous death of LMNs in the spinal cord/brainstem and UMNs in layer 5 of the motor cortex*.
*   In <span style="color:red">ALS Pathology</span>, a remarkable feature is the **selectivity of neuronal involvement**, sparing ocular motility, sacral parasympathetic neurons (bowel/bladder), and sensory apparatus*.
*   <span style="color:red">Spheroids</span> in ALS are focal enlargements in proximal motor axons caused by accumulations of neurofilaments and proteins.
*   <span style="color:red">LMN dysfunction in ALS</span> presents as insidious asymmetric distal limb weakness, cramping during volitional movements, and progressive wasting/atrophy.
*   <span style="color:red">Hand extensors</span> are typically weaker than flexors in patients suffering from <span style="color:red">ALS</span>.
*   <span style="color:red">Fasciculations</span>, which are involuntary contractions of muscle fibers, are **best seen in ALS**.
*   <span style="color:red">Pseudobulbar affect</span> in ALS is characterized by involuntary excess in weeping or laughing, caused by bilateral corticobulbar tract lesions.
*   The <span style="color:red">Definite ALS Diagnosis</span> requires evidence of UMN and LMN involvement in 3 out of 4 regions: bulbar, cervical, thoracic, and lumbosacral.
*   <span style="color:red">ALS Epidemiology</span> shows a median survival of only **3-5 years**, with death usually resulting from respiratory paralysis.
*   <span style="color:red">Riluzole</span> is a pharmacological treatment for ALS that is known to prolong survival.
*   <span style="color:red">ALS with Frontotemporal Dementia (FTD)</span> is the specific variant where cognitive functions are not spared.

### **Myasthenia Gravis (MG)**
*   <span style="color:red">Myasthenia Gravis (MG)</span> is a neuromuscular junction disorder defined by the cardinal features of **weakness and fatigability of skeletal muscles**.
*   <span style="color:red">Fatigability</span> in MG is defined as muscle weakness that worsens with repeated use and improves with rest.
*   The <span style="color:red">Fundamental defect in Myasthenia Gravis</span> is an antibody-mediated attack on nicotinic acetylcholine receptors (AChR), decreasing the number of available receptors at the postsynaptic fold.
*   <span style="color:red">Anti-AChR antibodies</span> are the **most common** antibodies causing MG, found in 85% of generalized cases.
*   The <span style="color:red">Thymus gland</span> is abnormal in 75% of AChR-positive MG patients, with 10% having a **thymoma**.
*   <span style="color:red">Ptosis</span> is one of the **earliest clinical manifestations** of MG because the eyes are always open and moving, making them highly susceptible to fatigability*.
*   <span style="color:red">Cranial muscle involvement in MG</span> leads to a "snarling" facial expression, nasal speech, and difficulty swallowing.
*   <span style="color:red">Ocular MG</span> is restricted to extraocular muscles; if it stays restricted for **3 years**, it is unlikely to generalize.
*   The <span style="color:red">Ice Pack Test</span> for MG works because cold reduces the activity of acetylcholinesterase, improving ptosis.
*   <span style="color:red">Repetitive Nerve Stimulation (RNS)</span> in MG shows a **>10% reduction** in Muscle Action Potential (MAP) amplitude at rates of 2-3 stimuli per second.
*   <span style="color:red">Edrophonium (Tensilon)</span> is used for the Anticholinesterase Test due to its rapid onset (30s) and short duration (5 min).
*   <span style="color:red">Pyridostigmine</span> is the most widely used anticholinesterase medication for MG, with an onset of 15-30 minutes.
*   <span style="color:red">Thymectomy</span> is indicated for all patients with thymoma and for many AChR-positive non-thymomatous <span style="color:red">MG</span> patients to improve long-term strength.
*   <span style="color:red">Allopurinol</span> must be avoided in patients taking Azathioprine for MG due to the risk of **severe bone marrow suppression**.
*   <span style="color:red">Myasthenic Crisis</span> is a life-threatening exacerbation of weakness causing respiratory failure, most commonly triggered by <font color="red">intercurrent infection</font>.

### **Duchenne and Becker Muscular Dystrophy (DMD/BMD)**
*   <span style="color:red">Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy (DMD)</span> is the **most common hereditary neuromuscular disease**, affecting 1 in 3,600 liveborn boys.
*   The <span style="color:red">Dystrophin gene</span>, located at **Xp21**, is the largest human gene; its absence causes DMD.
*   <span style="color:red">Gowers sign</span> is a classic finding in DMD where the child use their hands to "climb up" their legs to stand due to pelvic girdle weakness.
*   <span style="color:red">Calf pseudohypertrophy</span> in DMD is caused by the proliferation of connective tissue and fat, not muscle.
*   <span style="color:red">Intellectual impairment</span> is seen in **ALL patients** with DMD, though the severity varies.
*   <span style="color:red">Serum Creatine Kinase (CK)</span> in DMD is **greatly elevated** (15,000–35,000 IU/L); a normal CK is incompatible with the diagnosis unless it's the terminal stage.
*   <span style="color:red">DMD mortality</span> usually occurs in the late teens to 20s due to respiratory failure or cardiomyopathy.
*   <span style="color:red">Becker Muscular Dystrophy (BMD)</span> is a milder variant of DMD where dystrophin is <font color="red">semi-functional</font> rather than absent, leading to later onset and longer life expectancy.
*   <span style="color:red">Glucocorticoid therapy</span> (Prednisone) is the mainstay for DMD to slow the decline of muscle strength and delay scoliosis.

### **Spinal Muscular Atrophy (SMA)**
*   <span style="color:red">Spinal Muscular Atrophy (SMA)</span> is a degenerative disease of the <font color="red">lower motor neurons</font> caused by a homozygous deletion in the **SMN1 gene** at 5q13.
*   <span style="color:red">SMA Type 1 (Werdnig-Hoffmann)</span> is the most common phenotype, presenting before age 6 months with severe hypotonia and "frog-leg" posture.
*   <span style="color:red">Tongue fasciculations</span> are a specific and important clinical sign of denervation in <span style="color:red">SMA Type 1</span>.
*   <span style="color:red">SMA Type 1 patients</span> typically never achieve head control and die within the first 2 years without ventilatory support.
*   The <span style="color:red">Diaphragm and Extraocular muscles</span> are notably **spared** in SMA Type 1.
*   <span style="color:red">SMA Type 2</span> patients can sit but never walk, while <span style="color:red">SMA Type 3 (Kugelberg-Welander)</span> patients are able to walk but may lose the ability later.
*   <span style="color:red">Polyminimyoclonus</span> in SMA is a fine tremor of outstretched fingers caused by fasciculations.
*   The <span style="color:red">SMN2 gene copy number</span> is inversely correlated with the clinical severity of SMA (more SMN2 = milder disease).

### **Pathologic/Diagnostic Differentiations**
*   <span style="color:red">Lambert-Eaton Myasthenic Syndrome (LEMS)</span> is a **presynaptic** disorder distinguished from MG by **depressed DTRs** and **autonomic changes** (dry mouth/impotence).
*   <span style="color:red">LEMS Electrophysiology</span> shows an **incremental response** (increase in amplitude) on high-frequency stimulation, unlike the decrement seen in MG.
*   <span style="color:red">Botulism</span> is a toxin-mediated NMJ disorder that presents with myasthenia-like weakness but includes early autonomic findings and a reduction in CMAP.
*   <span style="color:red">Muscle Contractures</span> differ from cramps because they are associated with **energy failure** (glycolytic disorders) and show **electrical silence** on EMG.
*   <span style="color:red">Inclusion Body Myositis</span> is unique due to its **asymmetric** weakness of the <font color="red">finger flexors and quadriceps</font>.
*   <span style="color:red">Isaac's Syndrome</span> (acquired neuromyotonia) is caused by antibodies against potassium channels, resulting in continuous muscle fiber activity and sweating.
*   <span style="color:red">Stiff-Person Syndrome</span> characteristically involves antibodies against **glutamic acid decarboxylase** and exhibits muscles that relax during sleep.
*   <span style="color:red">Polymyalgia Rheumatica</span> involves shoulder and hip stiffness in patients >50 years old but has **normal CK and EMG**, unlike inflammatory myopathies.
*   <span style="color:red">True hypertrophy</span> is seen in Limb-Girdle MD, whereas **pseudohypertrophy** is the classic feature of DMD.
*   <span style="color:red">Elevated GGT</span> is used to establish that elevated AST/ALT is of liver origin rather than muscle origin.
*   <span style="color:red">Creatine Kinase (CK)</span> is the **most sensitive measure** of muscle damage, with the MM fraction being specific to skeletal muscle.
*   <span style="color:red">Forearm Exercise Test</span> identifies glycolytic defects if there is an **absent rise in lactic acid** after vigorous hand exercise.

### **Distinguishing Similar Entities (Exam High Yield)**
*   <font color="red">ALS vs. Cervical Cord Compression</font>: Normal cranial nerves and normal radiologic studies of the spine favor **ALS**, whereas cervical compression often has sensory levels.
*   <font color="red">MG vs. LEMS</font>: MG is postsynaptic (AChR) with normal reflexes; LEMS is presynaptic (Ca++ channel) with **depressed/absent DTRs** and autonomic symptoms.
*   <font color="red">MG vs. Botulism</font>: Botulism often presents with **early autonomic findings** and fixed/dilated pupils, which are absent in MG. 
*   <font color="red">ALS vs. SMA</font>: ALS involves both **UMN and LMN**; SMA is strictly an **LMN** disease.
*   <font color="red">DMD vs. BMD</font>: DMD has **onset <5 years** and <3% dystrophin; BMD has later onset and **semi-functional** dystrophin.
*   <font color="red">Myasthenic Crisis vs. Cholinergic Crisis</font>: Myasthenic crisis is caused by **too little** medication/infection (improves with Edrophonium); Cholinergic crisis is from **overdose** (worsens with Edrophonium).
*   <font color="red">Asthenia vs. Pathologic Fatigability</font>: Asthenia is a **subjective** feeling of tiredness; pathologic fatigability (MG) is an **objective** inability to sustain force.
*   <font color="red">Muscle Cramps vs. Myotonia</font>: Cramps are short-duration and triggered by contraction; myotonia is **prolonged contraction** followed by **slow relaxation** (difficulty releasing a grip).
*   <font color="red">SMA Type 1 vs. SMA Type 3</font>: Type 1 is the **most severe** (cannot sit, death <2 years); Type 3 is the **mildest** (can walk into adulthood).
*   <font color="red">Fasciculations in SMA vs. DMD</font>: Fasciculations (tongue) are a **salient feature of SMA** but are characteristically **absent in DMD**.
*   <font color="red">Bulbar Palsy vs. Pseudobulbar Palsy</font>: Bulbar palsy is an LMN lesion of the brainstem; **Pseudobulbar palsy** is an UMN lesion of the corticobulbar tracts (often showing the pseudobulbar affect).
*   <font color="red">MMCB vs. ALS</font>: Multifocal Motor Neuropathy with Conduction Block (MMCB) mimics ALS but is restricted to **LMN only** and is potentially treatable with IVIG.
*   <font color="red">Primary Lateral Sclerosis (PLS) vs. ALS</font>: PLS is purely **UMN**; ALS must eventually show **both UMN and LMN** symptoms.
*   <font color="red">Ice Pack Test vs. Edrophonium Test</font>: Ice pack test is specifically for **ptosis**; Edrophonium test can be used for ptosis or **limb weakness/dysarthria**.
*   <font color="red">DMD vs. SMA Type 1 Intelligence</font>: DMD patients frequently have **intellectual impairment**; SMA patients typically have **normal or superior** intelligence.
*   <font color="red">DMD vs. SMA Type 1 Weakness</font>: DMD weakness starts in **toddlerhood** (age 2-3); SMA Type 1 weakness is present at **birth or within 6 months**.
*   <font color="red">Post-Poliomyelitis vs. ALS</font>: Post-polio is a **delayed deterioration** of motor neurons in patients with a history of polio, whereas ALS is uniquely progressive in both UMN/LMN.
*   <font color="red">Cramps vs. Contractures (EMG)</font>: Cramps show high-frequency motor unit discharges; contractures show **electrical silence**.
*   <font color="red">Myotonia Congenita vs. Myotonic MD</font>: Myotonia congenita involves **no prominent weakness**; Myotonic MD involves significant muscle weakness and wasting.
*   <font color="red">SMA Type 1 Sparing</font>: SMA Type 1 **spares the diaphragm**, whereas DMD eventually involves and causes failure of the diaphragm.

65. How is <b><font color="red">Lambert-Eaton Myasthenic Syndrome (LEMS)</font></b> distinguished from MG? | Depressed DTRs; autonomic changes. <br>LEMS is a presynaptic disorder (dry mouth/impotence).
66. What is the electrophysiologic finding in <b><font color="red">LEMS</font></b>? | Incremental response. <br>Increase in amplitude on high-frequency stimulation.
67. How does <b><font color="red">Botulism</font></b> differ from MG in presentation? | Early autonomic findings; reduced CMAP. <br>A toxin-mediated NMJ disorder.
68. How do <b><font color="red">Muscle Contractures</font></b> differ from cramps? | Energy failure; electrical silence. <br>Associated with glycolytic disorders and silent EMG.
69. What is the unique presentation of <b><font color="red">Inclusion Body Myositis</font></b>? | Asymmetric finger flexor/quad weakness. <br>Distinctive distribution of weakness.
70. What is the cause of <b><font color="red">Isaac's Syndrome</font></b> (acquired neuromyotonia)? | Potassium channel antibodies. <br>Continuous muscle fiber activity and sweating.
71. What antibody is associated with <b><font color="red">Stiff-Person Syndrome</font></b>? | Anti-GAD (glutamic acid decarboxylase). <br>Muscles relax during sleep.
72. Contrast <b><font color="red">Polymyalgia Rheumatica</font></b> with inflammatory myopathies. | Normal CK and EMG. <br>Involves stiffness in shoulder/hip in patients >50.
73. <b><font color="red">True hypertrophy</font></b> vs <b><font color="red">Pseudohypertrophy</font></b>: which diseases? | True: Limb-Girdle MD. <br>Pseudo: DMD.
74. Why is <b><font color="red">Elevated GGT</font></b> measured alongside AST/ALT? | Confirms liver origin. <br>Distinguishes liver damage from muscle damage.
75. What is the most sensitive measure of muscle damage? | <b><font color="red">Creatine Kinase (CK)</font></b>. <br>The MM fraction is specific to skeletal muscle.

83. Distinguish <b><font color="red">Asthenia vs. Pathologic Fatigability</font></b>. | Asthenia: Subjective tiredness. <br>Fatigability: Objective inability to sustain force.
84. Distinguish <b><font color="red">Muscle Cramps vs. Myotonia</font></b>. | Cramps: Short-duration. <br>Myotonia: Prolonged contraction, slow relaxation.

86. Where are <b><font color="red">Fasciculations</font></b> found comparing SMA and DMD? | Present in SMA; absent in DMD. <br>Tongue fasciculations are salient in SMA.
87. Distinguish <b><font color="red">Bulbar vs. Pseudobulbar Palsy</font></b>. | Bulbar: LMN (brainstem). <br>Pseudobulbar: UMN (corticobulbar tracts).
88. Distinguish <b><font color="red">MMCB vs. ALS</font></b>. | MMCB: LMN only, treatable. <br>Multifocal Motor Neuropathy with Conduction Block responds to IVIG.
89. Distinguish <b><font color="red">Primary Lateral Sclerosis (PLS) vs. ALS</font></b>. | PLS: Purely UMN. <br>ALS: Eventually both UMN and LMN.

92. Distinguish <b><font color="red">DMD vs. SMA Type 1</font></b> weakness onset. | DMD: Toddlerhood (2-3 yrs). <br>SMA Type 1: Birth or <6 months.
