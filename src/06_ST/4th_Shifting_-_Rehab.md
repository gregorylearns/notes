* General Principles: In <font color="red">Rehabilitation Medicine</font>, the "right time" to start is "now" (upon admission) to prevent complications like contractures and cardiovascular deconditioning.
* General Principles: For <font color="red">Deconditioning Syndrome</font>, immobility is considered debilitating and disabling regardless of whether the individual is sick or healthy.
* General Principles: In the <font color="red">United States healthcare system</font>, rehabilitation is often automatic; nurses call therapists as soon as a patient is transferred to a station/floor.
* General Principles: In the <font color="red">Philippines</font>, rehabilitation is often treated as an "add-on" or "additional baggage" rather than an integral part of care.
* General Principles: The <font color="red">Physiatry specialty</font> was formerly called Physical Medicine and Rehabilitation, emphasizing the use of physical modalities (EMG, electrotherapy, exercise).
* General Principles: For <font color="red">Physiatrists</font>, the focus of treatment is a process designed to improve daily function through medications, physical energy interventions, and experiential training.
* Clinical Application: Regarding <font color="red">Back pain prevention</font>, individuals in a period of physical inactivity for more than 30 minutes are at risk and should move or walk every half hour.
* Clinical Application: In <font color="red">Stroke Recovery</font>, mature cortical neurons do not regenerate; recovery is achieved through motor relearning and cortical reorganization.
* Clinical Application: In <font color="red">Cardiovascular health</font>, a resting heart rate exceeding 80 beats per minute is an indicator of significant deconditioning and increased mortality risk.
* Clinical Application: For <font color="red">Abdominal Surgery</font>, mobilization is critical as early as post-op Day 1 to prevent adhesions; surgeons must reassure patients that sutures (multiple layers) are unlikely to dehisce unless infected.
* Clinical Application: In <font color="red">Oncologic Rehabilitation</font>, the objective is to ensure the patient "dies happy," involving a multidisciplinary team including chaplains.
* Referral Systems: A <font color="red">Blanket Referral</font> (as used in VSMMC/Sotto) allows the Rehab team to treat admitted patients even without a specific formal request, ensuring early mobilization.
* Referral Systems: In the <font color="red">Philippine Referral System</font>, a patient typically sees a specialist (e.g., Ortho/Neuro) BEFORE being referred to Rehab, which can increase the financial burden.
* Historical Background: In <font color="red">12th Century Medicine</font>, Maimonides emphasized that a sedentary life leads to pain and waned strength despite good nutrition.
* Historical Background: In <font color="red">20th Century Cardiac Care</font>, old guidelines recommended 3–8 weeks of bed rest for MI; modern medicine rejects this due to the deleterious effects of immobility.
* Space Medicine: In the <font color="red">Space program</font>, exercise is a positive treatment mode used to counteract muscle atrophy caused by weightlessness/lack of gravity.

### DIFFERENTIAL COMPARISONS FOR EXAMS

1. <font color="red">Activity Limitation (Disability)</font> vs. <font color="red">Participation Restriction (Handicap)</font>: Activity Limitation is a personal difficulty in executing a task (e.g., can’t climb stairs), whereas Participation Restriction is a societal difficulty in life situations (e.g., can’t enter a building because there are only stairs).
2. <font color="red">Body Functions</font> vs. <font color="red">Body Structures</font>: Body Functions are physiological or psychological (e.g., hearing, pumping blood), while Body Structures are anatomical parts (e.g., the ear, the heart muscle).
3. <font color="red">Capacity</font> vs. <font color="red">Performance</font>: Capacity is what a patient "can do" in a controlled clinical environment; Performance is what they "actually do" in their usual home or social environment.
4. <font color="red">Environmental Facilitator</font> vs. <font color="red">Environmental Barrier</font>: A facilitator assists function (e.g., a prosthetic limb or a ramp), while a barrier hinders it (e.g., lack of public transport or negative social attitudes).
5. <font color="red">Primary Prevention</font> vs. <font color="red">Secondary Prevention</font>: Primary prevention uses exercise to prevent disease onset in healthy individuals; Secondary prevention initiates rehab during the acute phase of an illness to prevent complications.
6. <font color="red">Restoration</font> vs. <font color="red">Optimization</font>: Rehabilitation medicine prioritizes Optimization (maximizing residual function) over Restoration (returning exactly to the pre-injury state), especially in neurological injuries.
7. <font color="red">Old MI Guidelines</font> vs. <font color="red">Modern MI Guidelines</font>: Old guidelines required up to 8 weeks of bed rest; modern guidelines start cardiac rehabilitation as early as Post-Op Day 1 to avoid immobility effects.
8. <font color="red">Aged Population</font> vs. <font color="red">Aging Population</font>: A population is "Aging" when 7% are 65+; it is "Aged" when 14% are 65+.
9. <font color="red">Skilled Nursing Facility</font> vs. <font color="red">Assisted Living Facility</font>: Skilled nursing provides 24/7 medical/nursing care; Assisted living is for independent individuals who need help with minor tasks like marketing or therapy.
10. <font color="red">Impairment (Stroke)</font>: In stroke, <font color="red">Hemiparesis</font> is a physical impairment, whereas <font color="red">Aphasia</font> is a communication impairment; both can lead to different activity limitations.
11. <font color="red">Direct Referral (International)</font> vs. <font color="red">Indirect Referral (Philippines)</font>: Internationally, therapists (DPT) can be first-contact practitioners; in the Philippines, specialized doctors usually act as the mandatory primary gatekeepers.
12. <font color="red">Physiatry</font> vs. <font color="red">Physical Therapy</font>: A Physiatrist is a Medical Doctor (MD) who diagnoses and prescribes; a Physical Therapist (PT) implements the physical interventions and exercises.
13. <font color="red">Labile Cells</font> vs. <font color="red">Nerve Cells</font>: Labile cells regenerate easily; Nerve cells do not regenerate, necessitating <font color="red">Neuroplasticity</font> for functional recovery.
14. <font color="red">Tertiary Prevention</font> vs. <font color="red">Hospice</font>: Tertiary prevention aims for the highest independence (e.g., walking); Hospice focuses on comfort and "dying happy" when recovery is no longer the goal.
15. <font color="red">Personal Factors</font> vs. <font color="red">Environmental Factors</font>: Personal factors are internal to the patient (age, grit, education); Environmental factors are external (laws, architecture, family support).

### PHYSIATRIC HISTORY AND PHYSICAL EXAMINATION

| Feature | Physiatric History and Functional Assessment |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Primary Focus** | The <font color="red">Physiatric History</font> emphasizes function and the deterioration of function over time rather than just a medical diagnosis. |
| **History Components** | The <font color="red">Functional History</font> must includes a chronological account of mobility, Activities of Daily Living (ADLs), and Instrumental Activities of Daily Living (IADLs). |
| **ADL vs. IADL** | <font color="red">Activities of Daily Living (ADLs)</font> are tasks performed on oneself (e.g., feeding, grooming, dressing), while <font color="red">Instrumental Activities of Daily Living (IADLs)</font> include complex environmental tasks (e.g., grocery shopping, cooking). |
| **Rehabilitation Goal** | The <font color="red">Past Medical History</font> in a rehab context is used to determine the goal of treatment: to regain the functional level the patient had immediately prior to the present admission. |
| **Pain Theory** | The <font color="red">Gate Control Theory</font> explains that applying heat or massage activates large, fast, myelinated <font color="red">A-beta fibers</font>, which reach the brain faster than slow, unmyelinated <font color="red">C fibers</font>, thereby prioritizing warmth over dull pain. |
| **Differentiating Pain** | A <font color="red">Lumbar strain</font> is often suspected when back pain is caused by the sudden lifting of a heavy object and presents without radiation to the extremities. |

### SPECIALIZED PHYSICAL EXAMINATION SIGNS

| Sign/Test | Clinical Significance and Finding |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Litten's Sign** | <font color="red">Litten's sign</font> is a flickering horizontal depression moving down the lateral chest wall during deep inspiration; its absence indicates diaphragm paralysis or pleural effusion. |
| **Beevor's Sign** | <font color="red">Beevor's sign</font> is the abnormal cephalad (upward) movement of the umbilicus during truncal flexion or straining, indicating that upper abdominal muscles are stronger than lower ones, often due to a <font color="red">T10 spinal cord lesion</font>. |
| **Reflex Grading** | In a <font color="red">Neurological PE</font>, hyperreflexia in the lower extremities combined with pathologic reflexes like <font color="red">Babinski</font> or <font color="red">Ankle clonus</font> suggests an Upper Motor Neuron (UMN) lesion above the conus medullaris. |
| **Sacral Sparing** | The <font color="red">Anal wink</font> and <font color="red">perianal sensation</font> are critical to check in spinal cord injuries; the absence of perianal sensation signifies a complete spinal cord injury. |
| **Kinematic Chains** | In an <font color="red">Open Kinematic Chain</font>, the distal limb is free and moves toward the origin; in a <font color="red">Closed Kinematic Chain</font>, the distal end is fixed (e.g., foot on ground), bringing the origin toward the insertion. |

### TARGETED FUNCTIONAL PERFORMANCE TESTS

- <font color="red">Nine-Hole Peg Test</font>: A timed test of fine motor coordination where subjects place and remove 9 tiny dowels in holes.

### COMPARISON OF CONFUSING CLINICAL CONCEPTS

- For <font color="red">FIM vs. Barthel Index</font>, the FIM includes a Cognitive domain (Communication/Social Cognition) and uses a 7-point scale, whereas the Barthel Index focuses primarily on physical ADLs and assigns specific point values (5, 10, 15) per task totaling 100.
- In <font color="red">FIM Levels 6 vs. 5</font>, Score 6 is used if the patient applies an orthosis or assistive device independently; Score 5 is used if a helper must apply the device for the patient.
- In <font color="red">FIM Levels 3 vs. 2</font>, Score 3 (Moderate Assist) implies the patient does at least half (50%+) of the work; Score 2 (Maximal Assist) implies the helper does more than half, and the patient does only 25-49%.
- Regarding <font color="red">ADLs vs. IADLs</font>, grooming and feeding are ADLs as they are self-directed, while "marketing" (grocery shopping) and laundry are IADLs as they involve interacting with the environment.
- Regarding <font color="red">Open vs. Closed Kinematic Chains</font>, a bicep curl is an open chain because the hand moves freely, while a pull-up is a closed chain because the hand is fixed on the bar and the body (insertion/origin) moves toward it.
- In <font color="red">C fibers vs. A-beta fibers</font>, C fibers are slow and unmyelinated carrying dull pain, while A-beta fibers are fast and heavily myelinated carrying touch/warmth; this is the basis for using heat packs for pain relief.
- Regarding <font color="red">Origin/Insertion vs. Proximal/Distal Attachment</font>, Rehabilitation Medicine prefers "proximal/distal attachment" over the classical anatomical terms "origin/insertion."
- For <font color="red">Beevor's Sign (T10)</font>, a positive sign is the umbilicus moving UP (towards the head), while a negative sign is no movement; however, if all abdominal muscles are paralyzed, it may appear as a "false negative."
- Regarding <font color="red">Litten's Sign vs. Diaphragm Paralysis</font>, a healthy patient shows a depression moving down the lateral chest on inspiration; if the diaphragm is paralyzed, this flickering movement disappears.
- In <font color="red">Complete vs. Incomplete SCI</font>, the defining factor is often the presence of sacral sparing, specifically perianal sensation (S4-S5); if sensation is absent, the injury is generally classified as complete.
- For <font color="red">FIM Scoring Level 7 vs. 6</font>, Level 7 is "Normal" speed and safety without tools; Level 6 is independent but involves a "Modified" factor such as an assistive device, extra time, or a safety risk.
- Regarding <font color="red">Pott’s Disease vs. Metastatic Cancer</font>, both can cause spinal cord compression and back pain, but Pott's is associated with TB exposure (e.g., family history), while cancer is suggested by rapid weight loss and history of primary tumors (e.g., Prostatic Cancer/BPH).
- For <font color="red">Hyperreflexia vs. Hyporeflexia</font>, hyperreflexia in the lower extremities indicates a lesion in the spinal cord (UMN), whereas hyporeflexia generally indicates a lower motor neuron (LMN) lesion or nerve root injury.

### **Synovial Joint Structure and Function**

* <font color="#FF0000">Synovial fluid</font> functions to provide nutrition and lubrication for the articular cartilage.
* The <font color="#FF0000">Knee Cruciate Ligaments (ACL/PCL)</font> are an exception to extra-articular tissue rules as they are considered intra-articular.
* <font color="#FF0000">Synovial Joint Components</font> include bone/subchondral bone, intra-articular tissues (hyaline cartilage, capsule, synovial membrane/cavity), and extra-articular tissues (ligaments, muscles, tendons, meniscus, bursa, labrum, fat pads, plica).
* The primary <font color="#FF0000">Functions of Synovial Joints</font> are to provide motion (mobility) and maintain stability.
* <font color="#FF0000">Synovial Joint Structure</font> is affected by factors including aging, immobilization, trauma, disease, habit, and psychological status.

### **Joint Kinematics: Osteo- vs. Arthrokinematics**

* <font color="#FF0000">Osteokinematic Movements</font> are physiological movements occurring between two bony segments.
* <font color="#FF0000">Swing</font> is an osteokinematic rotary motion about a fixed axis at the proximal segment (e.g., knee flexion).
* <font color="#FF0000">Spin</font> is an osteokinematic axial rotation about a longitudinal axis of the distal segment (e.g., forearm pronation).
* <font color="#FF0000">Arthrokinematic Movements</font> are accessory movements (joint play) occurring between two articular surfaces.
* <font color="#FF0000">Distraction (Traction)</font> is an arthrokinematic separation of two articular surfaces along the longitudinal axis of the distal segment.
* <font color="#FF0000">Compression</font> is an arthrokinematic "meeting together" of two articular surfaces along the longitudinal axis of the distal segment.
* <font color="#FF0000">Glide</font> is an arthrokinematic combination of roll and slide between two incongruent joint surfaces.
* <font color="#FF0000">Roll</font> occurs when new points on one rotating articular surface meet new points on another articular surface.
* <font color="#FF0000">Slide</font> occurs when the same point on one rotating articular surface meets new points on another articular surface.

### **Concave-Convex Rule**

* In <font color="#FF0000">Concave on Convex movement</font>, the concave articular surface moves in the <b>same direction</b> as the moving bone (physiological movement); e.g., Tibia moving on the Femur during knee flexion involves a posterior glide.
* In <font color="#FF0000">Convex on Concave movement</font>, the convex articular surface moves in the <b>opposite direction</b> of the moving bone; e.g., Humerus moving on the Scapula during shoulder abduction involves an inferior glide.

### **Joint Stability and Kinetic Chains**

* <font color="#FF0000">Collagen fibers</font> provide strength to ligaments; 94% of the ACL is composed of collagen.
* <font color="#FF0000">Elastin fibers</font> provide flexibility to ligaments; 60% of the ligamentum flavum consists of elastin.
* <font color="#FF0000">Rectin fibers</font> provide mass to the ligamentous structure.
* <font color="#FF0000">Open Kinetic Chain (OKC)</font> exercises involve a free terminal segment (hand/foot), which isolates muscles and increases range of motion.
* <font color="#FF0000">Closed Kinetic Chain (CKC)</font> exercises involve a fixed terminal segment, promoting stability, co-contraction, and functional movement.
* The <font color="#FF0000">Closed-Packed Position</font> is the state of maximum congruency and greatest stability, where ligaments and capsules are most taut.
* The <font color="#FF0000">Glenohumeral Closed-Packed Position</font> occurs at 90° abduction and full external rotation.
* <font color="#FF0000">Loose-Packed Positions</font> refer to all joint positions other than the closed-packed position.

### **Differential Headings and Comparison Points**

* <font color="#FF0000">Hinge vs. Pivot Joints</font>: Both are uniaxial (1 DOF), but a hinge joint (Ginglymus) allows flexion/extension (e.g., humeroulnar), while a pivot joint (Trochoid) allows rotation (e.g., radioulnar).
* <font color="#FF0000">Condyloid vs. Saddle Joints</font>: Both are biaxial (2 DOF), but Condyloid joints (Ovoid) have an ellipsoidal surface (e.g., wrist), while Saddle joints (Sellar) have a reciprocally concave-convex surface (e.g., thumb CMC).
* <font color="#FF0000">Synarthrosis vs. Amphiarthrosis</font>: Synarthrosis allows little to no movement (e.g., sutures), whereas Amphiarthrosis allow some movement and are typically linked by fibrocartilage (e.g., pubic symphysis).
* <font color="#FF0000">Osteokinematics vs. Arthrokinematics</font>: Osteokinematics describe visible physiological movement of bones (swing/spin), while Arthrokinematics describe invisible accessory movements between joint surfaces (roll/slide/glide).
* <font color="#FF0000">Roll vs. Slide</font>: In a Roll, new points on one surface hit new points on another; in a Slide, a single point on one surface hits multiple new points on the other.
* <font color="#FF0000">Concave Rule vs. Convex Rule</font>: Moving concave surfaces glide in the same direction as the bone; moving convex surfaces glide in the opposite direction of the bone.
* <font color="#FF0000">Open vs. Closed Kinetic Chain</font>: OKC has a free distal end and is non-weight bearing/isolating; CKC has a fixed distal end and is weight-bearing/functional.
* <font color="#FF0000">Closed-Packed vs. Loose-Packed Position</font>: Closed-packed is the position of maximum stability and congruency; Loose-packed (open-packed) refers to any other position where the capsule is lax.
* <font color="#FF0000">Collagen vs. Elastin</font>: Collagen provides tensile strength (dominant in ACL); Elastin provides flexibility (dominant in ligamentum flavum).
* <font color="#FF0000">Swing vs. Spin</font>: Swing is rotation around a fixed axis at the distal segment's end; Spin is rotation of a segment around its own longitudinal axis.
* <font color="#FF0000">Tennis Elbow vs. De Quervain’s</font>: Tennis elbow involves the wrist extensors (ECRB) at the lateral epicondyle; De Quervain’s involves the thumb abductor (APL) and extensor (EPB) at the radial styloid.
* <font color="#FF0000">Distraction vs. Compression</font>: Distraction pulls joint surfaces apart (traction), while compression pushes them together.
* <font color="#FF0000">Stationary vs. Moving Arm</font>: The stationary arm of the goniometer stays with the proximal segment, while the moving arm follows the distal segment being moved.

*   For <font color="red">Manual Muscle Testing (MMT)</font>, the examination is scored across four 25-point parameters: Proper Positioning of Patient, Proper Positioning of Examiner, Maneuver, and Dexterity (Total = 100 points).
*   During <font color="red">Goniometry Placement</font>, the fulcrum must be over the joint axis, the stationary arm aligned with the proximal bone, and the moving arm aligned with the distal bone.
*   In <font color="red">MMT Shoulder Flexion</font>, the prime movers are the Anterior Deltoid and Coracobrachialis; resistance is applied to the distal anteromedial surface of the humerus.
*   For <font color="red">Shoulder Flexion Gravity Reduced</font>, the patient is positioned in side-lying with the tested arm supported, moving the hand toward the head.
*   In <font color="red">MMT Shoulder Extension</font>, prime movers include the Latissimus Dorsi, Posterior Deltoid, and Teres Major; resistance is applied over the posterior surface of the distal humerus.
*   To test <font color="red">Shoulder Abduction MMT</font>, the patient sits with the arm slightly externally rotated (thumb up) to 90 degrees.
*   The <font color="red">Shoulder Abduction Gravity Reduced</font> position is supine with the arm at the side and the palm facing the ceiling.
*   For <font color="red">Shoulder Adduction MMT (Pectoralis Major)</font>, the student must test specific fibers: Clavicular head (start at 60° abduction) and Sternal head (start at 120° abduction).
*   During <font color="red">Shoulder Internal Rotation MMT</font>, a folded towel should be placed under the humerus in the prone position to maintain a horizontal alignment.
*   In <font color="red">Shoulder Internal Rotation MMT</font>, resistance is applied just proximal to the wrist in the direction of external rotation.
*   In <font color="red">Shoulder External Rotation MMT</font>, the prime movers are the Infraspinatus and Teres Minor; resistance is applied toward internal rotation.
*   If a patient fails to move in the gravity-removed position, the <font color="red">Next MMT Step</font> is to palpate the muscle for contractions to distinguish between Grade 1 and Grade 0.
*   To test <font color="red">Scapula Elevation MMT</font>, the patient is seated with the head turned away from the side being tested.

**COMPARING SIMILAR ENTITIES FOR EXAM DIFFERENTIATION**

*   <font color="red">MMT Grade 2 vs. MMT Grade 3</font>: Grade 2 requires full ROM in a gravity-eliminated (horizontal) plane, whereas Grade 3 requires full ROM against gravity but with zero added resistance.
*   <font color="red">MMT Grade 4 vs. MMT Grade 5</font>: Both involve full ROM against gravity and resistance, but Grade 4 is "moderate resistance" while Grade 5 is "maximal resistance."
*   <font color="red">Shoulder Flexion vs. Extension (Goniometry Fulcrum)</font>: Both use the lateral aspect of the greater tubercle as the fulcrum.
*   <font color="red">Shoulder Abduction vs. Adduction (Goniometry Fulcrum)</font>: Both use the anterior acromion as the fulcrum.
*   <font color="red">Shoulder Rotation (Internal vs. External) Goniometry</font>: Both use the olecranon process as the fulcrum and have the moving arm aligned with the ulna and ulnar styloid.
*   <font color="red">Forearm Supination vs. Pronation (Goniometry Fulcrum)</font>: Supination uses the LATERAL side of the ulnar styloid; Pronation uses the MEDIAL side of the ulnar styloid.
*   <font color="red">Wrist Flexion/Extension vs. Deviations (Goniometry Fulcrum)</font>: Flexion and extension use the lateral triquetrum; Radial and ulnar deviation use the dorsal capitate.
*   <font color="red">Hip Flexion vs. Hip Extension (Goniometry Fulcrum)</font>: Both use the greater trochanter as the fulcrum.
*   <font color="red">Hip Abduction vs. Hip Adduction (Goniometry Fulcrum)</font>: Both use the ASIS on the side being tested.
*   <font color="red">Hip Internal vs. External Rotation (Normal ROM)</font>: Both internal and external rotation of the hip have a normal range of 0–45°.
*   <font color="red">Shoulder Internal vs. External Rotation (Normal ROM)</font>: Shoulder Internal Rotation is 0–70°, while External Rotation is 0–90°.
*   <font color="red">Wrist Flexion vs. Extension (Normal ROM)</font>: Wrist Flexion (0–80°) generally has 10 degrees more range than Wrist Extension (0–70°).
*   <font color="red">Ankle Dorsiflexion vs. Plantarflexion (Normal ROM)</font>: Dorsiflexion is only 0–20°, while Plantarflexion is significantly more at 0–50°.
*   <font color="red">Ankle Inversion vs. Eversion (Normal ROM)</font>: Inversion (0–35°) has more than double the range of Eversion (0–15°).
*   <font color="red">Radial vs. Ulnar Deviation (Normal ROM)</font>: Radial deviation is 0–20°, while Ulnar deviation is slightly larger at 0–30°.
*   <font color="red">Wrist Flexion MMT (FCU vs. FCR Bias)</font>: To bias Flexor Carpi Ulnaris, lead with the pinky; to bias Flexor Carpi Radialis, lead with the thumb.
*   <font color="red">Knee Flexion MMT (Lateral vs. Medial Hamstrings)</font>: To bias lateral muscles (Biceps Femoris), externally rotate the tibia; to bias medial muscles (Semis), internally rotate the tibia.
*   <font color="red">Goniometry Stationary vs. Moving Arm</font>: The stationary arm always aligns with the stable, proximal bone; the moving arm always follows the distal, moving bone.
*   <font color="red">Goniometry Pronation vs. Supination (Arm Alignment)</font>: Pronation measures across the ventral (palm) forearm; Supination measures across the dorsal (back) forearm.
*   <font color="red">Hip Flexion vs. Knee Flexion (Normal ROM)</font>: Knee flexion (135°) allows for more range than hip flexion (120°).

### <b><font color="red">IV. Critical Grade Definitions</font></b>
If the instructor asks for the meaning of your assigned grade:

text
Grade 5 (Normal): Full ROM against gravity; Maximal resistance.
Grade 4 (Good):   Full ROM against gravity; Moderate resistance.
Grade 3 (Fair):   Full ROM against gravity; NO resistance.
Grade 2 (Poor):   Full ROM; Gravity-eliminated plane.
Grade 1 (Trace):  Palpable contraction; No joint movement.
Grade 0 (Zero):   No contraction; No movement.


### <b><font color="red">V. Exam Day Pro-Tips</font></b>
1. <b><font color="red">Stabilization:</font></b> Always stabilize the proximal bone to prevent "compensation" (e.g., don't let the patient lean their trunk during elbow flexion).
2. <b><font color="red">Instructions:</font></b> Always give clear commands: *"Hold, don't let me move you."*
3. <b><font color="red">Gravity Reduced:</font></b> If the patient cannot perform the movement against gravity, immediately transition to the side-lying or supported position to test for Grade 2.
4. <b><font color="red">Palpation:</font></b> If there is no movement in the gravity-reduced position, palpate the muscle belly to distinguish between Grade 1 and Grade 0.
