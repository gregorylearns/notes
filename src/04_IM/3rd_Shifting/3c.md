### <b><i style="color:red;">I. Major Nephrology Syndromes and Clinical Database</i></b>

| Feature | AKI | CKD | Nephrotic Syndrome | Nephritic Syndrome |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Primary Finding** | Rapid rise in SCr/Urea | GFR <60 for >3 months | Proteinuria >3.5g/d | Hematuria & HTN |
| **Urinary Sediment** | Muddy brown (ATN) or Bland | Broad waxy casts | Fatty casts/Lipiduria | Dysmorphic RBCs/RBC casts |
| **Common Symptoms** | Oliguria, Uremia | Uremia, Anemia, Bone disease | Massive Edema | Hematuria, HTN, Edema |

- The **<font color="red">10 Nephrology Syndromes</font>** include Acute Nephritic, Nephrotic, Isolated Urinary Abnormalities, AKI, CKD, UTI, Renal Tubular Defects, HTN, Obstruction, and Urolithiasis. [Clinical Database]
- The **<font color="red">Acute Nephritic Syndrome</font>** is characterized by glomerular inflammation leading to hematuria, dysmorphic RBCs, HTN, and reduced GFR. [Nephritic Syndrome]
- The **<font color="red">Nephrotic Syndrome</font>** is defined by massive proteinuria (>3.5 g/day), hypoalbuminemia, edema, and hyperlipidemia. [Nephrotic Syndrome]
- **<font color="red">Functional vs. Structural AKI</font>** is distinguished by prerenal states (decreased perfusion) vs. intrinsic renal disease (structural damage). [AKI Classification]

### <b><i style="color:red;">II. Acute Kidney Injury (AKI): Etiology and Pathophysiology</i></b>

| AKI Category | Mechanism | FENa | Sediment |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Prerenal** | Hypoperfusion (No damage) | <1% | Bland/Normal |
| **Intrinsic** | Structural Damage (ATN) | >1% (often >2%) | Muddy Brown Casts |
| **Postrenal** | Outflow Obstruction | Variable | Bland (unless infected) |

- **<font color="red">Acute Kidney Injury (AKI)</font>** is defined as an impairment of filtration and excretion occurring within 7 days, staged by SCr rise and oliguria duration. [Definition]
- **<font color="red">Prerenal AKI</font>** is caused by reduced renal perfusion; common triggers include Heart Failure (low CO), Hepatic Failure (excess Nitric Oxide causing vasodilation), and Burn injuries (capillary leak). [Prerenal States]
- **<font color="red">Dehydration vs. Hypovolemia</font>** in AKI: Dehydration is a total body water deficit, whereas Hypovolemia is a decrease in intravascular volume that directly reduces renal perfusion. [Prerenal States]
- **<font color="red">Intrinsic AKI</font>** involves direct tissue damage, most commonly **<font color="red">Acute Tubular Necrosis (ATN)</font>**, with the **<font color="red">S3 segment of the proximal tubule</font>** being the most vulnerable site. [Intrinsic Renal Disease]
- **<font color="red">Fractional Excretion of Sodium (FENa)</font>** is typically **<font color="red"><1%</font>** in prerenal states (conserving Na) and **<font color="red">>1%</font>** in intrinsic AKI (tubular inability to reabsorb Na). [FENa]
- **<font color="red">Postrenal AKI</font>** results from urinary tract obstruction such as BPH, urolithiasis, or malignancy. [Postrenal States]
- **<font color="red">NSAIDs</font>** cause AKI by inhibiting prostaglandins, leading to **<font color="red">afferent arteriolar vasoconstriction</font>** and reduced GFR. [Pathophysiology]
- **<font color="red">ACEi and ARBs</font>** cause AKI by inhibiting Angiotensin II, leading to **<font color="red">efferent arteriolar vasodilation</font>** and reduced glomerular pressure. [Pathophysiology]
- **<font color="red">Endothelin-1 (ET-1)</font>** is noted as the most potent vasoconstrictor in the context of renal regulation. [Pathophysiology]

### <b><i style="color:red;">III. Glomerular Diseases and Histopathology</i></b>

| Disease | Key Association | Clinical/Biopsy Clue |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Minimal Change (MCD)** | Children, NSAIDs, Hodgkin | Normal light microscopy |
| **FSGS** | HIV, Heroin, Obesity | Segmental scarring; "Collapsing" in HIV |
| **Membranous (MN)** | Hepatitis B, Solid tumors | Subepithelial spikes; elderly screening |
| **MPGN** | Hepatitis C, Cryoglobulinemia | "Tram-track" double contour |
| **IgA Nephropathy** | URI, SEA populations | Hematuria within days of infection |
| **RPGN** | Vasculitis, Anti-GBM | **Crescents** on biopsy |

- **<font color="red">IgA Nephropathy</font>** is the most common glomerulonephritis worldwide and in Southeast Asia, typically following a URI. [Glomerular Disease]
- **<font color="red">Rapidly Progressive Glomerulonephritis (RPGN)</font>** is a clinical syndrome characterized by a rapid decline in GFR and the presence of **<font color="red">crescents</font>** on renal biopsy. [Glomerular Disease]
- **<font color="red">Alport’s Syndrome</font>** is a hereditary disease presenting with the triad of hematuria, neurosensory hearing loss, and ocular abnormalities. [Hereditary Disease]
- **<font color="red">Diabetes Mellitus</font>** is the most common cause of nephrotic-range proteinuria and the leading cause of CKD in adults. [Diagnostic Evaluation]
- **<font color="red">Multiple Myeloma</font>** in older patients with renal failure and proteinuria is suggested by the presence of Bence-Jones proteins on **<font color="red">UPEP/SPEP</font>**. [Diagnostic Evaluation]

### <b><i style="color:red;">IV. Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD): Staging and Pathophysiology</i></b>

| Stage | GFR (mL/min/1.73m²) | Description |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1** | >90 | Normal or high with kidney damage |
| **2** | 60-89 | Mildly decreased |
| **3a** | 45-59 | Mildly to moderately decreased |
| **3b** | 30-44 | Moderately to severely decreased |
| **4** | 15-29 | Severely decreased |
| **5** | <15 | End-stage renal disease (ESRD) |

- **<font color="red">Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)</font>** is defined by abnormal kidney function or progressive GFR decline for **<font color="red">>3 months</font>**. [Definition]
- The **<font color="red">Normal decline in GFR</font>** with age after the 3rd decade is approximately **<font color="red">1 mL/min per year</font>**. [Staging]
- **<font color="red">Hyperfiltration and Hypertrophy</font>** are nonspecific mechanisms where remaining nephrons compensate for lost mass, eventually leading to overwork and failure. [Pathophysiology]
- **<font color="red">Spot Protein-to-Creatinine Ratio (UACR)</font>** is more practical than 24-hr collection and is preferred for monitoring glomerular injury and therapy response. [Proteinuria]
- **<font color="red">ACEi and ARBs</font>** are mainstay drugs in CKD for controlling intraglomerular HTN and proteinuria, but are contraindicated in AKI. [Management]

### <b><i style="color:red;">V. Electrolyte, Mineral, and Hematologic Complications</i></b>

- **<font color="red">Hyperkalemia in Diabetes/Obstruction</font>** is often caused by **<font color="red">hyporeninemic hypoaldosteronism</font>**, appearing out of proportion to GFR decline. [Electrolytes]
- **<font color="red">Hyperkalemia Emergency Management</font>** includes: 
    1) **<font color="red">Calcium gluconate</font>** (stabilizes myocardium); 
    2) **<font color="red">Insulin + Glucose</font>** (shifts K into cells); 
    3) **<font color="red">Inhaled Beta-agonists</font>** (shifts K into cells). [Management]
- **<font color="red">Metabolic Acidosis in CKD</font>** is typically due to decreased **<font color="red">Ammonia (NH3) production</font>** by diseased kidneys. [Acid-Base]
- **<font color="red">Anemia in CKD</font>** is usually normocytic and normochromic, primarily caused by **<font color="red">insufficient Erythropoietin (EPO)</font>** production; target Hb is **<font color="red">10-11.5 g/dL</font>**. [Anemia]
- **<font color="red">Secondary Hyperparathyroidism</font>** in CKD is driven by phosphate retention, decreased calcitriol, and hypocalcemia. [Bone Disease]
- **<font color="red">Osteitis Fibrosa Cystica</font>** results from high bone turnover due to hyperparathyroidism; advanced stages may show hemorrhagic cysts known as **<font color="red">Brown Tumors</font>**. [Bone Disease]
- **<font color="red">Calciphylaxis</font>** is a condition "almost exclusive" to advanced CKD, characterized by painful livedo reticularis and ischemic skin necrosis. [Bone Disease]
- **<font color="red">Fibroblast Growth Factor-23 (FGF-23)</font>** is a phosphaturic hormone that acts as an independent risk factor for LVH and mortality in CKD. [Mineral Metabolism]
- **<font color="red">Nephrogenic Systemic Fibrosis (NSF)</font>** is a skin condition unique to CKD patients exposed to **<font color="red">Gadolinium</font>**-based MRI contrast. [Contrast Injury]

### <b><i style="color:red;">VI. Clinical Management and Dialysis</i></b>

- **<font color="red">Renal Replacement Therapy (RRT)</font>** indications in AKI follow the "AEIOU" mnemonic: Refractory **<font color="red">A</font>**cidosis, **<font color="red">E</font>**lectrolyte issues (K+), **<font color="red">I</font>**ntoxications (Lithium, alcohols), Fluid **<font color="red">O</font>**verload, and **<font color="red">U</font>**remic symptoms. [RRT Indications]
- **<font color="red">Uremic Pericarditis</font>** is an absolute indication for urgent dialysis initiation or intensification of dialysis prescription. [Management]
- **<font color="red">Hypotension</font>** is the most common acute complication of hemodialysis, especially in diabetic patients. [Dialysis]
- **<font color="red">Type A Dialyzer Reaction</font>** is an IgE-mediated hypersensitivity to ethylene oxide (occurs within minutes); **<font color="red">Type B Reaction</font>** is complement/cytokine-mediated (nonspecific chest/back pain). [Dialysis]
- **<font color="red">CAPD Peritonitis</font>** is most commonly caused by **<font color="red">Coagulase-negative Staphylococcus</font>** (skin flora). [Peritoneal Dialysis]
- **<font color="red">Arteriovenous (AV) Fistulas</font>** can lead to **<font color="red">High-output Heart Failure</font>** due to blood shunting. [Complications]

### <b><i style="color:red;">VII. High-Yield Distinctions and Comparisons</i></b>

- **<font color="red">Prerenal vs. Intrinsic AKI</font>**: Prerenal has FENa <1% and high urine osmolarity; Intrinsic (ATN) has FENa >1% and "muddy brown" casts.
- **<font color="red">Nephrotic vs. Nephritic Proteinuria</font>**: Nephrotic is massive (>3.5g/d) with no active sediment; Nephritic is often <3.5g/d and accompanied by dysmorphic RBCs/casts.
- **<font color="red">Afferent vs. Efferent Arteriolar Drugs</font>**: NSAIDs vasoconstrict the afferent arteriole (bad in AKI); ACEi/ARBs vasodilate the efferent arteriole (bad in AKI, good in chronic proteinuria).
- **<font color="red">Hepatitis B vs. Hepatitis C Renal Association</font>**: Hepatitis B is linked to Membranous Nephropathy (MN); Hepatitis C is linked to Membranoproliferative GN (MPGN).
- **<font color="red">Calcium Gluconate vs. Insulin in Hyperkalemia</font>**: Calcium gluconate protects the heart but does not lower K+; Insulin/Glucose actually lowers serum K+.
- **<font color="red">Dehydration vs. Hypovolemia</font>**: Dehydration is loss of total body water; Hypovolemia is loss of intravascular volume and is more likely to cause AKI.
- **<font color="red">Type A vs. Type B Dialyzer Reactions</font>**: Type A is anaphylactic (IgE/Ethylene oxide); Type B is mild chest/back pain (Complement/Cytokines).
- **<font color="red">Uremic vs. Spontaneous Tumor Lysis</font>**: Both cause AKI; Spontaneous TLS occurs due to overwhelming tumor burden, whereas standard TLS follows chemotherapy.
- **<font color="red">Thiazide vs. Loop Diuretics in CKD</font>**: Thiazides lose efficacy as GFR drops below 30 (Stage 4-5); Loop diuretics are the preferred choice, though higher doses are required.
- **<font color="red">24-hour Protein vs. Spot UACR</font>**: 24-hour is gold standard but cumbersome; Spot Morning UACR is preferred for practicality and correlates well.
- **<font color="red">Target Hb in General Population vs. CKD</font>**: Normal Hb is >12-13; CKD Hb target is restricted to 10-11.5 g/dL to avoid cardiovascular/clotting risks.
- **<font color="red">Osteitis Fibrosa Cystica vs. Adynamic Bone Disease</font>**: Osteitis fibrosa cystica is "high turnover" (high PTH); Adynamic bone disease is "low turnover" (very low PTH).
- **<font color="red">Contrast Nephropathy vs. NSF</font>**: Contrast Nephropathy is AKI from iodinated CT contrast; Nephrogenic Systemic Fibrosis (NSF) is skin induration from MR Gadolinium contrast.
- **<font color="red">AKI (7 days) vs. CKD (3 months)</font>**: AKI describes an acute, potentially reversible drop in function; CKD describes irreversible, structural, or functional decline over at least 90 days.
- **<font color="red">Low BP Prognosis</font>**: In the general population, low BP is often good; in dialysis/ESRD patients, low BP (under 140) often carries a *worse* prognosis/higher mortality.

### <b>GENERAL CLASSIFICATION OVERVIEW</b>

| <b>Imaging</b> | Often normal size or enlarged kidneys. | Increased echogenicity, loss of corticomedullary differentiation, cortical scarring, small/shrunken kidneys. |

***

### <b>COMPREHENSIVE FLASHCARD BULLET POINTS</b>

<b>I. ACUTE INTERSTITIAL NEPHRITIS (AIN) & ETIOLOGIES</b>

- In the context of <font color="red">Acute Interstitial Nephritis (AIN)</font>, the disorder is far more often encountered today as an <b>allergic reaction to a drug</b> rather than a postinfectious complication.
- For <font color="red">Allergic Interstitial Nephritis</font>, therapeutic agents associated with the condition include antibiotics (β-lactams, sulfonamides, quinolones), NSAIDs, diuretics, anticonvulsants, and proton pump inhibitors (PPIs).
- The <font color="red">Classic Presentation of Allergic AIN</font> involves fever, rash, peripheral eosinophilia, and oliguric AKI occurring 7–10 days after starting <b>methicillin or other β-lactams</b>, though this is the exception, not the rule.
- In <font color="red">NSAID-induced AIN</font>, fever, rash, and eosinophilia are <b>rare</b>, but acute kidney injury with <b>heavy (nephrotic-range) proteinuria</b> is common.
- A <b>particularly severe and rapid-onset</b> <font color="red">Acute Interstitial Nephritis (AIN)</font> may occur specifically upon the <b>reintroduction of rifampin</b> after a drug-free period.
- For <font color="red">Diagnosis of AIN</font>, peripheral blood <b>eosinophilia</b> is a supportive finding but is present in only a minority of patients.
- Within the <font color="red">Urinalysis of AIN</font>, findings typically reveal pyuria with <b>white blood cell casts</b> and hematuria; however, <b>urinary eosinophils</b> are neither sensitive nor specific and are not recommended for testing.
- The <b>primary treatment</b> for <font color="red">Allergic Interstitial Nephritis</font> is the <b>discontinuation of the offending agent</b>, which often leads to reversal of the injury.
- In <font color="red">Glucocorticoid Therapy for AIN</font>, use is reserved for severe kidney injury where dialysis is imminent or if kidney function fails to improve after stopping the drug; <b>delaying steroids</b> once dialysis is indicated leads to worse outcomes.

<b>II. AUTOIMMUNE & SYSTEMIC TUBULOINTERSTITIAL DISORDERS</b>

- The <font color="red">Most Common Renal Manifestation of Sjögren's Syndrome</font> is tubulointerstitial nephritis with a <b>predominant lymphocytic infiltrate</b>, which may cause distal RTA or nephrogenic diabetes insipidus.
- The <b>hallmark feature</b> of <font color="red">Tubulointerstitial Nephritis with Uveitis (TINU)</font> is a <b>painful anterior uveitis</b> (bilateral, blurred vision, photophobia) accompanying a lymphocyte-predominant AIN.
- <font color="red">Tubulointerstitial Nephritis with Uveitis (TINU)</font> occurs in <b>females three times more often than males</b>, has a median age of 15, and features <b>sterile pyuria</b> and Fanconi's syndrome features.
- In <font color="red">Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)</font>, tubulointerstitial inflammation usually accompanies class III or IV lupus nephritis, but may occasionally manifest alone with <b>azotemia and Type IV RTA</b>.
- <font color="red">Granulomatous Interstitial Nephritis</font> biopsy reveals a chronic inflammatory infiltrate with granulomas and giant cells; it is often idiopathic or associated with <b>sarcoidosis or tuberculosis</b>.
- <font color="red">IgG4-Related Systemic Disease</font> presents as AIN with a dense infiltrate of <b>IgG4-expressing plasma cells</b> and may involve "pseudotumors," autoimmune pancreatitis, or retroperitoneal fibrosis.
- In <font color="red">AIN associated with Immune Checkpoint Inhibitors</font>, kidney impact occurs in 2-5% of cases, usually within 15 weeks of therapy; treatment involves <b>corticosteroids and stopping inciting drugs</b> like PPIs or NSAIDs.
- <font color="red">Infection-Associated AIN</font> is most commonly seen in immunocompromised patients (e.g., kidney transplant recipients) due to <b>reactivation of Polyomavirus BK</b>.
- A significant <b>increase in Acute TIN or TINU</b> has been noted in <font color="red">Children following SARS-CoV-2 infection</font>.

<b>III. ACUTE OBSTRUCTIVE DISORDERS & CRYSTAL NEPHROPATHIES</b>

- <font color="red">Acute Urate Nephropathy</font> causes oliguric AKI due to <b>intratubular obstruction</b> by uric acid crystals, typically following <b>Tumor Lysis Syndrome</b> in lympho- or myeloproliferative disorders.
- The <b>treatment for Acute Urate Nephropathy</b> once oliguria develops usually requires <b>emergent hemodialysis or Rasburicase</b> (recombinant urate oxidase) to lower uric acid levels.
- <font color="red">Acute Phosphate Nephropathy</font> is a serious complication of <b>oral Phosphosoda</b> used for colonoscopy preparation, resulting in calcium phosphate crystal deposition in those with hypovolemia.
- <font color="red">Light Chain Cast Nephropathy (Myeloma Kidney)</font> occurs when filtered monoclonal <b>Bence-Jones proteins</b> form aggregates with <b>Tamm-Horsfall protein</b> in the distal tubule, causing obstruction and a giant cell reaction.
- A <b>clinical clue</b> for <font color="red">Light Chain Cast Nephropathy</font> is a <b>negative urinary dipstick</b> (which detects albumin) but <b>high total protein</b> on a spot urine specimen (due to light chains).
- In <font color="red">Crystal-induced AKI from Drugs</font>, medications like <b>Acyclovir (needle-shaped crystals)</b>, <b>Indinavir</b>, or <b>Sulfadiazine</b> precipitate in tubules during hypovolemia; this is reversible with saline repletion.

<b>IV. CHRONIC TUBULOINTERSTITIAL DISEASES (CIN)</b>

- <font color="red">Reflux Nephropathy</font> (formerly "Chronic Pyelonephritis") is the consequence of <b>vesicoureteral reflux (VUR)</b> in early childhood, leading to patchy interstitial scarring and <b>secondary FSGS</b>.
- The <b>classic imaging finding</b> for <font color="red">Reflux Nephropathy</font> on ultrasound is <b>small, asymmetric kidneys with irregular outlines and thinned cortices</b> over clubbed calyces.
- In <font color="red">Sickle Cell Nephropathy</font>, the <b>most common early signs</b> are polyuria (impaired concentrating ability) and Type IV RTA; <b>papillary necrosis</b> may present as gross hematuria.
- <font color="red">Analgesic Nephropathy</font> results from long-term use of compound preparations (phenacetin, aspirin, caffeine) and is characterized by <b>papillary necrosis and small, scarred kidneys with calcifications</b>.
- <font color="red">Aristolochic Acid Nephropathy (AAN)</font>, which includes "Chinese Herbal Nephropathy" and "Balkan Endemic Nephropathy," is a CIN associated with a <b>very high incidence of upper urinary tract urothelial cancers</b>.
- <font color="red">Lithium-Associated Nephropathy</font> most commonly presents as <b>Nephrogenic Diabetes Insipidus</b> (polyuria/polydipsia) due to ENaC-mediated entry into collecting duct cells and downregulation of aquaporin.
- On <font color="red">Lithium-Associated Nephropathy Biopsy</font>, a highly characteristic finding is <b>small cysts or dilation of the distal tubule and collecting duct</b>.
- <font color="red">Calcineurin Inhibitor (CNI) Nephrotoxicity</font> (Cyclosporine/Tacrolimus) shows a <b>"striped" pattern</b> of patchy interstitial fibrosis and tubular hyalinosis on biopsy.
- The <font color="red">Triad of "Saturnine Gout"</font> consists of <b>hypertension, hyperuricemia (due to lead-induced urate secretion failure), and impaired kidney function</b> associated with <b>Lead Nephropathy</b>.
- In <font color="red">Hypercalcemic Nephropathy</font>, the <b>earliest lesion</b> is a focal degeneration in the <b>collecting ducts</b>, and the <b>most striking clinical defect</b> is an inability to maximally concentrate urine.
- <font color="red">Hypokalemic Nephropathy</font> (from chronic laxative abuse or vomiting) is characterized histologically by <b>vacuolar degeneration</b> of proximal and distal tubular cells.

### <b>COMPARISON POINTS FOR EXAM DIFFERENTIATION</b>

1. <font color="red">Beta-Lactam AIN vs. NSAID-induced AIN</font>: Beta-lactam AIN typically presents with the <b>systemic triad (fever, rash, eosinophilia)</b> and mild proteinuria, while NSAID-induced AIN usually <b>lacks systemic symptoms</b> but presents with <b>nephrotic-range proteinuria</b>.
2. <font color="red">Acute TIN vs. Chronic TIN (Urinalysis)</font>: <font color="red">Acute TIN</font> features an <b>active sediment</b> (leukocytes and WBC casts), whereas <font color="red">Chronic TIN</font> usually presents with a <b>bland sediment</b> and features of tubular dysfunction (glycosuria, bicarbonaturia).
3. <font color="red">Type II RTA vs. Type IV RTA</font>: <font color="red">Type II (Proximal) RTA</font> is part of Fanconi's syndrome (bicarbonaturia); <font color="red">Type IV RTA</font> features <b>hyperkalemia</b> and impaired ammoniagenesis and is common in SLE or Sickle Cell.
4. <font color="red">Light Chain Cast Nephropathy vs. Glomerular Amyloidosis</font>: <font color="red">Myeloma Kidney (LCCN)</font> presents with a <b>negative dipstick</b> but positive sulfosalicylic acid test for protein; Amyloidosis (glomerular) would be <b>dipstick-positive</b> for high albumin.
5. <font color="red">Acute Urate Nephropathy vs. Gouty Nephropathy (CIN)</font>: <font color="red">Acute Urate Nephropathy</font> is a sudden <b>tubular obstruction</b> (Tumor Lysis); <font color="red">Gouty Nephropathy</font> is a <b>chronic medullary fibrosis</b> due to monosodium urate deposits over years.
6. <font color="red">TINU vs. Sjögren's Syndrome</font>: <font color="red">TINU</font> is classically seen in <b>adolescents</b> with painful uveitis; <font color="red">Sjögren's</font> is seen in <b>middle-aged women</b> with sicca symptoms (dry eyes/mouth) and anti-Ro/La antibodies.
7. <font color="red">Reflux Nephropathy Imaging vs. Analgesic Nephropathy Imaging</font>: <font color="red">Reflux Nephropathy</font> shows <b>asymmetric scarring</b> at the poles over <b>clubbed calyces</b>; <font color="red">Analgesic Nephropathy</font> shows <b>bilateral small kidneys</b> with <b>papillary calcifications</b> ("ring sign").
8. <font color="red">Lead Nephropathy vs. Other CIN</font>: <font color="red">Lead Nephropathy</font> is uniquely associated with <b>"Saturnine Gout"</b> (hyperuricemia out of proportion to GFR) and exposure to <b>moonshine or batteries</b>.
9. <font color="red">Lithium Nehpropathy vs. CNI Nephrotoxicity</font>: <font color="red">Lithium</font> causes <b>distal tubular/collecting duct cysts</b> and diabetes insipidus; <font color="red">CNIs</font> cause a <b>"striped" fibrosis</b> and arteriolar hyalinosis.
10. <font color="red">Urine Bence-Jones Protein vs. Albumin</font>: <b>Standard dipsticks</b> ONLY detect <b>Albumin</b>; they will miss the light chains found in <font color="red">Multiple Myeloma</font>.
11. <font color="red">Hypercalcemia vs. Hypokalemia (Pathology)</font>: <font color="red">Hypercalcemia</font> causes <b>nephrocalcinosis</b> and collecting duct injury; <font color="red">Hypokalemia</font> causes pathognomonic <b>vacuolar degeneration</b> of proximal tubules.
12. <font color="red">BK Virus vs. Bacterial Pyelonephritis</font>: <font color="red">BK Virus</font> causes <b>Tubulointerstitial Nephritis</b> (AKI) in transplant patients; <b>Acute Bacterial Pyelonephritis</b> rarely causes AKI unless it is bilateral or leads to sepsis.
13. <font color="red">Acyclovir vs. Indinavir Crystals</font>: <font color="red">Acyclovir</font> crystals are <b>red-green birefringent needles</b>; <font color="red">Indinavir</font> crystals are <b>rectangular plates or "fans."</b>
14. <font color="red">Balkan Endemic vs. Chinese Herbal Nephropathy</font>: Both are now <b>Aristolochic Acid Nephropathy (AAN)</b>; the former is from <b>grain contamination</b>, the latter from <b>slimming tea/weight-loss</b> agents.
15. <font color="red">Steroid Use in Allergic AIN vs. Autoimmune AIN</font>: In <font color="red">Allergic AIN</font>, steroids are <b>relative indications</b> (if no recovery after stopping drug); in <font color="red">TINU or Sarcoidosis</font>, steroids are <b>absolute indications</b>.

### <b><i style="color:red;">IX. Confusion Busters: Key High-Yield Distinctions</i></b>

1.  <b><font color="red">Prerenal vs. Intrinsic AKI</font></b>: Prerenal features a FENa <1% (intact tubules conserving Na), while Intrinsic shows FENa >1% (damaged tubules).
2.  <b><font color="red">Nephrotic vs. Nephritic</font></b>: Nephrotic is defined by massive protein loss (>3.5g/d) and edema; Nephritic is defined by RBC casts, hematuria, and HTN.
3.  <b><font color="red">MCD vs. FSGS</font></b>: Minimal Change Disease shows normal light microscopy; FSGS shows segmental scarring. MCD is common in kids; FSGS in HIV/obesity.
4.  <b><font color="red">Hepatitis B vs. Hepatitis C</font></b>: Hep B is associated with Membranous Nephropathy; Hep C is associated with MPGN and Cryoglobulinemia.
5.  <b><font color="red">NSAIDs vs. ACEi/ARBs</font></b>: NSAIDs cause **Afferent** vasoconstriction; ACEi/ARBs cause **Efferent** vasodilation.
6.  <b><font color="red">Dehydration vs. Hypovolemia</font></b>: Dehydration is a lack of pure water (total body); Hypovolemia is a lack of blood/plasma volume (intravascular).
7.  <b><font color="red">Type A vs. Type B Dialyzer Reaction</font></b>: Type A is immediate anaphylaxis (minutes); Type B is mild chest/back pain (later in session).
8.  <b><font color="red">Hyperacute vs. Acute Rejection</font></b>: Hyperacute occurs in minutes (preformed antibodies); Acute occurs in weeks/months (T-cell or Humoral).
9.  <b><font color="red">HLA Class I vs. Class II</font></b>: Class I (A, B, C) involves CD8+ (Cytotoxic) cells; Class II (DR, DQ, DP) involves CD4+ (Helper) cells.
10. <b><font color="red">Osteitis Fibrosa Cystica vs. Adynamic Bone Disease</font></b>: Osteitis is high-turnover (High PTH); Adynamic is low-turnover (Low PTH).
11. <b><font color="red">Calcium Gluconate vs. Insulin</font></b>: Calcium protects the heart (membrane stabilizer); Insulin lowers the actual potassium level (cellular shift).
12. <b><font color="red">Contrast Nephropathy vs. NSF</font></b>: Contrast Nephropathy is from Iodinated contrast; NSF is from Gadolinium in CKD patients.
13. <b><font color="red">HD vs. PD Fluid Removal</font></b>: Hemodialysis uses hydrostatic pressure (ultrafiltration); Peritoneal Dialysis uses osmotic pressure (glucose/starch).
14. <b><font color="red">Blood Flow in HD vs. PD</font></b>: HD requires high flow rates (250-450 mL/min) and a pump; PD is slow, relying on gravity and peritoneal capillary flow.
15. <b><font color="red">Uremic vs. Spontaneous Tumor Lysis</font></b>: AKI in tumor lysis is often triggered by chemotherapy; Spontaneous occurs without chemo due to high tumor burden.
16. <b><font color="red">Thiazide vs. Loop Diuretics in CKD</font></b>: Thiazides lose efficacy as GFR drops; Loop diuretics remain effective and are preferred in late-stage CKD.
17. <b><font color="red">Target Hb – General vs. CKD</font></b>: General target is >12; CKD target is lower (10-11.5) to avoid cardiovascular events.
18. <b><font color="red">Direct vs. Indirect Transplantation Pathway</font></b>: Direct involves recipient T-cells recognizing donor APCs; Indirect involves recipient APCs processing donor MHC into peptides.
19. <b><font color="red">Belatacept vs. CNI</font></b>: Belatacept is a monthly IV infusion (no nephrotoxicity); CNIs are daily orals with risk of chronic nephrotoxicity.
20. <b><font color="red">Low BP Prognosis</font></b>: Below 140/90 is target for many, but in ESRD/Dialysis, very low BP is actually associated with *higher* mortality.

### GLOMERULAR DISEASES: PATHOGENESIS, PROGRESSION, AND DIAGNOSTIC APPROACH

| Topic | Key Features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Glomerular Architecture** | The <font color="red">glomerular capillary tuft</font> represents an arteriolar portal system fed by an afferent arteriole and drained by an efferent arteriole. |
| **Filtration Barrier** | The <font color="red">selective filtration barrier</font> of the glomerulus is composed of fenestrated endothelial cells, the glomerular basement membrane (GBM), and epithelial podocytes interconnected by slit-pore membranes. |
| **Normal Proteinuria** | In a <font color="red">healthy state</font>, humans excrete an average of 8-10 mg of albumin daily, which accounts for 20-60% of total excreted protein. |
| **Progression Marker** | The <font color="red">best histologic correlate</font> for kidney failure in glomerulonephritis is the appearance of tubulointerstitial nephritis and fibrosis rather than the specific type of glomerular injury. |
| **Fibrogenesis** | During <font color="red">renal fibrosis</font>, myofibroblasts are primarily derived from resident fibroblast proliferation (50%) and bone marrow-derived fibrocytes (35%). |
| **Hematuria Origin** | The <font color="red">presence of red blood cell (RBC) casts</font> or dysmorphic RBCs in the urine sediment strongly suggests a diagnosis of glomerulonephritis. |
| **Urine Albumin Levels** | In <font color="red">diabetic nephropathy</font>, microalbuminuria is defined as 30-300 mg/24 h, while frank proteinuria is defined as >300 mg/24 h. |

### ACUTE NEPHRITIC SYNDROMES

| Disease | Pathogenesis | Clinical Manifestations | Diagnosis/Pathology | Treatment |

| Topic | Key Features |
| :--- | :--- |

### DETAILED SYNDROME SPECIFICS (FLASHCARDS)

*   The <font color="red">nephrotic syndrome</font> is clinically defined by proteinuria >3.5 g/24 h, hypoalbuminemia <3 g/dL, edema, hyperlipidemia, and lipiduria.
*   The <font color="red">acute nephritic syndrome</font> features include hematuria, red blood cell casts, hypertension, and mild to moderate proteinuria (<3 g/24 h).
*   In <font color="red">IgA Nephropathy</font>, the "synpharyngitic" presentation refers to gross hematuria occurring simultaneously with or within 1-2 days of a respiratory infection (unlike PSGN which has a latent period).
*   For <font color="red">Lupus Nephritis Class IV</font>, the standard of care to balance efficacy and safety involves induction with high-dose steroids plus either mycophenolate mofetil (MMF) or cyclophosphamide.
*   In <font color="red">Minimal Change Disease</font>, the proteinuria is considered "selective," meaning it consists primarily of albumin rather than higher-molecular-weight proteins.
*   The <font color="red">most common risk factor</font> for FSGS in African Americans is the presence of high-risk APOL1 gene polymorphisms.
*   The <font color="red">PLA2R antibody</font> is highly specific for primary membranous nephropathy and can be used to monitor treatment response and predict relapse.
*   In <font color="red">Alport's Syndrome</font>, the characteristic electron microscopy finding is a "basket-weave" appearance of the basement membrane due to irregular thinning and thickening (splitting).
*   The <font color="red">treatment for TTP</font> involves emergent large-volume plasma exchange (plasmapheresis) to remove antibodies and provide functional ADAMTS13.
*   In <font color="red">Diabetic Nephropathy</font>, retinopathy is present in over 90% of Type 1 DM patients with nephropathy, making its absence a reason to consider alternative diagnoses.
*   For <font color="red">Anti-GBM disease</font>, serum testing must specifically target the α3 NC1 domain of collagen IV to avoid false positives from non-nephritic antibodies.
*   In <font color="red">C3 Glomerulopathy</font>, the "C3 Nephritic Factor" is an autoantibody that stabilizes C3 convertase, leading to continuous complement consumption.

### COMPARISONS FOR EXAM DIFFERENTIATION

*   <font color="red">PSGN vs. IgA Nephropathy</font>: PSGN has a latent period of 1-3 weeks after infection and low C3 levels; IgA Nephropathy is "synpharyngitic" (occurs with the infection) and has normal C3 levels.
*   <font color="red">MCD vs. FSGS</font>: MCD shows normal glomeruli on light microscopy and is highly steroid-responsive; FSGS shows segmental scars and is often steroid-resistant.
*   <font color="red">Linear vs. Granular IF</font>: Linear IgG staining is pathognomonic for <font color="red">Anti-GBM disease</font>; Granular ("lumpy-bumpy") staining indicates immune-complex diseases like <font color="red">Lupus</font>, <font color="red">PSGN</font>, or <font color="red">Membranous GN</font>.
*   <font color="red">GPA vs. MPA</font>: Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (GPA) features granulomatous inflammation and PR3-ANCA; Microscopic Polyangiitis (MPA) lacks granulomas and features MPO-ANCA.
*   <font color="red">Hump vs. Ribbon vs. Spike</font>: Subepithelial "humps" = <font color="red">PSGN</font>; Intramembranous "ribbons" = <font color="red">Dense Deposit Disease</font>; Subepithelial "spikes" = <font color="red">Membranous GN</font>.
*   <font color="red">Selective vs. Non-selective Proteinuria</font>: Selective (Albumin only) is characteristic of <font color="red">Minimal Change Disease</font>; Non-selective (Albumin + Globulins) is seen in most other <font color="red">Nephrotic syndromes</font>.
*   <font color="red">TTP vs. HUS</font>: TTP is defined by ADAMTS13 deficiency and prominent neurologic symptoms; HUS is defined by Shiga-toxin or complement dysregulation and prominent AKI.
*   <font color="red">AL vs. AA Amyloidosis</font>: AL is derived from Ig light chains (plasma cell dyscrasias); AA is derived from Serum Amyloid A (chronic inflammation).
*   <font color="red">Alport vs. Thin Basement Membrane</font>: Alport features split/thickened GBM, deafness, and renal failure; TBMD features diffuse thinning only and a benign course.
*   <font color="red">Primary vs. Secondary FSGS</font>: Primary FSGS presents with sudden, massive nephrotic syndrome; Secondary FSGS (e.g., from obesity or reduced mass) presents with gradual, sub-nephrotic proteinuria and less hypoalbuminemia.
*   <font color="red">C3 vs. C4 Levels</font>: Low C3 with normal C4 suggests alternative pathway activation (e.g., <font color="red">PSGN</font>, <font color="red">DDD</font>); Low C3 and Low C4 suggest classical pathway activation (e.g., <font color="red">Lupus Nephritis</font>).
*   <font color="red">Kidney Size</font>: Small kidneys suggest chronic disease; however, <font color="red">Diabetic Nephropathy</font>, <font color="red">Amyloidosis</font>, and <font color="red">HIVAN</font> often present with normal or enlarged kidneys despite advanced failure.
*   <font color="red">PLA2R vs. THSD7A</font>: PLA2R is the antigen in 70-80% of primary <font color="red">Membranous GN</font>; THSD7A is a rarer antigen (1-5%) sometimes associated with underlying malignancy.
*   <font color="red">Subendothelial vs. Subepithelial Deposits</font>: Subendothelial (and mesangial) deposits usually cause a <font color="red">Nephritic/inflammatory</font> response; Subepithelial deposits (outside the GBM) usually cause a <font color="red">Nephrotic</font> response with podocyte injury.
*   <font color="red">Class IV-S vs. Class IV-G Lupus</font>: Class IV-S (Segmental) traditionally carries a worse prognosis than Class IV-G (Global) in <font color="red">Lupus Nephritis</font>.

### General Concepts and Pathogenesis

- The <font color="red">Polycystic Kidney Diseases (PKD)</font> are a group of genetically heterogeneous disorders and represent a leading cause of kidney failure.
- <font color="red">Autosomal Dominant Polycystic Kidney Disease (ADPKD)</font> is the most common life-threatening monogenic disease, affecting 12 million people worldwide.
- <font color="red">Autosomal Recessive Polycystic Kidney Disease (ARPKD)</font> is rarer than the dominant form and primarily affects the pediatric population.
- <font color="red">Ciliopathies</font> is the collective term for a wide spectrum of diseases, including PKD, that underlie defects in the structure or function of the primary cilia.
- The <font color="red">Primary Cilium</font> is a hair-like structure on the apical membrane of tubular epithelial cells connected to the basal body through the transition zone.
- <font color="red">Polycystin-1 (PC1)</font>, encoded by `PKD1`, is a large 11-transmembrane protein that functions like a G protein–coupled receptor (GPCR).
- <font color="red">Polycystin-2 (PC2)</font>, encoded by `PKD2`, is a calcium-permeable six-transmembrane protein belonging to the TRP cation channel family.
- The <font color="red">PC1/PC2 Protein Complex</font> serves as a mechanosensor or chemical sensor on the primary cilium, regulating calcium and G-protein signaling.
- High levels of <font color="red">cAMP</font> in ADPKD kidneys promote protein kinase A activity, leading to cyst growth via cell proliferation and fluid secretion through chloride and aquaporin channels.
- The <font color="red">"Second Hit" Mutation</font> hypothesis in ADPKD suggests that while every cell carries a germline mutant allele, cysts only develop from cells that receive a somatic mutation in the "normal" allele.

### Autosomal Dominant Polycystic Kidney Disease (ADPKD)

- <font color="red">ADPKD Genetics</font> involve `PKD1` (85% of cases, more severe) on chromosome 16p13 and `PKD2` (15% of cases, milder) on chromosome 4q21-q23. *
- <font color="red">ADPKD Renal Manifestations</font> are characterized by progressive bilateral formation of renal cysts that can increase kidney weight up to 20 times the normal weight.
- <font color="red">ADPKD Pain</font> is a frequent symptom (~60% of patients) and may result from cyst infection, hemorrhage, or nephrolithiasis.
- <font color="red">Gross Hematuria</font> in ADPKD resulting from cyst rupture occurs in ~40% of patients and may coexist with flank pain if the cyst connects to the collecting system.
- <font color="red">Infection</font> is the second most common cause of death for patients with ADPKD, often manifesting as infected cysts or acute pyelonephritis due to gram-negative bacteria.
- <font color="red">Nephrolithiasis</font> in ADPKD occurs in ~20% of patients; notably, more than half of these stones are composed of <font color="red">Uric Acid</font>, unlike the general population.
- <font color="red">Cardiovascular Complications</font> are the major cause of mortality in patients with ADPKD.
- <font color="red">Hypertension</font> in ADPKD is common and typically occurs *before* any reduction in GFR; it is a significant risk factor for both CV disease and kidney progression.
- <font color="red">Polycystic Liver Disease</font> is the most common extrarenal complication of ADPKD, occurring almost exclusively in women, particularly those with multiple pregnancies.
- <font color="red">Intracranial Aneurysm (ICA)</font> occurs four to five times more frequently in ADPKD patients than the general population, with family history being a major risk factor for rupture.
- <font color="red">ADPKD Vascular Abnormalities</font> include mitral valve prolapse (up to 30%), tricuspid valve prolapse, and diffuse arterial dolichoectasias.
- <font color="red">ADPKD Diagnosis Criteria (Ultrasound)</font> for at-risk subjects:
    1. Ages 15–29: At least two renal cysts (unilateral or bilateral).
    2. Ages 30–59: At least two cysts in each kidney.
    3. Ages ≥60: At least four cysts in each kidney. *
- <font color="red">Disease Exclusion</font> in ADPKD for subjects aged 30–59 is defined as the absence of at least two cysts in each kidney (0% false-negative rate).
- <font color="red">MRI (T2-weighted)</font> with gadolinium is more sensitive than ultrasound for ADPKD, capable of detecting cysts only 2–3 mm in diameter.
- <font color="red">ADPKD Blood Pressure Management</font> recommends a target of 140/90 mmHg, though rigorous control to 110 mmHg systolic may slow cyst growth but increase risk of renal blood flow reduction.
- <font color="red">Cyst Infection Treatment</font> in ADPKD requires lipid-soluble antibiotics like trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole or quinolones to penetrate cyst walls, often for 4–6 weeks.
- <font color="red">Tolvaptan</font> is an FDA-approved V2 receptor (V2R) antagonist that inhibits cAMP pathways to slow the decline of renal function in patients at risk of rapidly progressing ADPKD.
- <font color="red">Somatostatin Analogues</font> (e.g., octreotide) reduce renal cAMP levels and have been shown to slow the decline of renal function in ADPKD trials.

### Autosomal Recessive Polycystic Kidney Disease (ARPKD)

- <font color="red">ARPKD Ecology</font> results from mutations in a single gene, `PKHD1`, which encodes <font color="red">Fibrocystin/Polyductin (FPC)</font>.
- <font color="red">Neonatal ARPKD</font> often presents with greatly enlarged echogenic kidneys and reduced fetal urine production, leading to <font color="red">Oligohydramnios</font> and pulmonary hypoplasia. *
- <font color="red">ARPKD Mortality</font> is high, with about 30% of neonates dying shortly after birth due to respiratory insufficiency; 60% of total mortality occurs within the first month.
- <font color="red">Caroli Disease</font> is a hallmark of ARPKD liver disease and involves the dilatation of intrahepatic bile ducts. *
- <font color="red">Congenital Hepatic Fibrosis (CHF)</font> in ARPKD can lead to portal hypertension, hepatosplenomegaly, and variceal bleeding.
- <font color="red">ARPKD Diagnosis (Ultrasound)</font> typically reveals large, echogenic kidneys with poor corticomedullary differentiation.
- <font color="red">ARPKD Hypertension</font> is systemic and common in all patients, even those with normal renal function.

### Other Inherited Cystic Diseases

- <font color="red">Tuberous Sclerosis (TS)</font> renal findings most commonly include <font color="red">Angiomyolipomas</font> (often bilateral/multiple and prone to bleeding if >4 cm).
- <font color="red">TSC2 Gene</font> is adjacent to `PKD1`; deletions can lead to a contiguous gene syndrome with features of both ADPKD and TS.
- <font color="red">Von Hippel-Lindau (VHL) Disease</font> is an autosomal dominant cancer syndrome where kidney manifestations include multiple bilateral cysts and a high risk of <font color="red">Renal Cell Carcinoma (RCC)</font>.
- <font color="red">Autosomal Dominant Tubulointerstitial Kidney Disease (ADTKD)</font> is characterized by progressive kidney failure, a benign urine sediment, and kidneys that are usually small and fibrotic rather than enlarged.
- <font color="red">ADTKD-UMOD</font> (MCKD II) is frequently associated with <font color="red">Hyperuricemia and Gout</font> in early adulthood. *
- <font color="red">ADTKD-MUC1</font> (MCKD I) patients typically do *not* have elevated uric acid levels.
- <font color="red">Nephronophthisis (NPHP)</font> is the most common inherited childhood form of kidney failure requiring replacement therapy.
- <font color="red">NPHP Presentation</font> includes small fibrotic kidneys, medullary cysts, and a lack of significant proteinuria or active urine sediment.
- <font color="red">Senior-Loken Syndrome</font> is defined as the clinical combination of NPHP and <font color="red">Retinitis Pigmentosa</font>.
- <font color="red">Bardet-Biedl Syndrome (BBS)</font> features truncal obesity, polydactyly, retinal dystrophy, and NPHP-like kidney phenotype.
- <font color="red">Medullary Sponge Kidney (MSK)</font> is usually a sporadic (not inherited) benign condition characterized by cystic dilatation of the collecting ducts, often appearing incidentally on imaging.
- <font color="red">MSK Complications</font> include an increased frequency of <font color="red">Calcium Phosphate/Oxalate stones</font> and recurrent UTIs.
- <font color="red">CAKUT</font> (Congenital Abnormalities of the Kidney and Urinary Tract) accounts for more than one-third of end-stage kidney disease in children.

### High-Yield Comparisons for Exams

1. In <font color="red">ADPKD</font>, the kidneys are markedly enlarged, whereas in <font color="red">ADTKD</font> or <font color="red">NPHP</font>, the kidneys are typically small and fibrotic.
2. <font color="red">PKD1</font> mutations cause more severe ADPKD with earlier onset of ESRD (~54 years) compared to the milder <font color="red">PKD2</font> (~74 years).
3. <font color="red">Uric Acid Stones</font> are the predominant stone type in ADPKD, whereas the general population more commonly forms Calcium Oxalate stones.
4. <font color="red">ADTKD-UMOD</font> presents with early-onset gout/hyperuricemia, while <font color="red">ADTKD-MUC1</font> has a similar renal course but lacks the gout/uric acid finding.
5. <font color="red">Liver Cysts</font> are common in ADPKD but do not usually result in liver failure; conversely, <font color="red">ARPKD</font> liver involvement involves CHF and biliary dysgenesis that can lead to portal hypertension.
6. <font color="red">ICA Screening</font> is specifically indicated for ADPKD patients with a positive family history of aneurysms, not for everyone with ADPKD.
7. <font color="red">ADPKD Infections</font> are often gram-negative; lipid-soluble antibiotics are required to penetrate the cysts.
8. <font color="red">Potter Sequence</font> (oligohydramnios, pulmonary hypoplasia, limb deformities) is a classic presentation of <font color="red">ARPKD</font> in utero/neonatally.
9. <font color="red">Angiomyolipomas</font> are the "most common" renal finding in Tuberous Sclerosis, distinguishing it from ADPKD where simple cysts predominate.
10. <font color="red">VHL</font> is strongly associated with Renal Cell Carcinoma (RCC), necessitating annual CT/MRI screening.
11. <font color="red">NPHP</font> often presents with salt-wasting and polyuria in children, whereas ADPKD is usually asymptomatic until adulthood.
12. <font color="red">MSK</font> shows a "medullary blush" on IVP (historically) due to dilated collecting ducts, unlike the discrete "grape-like" cysts of ADPKD.
13. <font color="red">Tolvaptan</font> is used for ADPKD, while <font color="red">Everolimus</font> (mTOR inhibitor) is specifically approved for TS-associated kidney tumors.
14. <font color="red">NPHP and ARPKD</font> are Both Autosomal Recessive, but NPHP kidneys are usually small while ARPKD kidneys are massively enlarged.

16. <font color="red">ADPKD protein PC1</font> acts as a GPCR-like receptor, while <font color="red">PC2</font> acts as a calcium-permeable TRP channel.
17. <font color="red">Proteinuria</font> is generally mild or absent in most hereditary cystic diseases; if nephrotic-range proteinuria is present, consider secondary glomerulosclerosis.
18. <font color="red">ARPKD cysts</font> are derived from the collecting ducts, whereas <font color="red">ADPKD cysts</font> can derive from any part of the nephron.
19. <font color="red">Renal Stones</font> in MSK are treated the same as stones in the general population, but patients may have reduced concentrating ability.
20. <font color="red">ADPLD</font> (Autosomal Dominant Polycystic Liver Disease) caused by `PRKCSH/SEC63` does *not* typically progress to renal failure, unlike the liver cysts seen in ADPKD.
