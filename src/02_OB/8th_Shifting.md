### GENERAL PRINCIPLES & PHYSIOLOGY
- <font color="red">Progesterone</font> in pregnancy causes decreased GI motility and decreased LES tone, which can lead to constipation and GERD.
- <font color="red">Estrogen</font> in pregnancy increases nausea and alters bile composition, predisposing the mother to <font color="red">gallstones</font>.
- <font color="red">hCG</font> peaks in the 1st trimester and is the primary hormone linked to pregnancy-related vomiting.
- <font color="red">Epigastric pain</font> in the 3rd trimester is a "red flag" that requires checking blood pressure and liver enzymes to rule out preeclampsia/HELLP.
- <font color="red">Alkaline phosphatase</font> levels naturally rise 3-4 fold during normal pregnancy due to placental production, not liver damage.
- <font color="red">AST, ALT, and Bilirubin</font> levels do **NOT** change in a normal pregnancy; any elevation is considered pathologic.
- <font color="red">Serum Albumin</font> and total protein concentrations decrease during normal pregnancy due to hemodilution.
- <font color="red">Gastrointestinal disorders</font> in the Philippines are often complicated by high rates of anemia, malnutrition, and parasitic infections.

### DIAGNOSTIC TECHNIQUES & NUTRITION
- <font color="red">Upper GI endoscopy</font> is considered safe for diagnosis and management during pregnancy when indicated.
- <font color="red">Flexible sigmoidoscopy</font> is the preferred method for visualizing the large bowel in pregnant women.
- <font color="red">Polyethylene glycol</font> is used for bowel prep; maternal dehydration must be avoided to maintain <font color="red">uteroplacental perfusion</font>.
- <font color="red">ERCP</font> is useful for diagnosing and treating <font color="red">choledocholithiasis</font> and pancreatic issues in pregnancy.
- <font color="red">Abdominal sonography</font> is the ideal first-line technique for GI imaging due to its lack of radiation.
- <font color="red">Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)</font> is the preferred modality for viewing the retroperitoneal space and diagnosing appendicitis without radiation.
- <font color="red">Laparoscopy</font> is the preferred surgical approach in the 1st and 2nd trimesters; it carries a risk of <font color="red">preterm labor</font> in the 3rd trimester.
- <font color="red">Enteral nutrition</font> (nasogastric tube) is always preferred over parenteral nutrition because it has fewer complications.
- <font color="red">Central Parenteral Nutrition (CPN)</font> is reserved for conditions like <font color="red">short bowel syndrome</font> and requires central venous access for hyperosmolar solutions.

### UPPER GI DISORDERS: KEY FACTS
- <font color="red">Hyperemesis Gravidarum</font> can cause <font color="red">Wernicke Encephalopathy</font> due to thiamine deficiency, characterized by the triad of ocular signs, confusion, and ataxia.
- <font color="red">Vitamin K deficiency</font> in hyperemesis can lead to maternal coagulopathy and <font color="red">fetal intracranial hemorrhage</font>.
- <font color="red">Ondansetron (Zofran)</font> should be reserved for cases after 8 weeks' gestation due to risks of prolonged QT interval and serotonin syndrome.
- <font color="red">Boerhaave Syndrome</font> is a serious esophageal rupture caused by sustained, forceful retching in hyperemesis.
- <font color="red">Mallory-Weiss tears</font> are small mucosal tears at the GE junction causing upper GI bleeding after persistent vomiting.
- <font color="red">Diaphragmatic hernia</font> repair is recommended *during* pregnancy, even if asymptomatic, because the maternal mortality rate of rupture is approximately 45%.
- <font color="red">Vaginal delivery</font> is generally contraindicated in unrepaired diaphragmatic hernias due to the risk of rupture from increased intraabdominal pressure.
- <font color="red">Hiatal hernias</font> are found in 20% of multiparas in late pregnancy and may cause vomiting or epigastric pain.

### INTESTINAL & COLONIC DISORDERS: KEY FACTS
- <font color="red">Acute diarrhea</font> evaluation is required if it lasts >48 hours, involves fever >38°C, or grossly bloody stools.
- <font color="red">Loperamide (Imodium)</font> should be avoided in acute diarrhea if the etiology is unknown, as it may prolong the presence of toxins.
- <font color="red">Clostridioides difficile</font> is the most common nosocomial infection and is typically triggered by <font color="red">aminopenicillins or cephalosporins</font>.
- <font color="red">Fecal calprotectin</font> is a valid inflammatory biomarker used to identify IBD flares in pregnant patients.
- <font color="red">Toxic megacolon</font> is a catastrophic complication of Ulcerative Colitis that may necessitate an emergency colectomy.
- <font color="red">Bowel obstruction</font> in pregnancy is most commonly caused by the growing uterus exerting pressure on <font color="red">pre-existing adhesions</font>.
- <font color="red">Tocolytics</font> are not recommended for contractions following appendectomy due to the high risk of <font color="red">pulmonary edema</font> in the setting of sepsis.

### HEPATIC, BILIARY, & VIRAL DISORDERS: KEY FACTS
- <font color="red">Acute Liver Failure</font> in the non-pregnant population is most commonly caused by <font color="red">Acetaminophen toxicity</font>.
- <font color="red">Spider angiomata</font> and palmar erythema occur in 2/3 of normal pregnancies due to high estrogen levels and are not necessarily signs of liver disease.
- <font color="red">Intrahepatic Cholestasis of Pregnancy (ICP)</font> increases the risk of fetal death if bile acid levels exceed <font color="red">100 µmol/L</font> due to cardiotoxicity.
- <font color="red">Vaginal delivery</font> is the preferred mode for liver failure patients to minimize incision-related bleeding, as the liver produces necessary coagulation factors.
- <font color="red">Hepatitis B</font> screening should be done for **ALL** pregnant women at the first visit; it is a DNA virus.
- <font color="red">HBsAg</font> is the first serologic marker to appear in an acute Hepatitis B infection.
- <font color="red">Vertical transmission</font> of Hep B is highest (90%) if the mother is <font color="red">HBeAg positive</font> at delivery.
- <font color="red">Neonatal intervention</font> for Hep B involves giving the infant both <font color="red">HBIG</font> and the Hep B vaccine series within 12 hours of birth.
- <font color="red">Hepatitis C</font> vertical transmission is low (<5%) but higher if the mother is coinfected with <font color="red">HIV</font>.
- <font color="red">Hepatitis A</font> does **NOT** cause birth defects and maternal-fetal transmission has not been observed.
- <font color="red">Non-Alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease (NAFLD)</font> is the most common chronic liver disease and is linked to obesity and metabolic syndrome.
- <font color="red">Cholelithiasis</font> (gallstones) is more common in pregnancy because gallbladder fasting and residual volumes **double** after the 1st trimester.
- <font color="red">Bile sludge</font> often regresses spontaneously after delivery.
- <font color="red">Cholecystectomy</font> is safe in all trimesters, but prophylactic removal of asymptomatic stones is **NOT** warranted.

### DIFFERENTIAL COMPARISONS FOR EXAMS
- <font color="red">Hyperemesis Gravidarum</font> vs. <font color="red">Normal Morning Sickness</font>: HG involves weight loss >5% and ketonuria; morning sickness typically resolves by 16 weeks and doesn't cause dehydration.
- <font color="red">Acute Fatty Liver (AFLP)</font> vs. <font color="red">HELLP Syndrome</font>: AFLP is characterized by severe <font color="red">hypoglycemia</font> and prolonged clotting times/hypofibrinogenemia; HELLP focus is on hemolysis and low platelets. 
- <font color="red">ICP</font> vs. <font color="red">Viral Hepatitis</font>: ICP presents with <font color="red">pruritus</font> and high bile acids with minimal transaminase elevation; Hepatitis presents with malaise and very high transaminases (>1000 U/L).
- <font color="red">Ulcerative Colitis</font> vs. <font color="red">Crohn Disease</font>: UC is continuous and involves only the mucosa/submucosa of the colon; Crohn's is patchy, transmural, and can affect the small bowel (distal ileum).
- <font color="red">pANCA</font> vs. <font color="red">ASCA</font>: pANCA is associated with Ulcerative Colitis (70%); ASCA (Anti-S. cerevisiae) is associated with Crohn Disease (50%).
- <font color="red">Boerhaave Syndrome</font> vs. <font color="red">Mallory-Weiss Tear</font>: Boerhaave is a full-thickness esophageal **rupture** (emergency); Mallory-Weiss reflects linear mucosal **tears** (usually self-limiting bleeding).
- <font color="red">Epigastric Pain in 3rd Trimester</font>: If BP is high, think <font color="red">Preeclampsia/HELLP</font>; if BP is normal and patient has fatty food intolerance, think <font color="red">Cholecystitis</font>.
- <font color="red">Progesterone Effects</font> vs. <font color="red">Mechanical Effects</font>: Progesterone causes decreased LES tone (GERD) and slow GI transit; the enlarging uterus increases pressure on the stomach and displaces the appendix.
- <font color="red">HBsAg</font> vs. <font color="red">Anti-HBs</font>: HBsAg indicates **infection** (active/carrier); Anti-HBs indicates **immunity** (previous infection or vaccination).
- <font color="red">HBeAg</font> vs. <font color="red">Anti-HBe</font>: HBeAg indicates high viral replication and **high infectivity**; Anti-HBe suggests lower viral titers.
- <font color="red">Enteral</font> vs. <font color="red">Parenteral Nutrition</font>: Enteral is via the GI tract (preferred); Parenteral is via the veins (used only if the GI tract must remain "quiescent").
- <font color="red">PPN</font> vs. <font color="red">CPN</font>: PPN is short-term and peripheral; CPN is long-term, uses 24-40 kcal/kg/day, and requires high-flow central veins for hypertonic solutions.
- <font color="red">Appendicitis Diagnosis</font>: Ultrasound is first-line to rule out OB causes; MRI is the **gold standard** for definitive diagnosis in pregnancy.
- <font color="red">Treatment of C. diff</font>: Oral <font color="red">Vancomycin</font> is 1st line; avoid Loperamide which can worsen the infection.
- <font color="red">Hepatitis Transmission</font>: Hep A is fecal-oral; Hep B and C are parenteral (blood/body fluids).
- <font color="red">Management of Bile Stones</font>: Symptomatic gallstones = Laparoscopic Cholecystectomy; Asymptomatic gallstones = Observation until postpartum.
- <font color="red">Achalasia Appearance</font>: Barium swallow shows a "bird beak" narrowing; GERD does not show this specific narrowing unless a stricture has formed.
- <font color="red">Methotrexate</font>: Excellent for ectopic pregnancy but strictly **contraindicated** for IBD management in a viable pregnancy due to teratogenicity.
- <font color="red">Delivery in IBD</font>: Vaginal delivery is the goal; Cesarean is only preferred if active **perianal disease** (fistulas/abscesses) is present in Crohn's.
- <font color="red">Delivery in ICP</font>: Routine induction at 38 weeks; if jaundice or bile acids are >100, deliver at 36 weeks.
- <font color="red">Management of HG</font>: Diclegis is the 1st step; if intractable, use IV fluids with <font color="red">Thiamine</font> **before** any glucose-containing fluids to avoid Wernicke's.

### PHYSIOLOGIC ADAPTATION IN PREGNANCY

| Feature | Physiologic Change in Pregnancy |
| --- | --- |
| **Pathogenesis** | Plasma volume expands by 40-50%, while red blood cell mass increases only by 15-25%, leading to hemodilution. |
| **Clinical Manifestations** | Physiologic anemia occurs; whole blood viscosity decreases; maternal heart rate and stroke volume increase. |
| **Laboratory Findings** | Hct decreases from ~38-45% to ~34% (singleton) or ~30% (multifetal); fibrinogen levels double by term. |
| **Unique Findings** | Maternal <font color="red">hepcidin</font> is profoundly decreased to augment iron absorption and fetal transport. |

- (Maternal Physiology) <font color="red">Plasma volume expansion</font> by 40-50% disproportionately exceeds the 15-25% increase in red cell mass, resulting in hemodilution and physiologic anemia.
- (Maternal Physiology) <font color="red">Hematocrit levels</font> typically decrease during pregnancy to an average of 34% in singleton pregnancies and 30% in multifetal gestations.
- (Maternal Physiology) <font color="red">Oxygen-carrying capacity</font> remains normal during pregnancy despite hemodilution and physiologic anemia.
- (Maternal Physiology) <font color="red">Whole blood viscosity</font> decreases during pregnancy due to the decline in hemoglobin and hematocrit concentrations.
- (Maternal Physiology) <font color="red">Hemoglobin concentration</font> at term is normally 12.5 g/dL; values below 11.0 g/dL are considered abnormal and usually signify iron deficiency.
- (Maternal Physiology) <font color="red">Maternal blood volume</font> expansion reaches a plateau during the last weeks of pregnancy after rising most rapidly during the midtrimester.
- (Maternal Physiology) <font color="red">Iron requirements</font> increase from 1 mg/day in non-pregnant women to approximately 7 mg/day in the third trimester to support the fetus, placenta, and expanded maternal red cell mass.
- (Maternal Physiology) <font color="red">Total iron requirement</font> for a healthy normal pregnancy is approximately 1000 mg, with 300 mg transferred to the fetus/placenta and 200 mg lost through normal excretion.
- (Maternal Physiology) <font color="red">Maternal hepcidin</font> levels are suppressed 10-fold in the second trimester compared to the first to facilitate greater iron absorption via ferroportin in enterocytes.
- (Maternal Physiology) <font color="red">Coagulation factors</font> VII, VIII, IX, and X increase during pregnancy, creating a physiologic hypercoagulable state to prevent hemorrhage.
- (Maternal Physiology) <font color="red">Fibrinogen levels</font> double by term (reaching up to 600+ mg/dL), while fibrinolysis is simultaneously inhibited.
- (Maternal Physiology) <font color="red">Protein S</font> functional activity significantly decreases during pregnancy (dropping from ~65-140% to ~16-42% by the 3rd trimester), contributing to the prothrombotic state.
- (Maternal Physiology) <font color="red">Platelet counts</font> decrease from the mid-second to third trimester due to dilution, increased spleen size (50% increase), and pooling in the intervillous space.
- (Maternal Physiology) <font color="red">Left ventricular mass</font> increases throughout pregnancy, beginning at 26 to 30 weeks' gestation.
- (Maternal Physiology) <font color="red">Arterial blood pressure</font> usually declines to a nadir at 24 to 26 weeks, with diastolic pressure decreasing more than systolic pressure.

| **1st Trimester** | Hemoglobin < 11.0 g/dL |
| **2nd Trimester** | Hemoglobin < 10.5 g/dL |
| **3rd Trimester** | Hemoglobin < 11.0 g/dL |

- (Anemia Diagnosis) <font color="red">Anemia</font> is defined as a decreased blood concentration of hemoglobin, regardless of cause, morphology, or RBC function.
- (Anemia Diagnosis) <font color="red">Anemia thresholds</font> for pregnant women differ by trimester: <11 g/dL in the first/third and <10.5 g/dL in the second trimester according to the CDC.
- (Anemia Diagnosis) <font color="red">Microcytic anemia evaluation</font> requires measuring serum ferritin and performing hemoglobin electrophoresis to differentiate iron deficiency from hemoglobinopathies.
- (Anemia Diagnosis) <font color="red">Macrocytic anemia evaluation</font> primarily involves checking serum folate and vitamin B12 levels.
- (Acute Blood Loss) <font color="red">Oral iron therapy</font> can be provided for three months to a moderately anemic woman (Hgb ~7 g/dL) who is hemodynamically stable, able to ambulate, and not septic.

### IRON DEFICIENCY ANEMIA (IDA)

| Category | Features of Iron Deficiency Anemia (IDA) |
| --- | --- |
| **Pathogenesis** | Insufficient iron to meet the ~1000 mg demand of pregnancy, fetal transfer, and red cell mass expansion. |

| **Unique Findings** | **Most common** cause of anemia in pregnancy; fetal iron uptake is preserved even if the mother is severely anemic. |

- (Iron Deficiency Anemia) <font color="red">Iron deficiency anemia</font> is the **most common** cause of anemia in pregnancy, resulting from an inability to meet the 1000 mg requirement.
- (Iron Deficiency Anemia) <font color="red">Serum ferritin</font> levels below 10–15 μg/L specifically confirm iron-deficiency anemia in gravidas.
- (Iron Deficiency Anemia) <font color="red">Ferritin</font> acts as an acute phase reactant, so levels up to 100 ng/mL may still be compatible with IDA in the presence of inflammation or infection.
- (Iron Deficiency Anemia) <font color="red">Erythropoietin (EPO)</font> synthesis increases in pregnancy due to hypoxia sensed by the kidneys, supporting RBC mass expansion.
- (Iron Deficiency Anemia) <font color="red">Erythroferrone (ERFE)</font> is upregulated by EPO to sequester hepcidin-inducer BMP6, thereby lowering hepcidin and increasing iron flow.
- (Iron Deficiency Anemia) <font color="red">Red cell distribution width (RDW)</font> is high in IDA, reflecting significant anisopoikilocytosis on a peripheral smear.

- (Iron Deficiency Anemia) <font color="red">Maternal complications</font> of IDA include increased risk of infections, preterm labor, PPROM, postpartum hemorrhage, and increased mortality.
- (Iron Deficiency Anemia) <font color="red">Fetal complications</font> of maternal IDA include low birth weight (LBW), IUGR, and impaired long-term neurodevelopment.

### HIGH-YIELD DIFFERENTIATION AND COMPARISON

- (Comparison: TTP vs. HELLP) <font color="red">ADAMTS13 deficiency (<10%)</font> is the hallmark of **TTP**, while it is only mild-to-moderately reduced in HELLP.
- (Comparison: Delivery effect) <font color="red">Delivery</font> reverses the pathology of HELLP and Preeclampsia, but has **no effect** on the progression of TMA/TTP.
- (Comparison: Thalassemia Types) <font color="red">Alpha-thalassemia</font> is more common in Asian Americans, while **Beta-thalassemia** is diagnosed by elevated HbA2 levels.
- (Comparison: Hemophilia vs. vWD) <font color="red">Hemophilia</font> is X-linked (mostly males affected), whereas **von Willebrand disease** is autosomal (affects males and females equally).

- (Comparison: Thrombophilia risk) <font color="red">Heterozygous Factor V Leiden</font> is a low-risk thrombophilia, while **Homozygous Factor V Leiden** is a high-risk condition.
- (Comparison: Relative vs. Absolute Polycythemia) <font color="red">Relative polycythemia</font> is caused by volume loss (dehydration/diuretics), while **Absolute polycythemia** (Polycythemia Vera) involves a JAK2 mutation and increased RBC mass.

## Summary

### DIABETES MELLITUS IN PREGNANCY: OVERVIEW AND COMPARISON

| Feature | <font color="red">Type 1 Diabetes</font> | <font color="red">Type 2 Diabetes</font> | <font color="red">Gestational Diabetes (GDM)</font> | <font color="red">Overt Diabetes</font> |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Pathogenesis** | Pancreatic $\beta$-cell destruction leading to absolute insulin deficiency. | Ranges from insulin resistance to insulin secretory defect with resistance. | Pregnancy-induced insulin resistance with inadequate $\beta$-cell compensation. | Likely pre-existing Type 2 DM undocumented before pregnancy. |
| **Onset** | Usually clinically apparent before age 30. | Often related to lifestyle, obesity, and heredofamilial factors. | Onset or first recognition during pregnancy, typically 24-28 weeks. | Discovered for the 1st time during pregnancy, usually in the 1st trimester. |
| **Typical Screening** | Identified before reproductive age. | Identified before or early in pregnancy. | Universal screening at 24-28 weeks (or earlier if high risk). | Screened at first prenatal visit via FBS. |
| **Diagnosis Criteria** | Standard non-pregnant criteria. | Standard non-pregnant criteria. | 75-g OGTT: FBS $\geq$ 92, 1h $\geq$ 180, or 2h $\geq$ 153 (IADPSG). | FBS $\geq$ 126 mg/dL OR HbA1c $\geq$ 6.5% OR RPG $\geq$ 200 mg/dL. |
| **Management** | Insulin always required. | MNT, then oral agents or insulin. | MNT trial for 2 weeks; Insulin if uncontrolled. | Immediate pharmacological management (Insulin/Metformin). |

---

### EPIDEMIOLOGY AND RISK FACTORS

*   <font color="red">Filipino women</font> are considered a high-risk ethnic group for hyperglycemia in pregnancy, with an estimated prevalence of 6-20% in the Philippines.
*   <font color="red">Southeast Asian race</font> is a significant risk factor as this population has the highest global risk for hyperglycemia.
*   <font color="red">History of Macrosomia</font> (defined as a birthweight of $\geq$ 9 lbs or 4 kg) is a major risk factor for Gestational Diabetes Mellitus and may indicate undiagnosed GDM in previous pregnancies.
*   <font color="red">Advanced Maternal Age</font> (e.g., 36 years old) increases the risk of developing glucose intolerance during pregnancy.
*   <font color="red">Obesity</font> (BMI $\geq$ 30 or BMI $\geq$ 25 in Asians) contributes to GDM through central adiposity and the release of leptin and proinflammatory cytokines.
*   <font color="red">Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS)</font> is a pertinent risk factor for GDM due to its underlying insulin resistance.
*   <font color="red">History of unexplained stillbirths or abortions</font> is a clinical indicator for screening, as increased sugar can cause a lack of oxygen and sudden cessation of the fetal heartbeat.
*   <font color="red">Persistent glycosuria</font> (measured as +3 or +4 glucose in urine) warrants immediate investigation for diabetes in pregnancy.
*   <font color="red">Strong family history</font> of diabetes is a critical factor; if a direct relative has DM, the chance of the patient developing it is significantly high.

### MATERNAL AND FETAL PATHOPHYSIOLOGY

*   <font color="red">Pregnancy-related hormones</font> (Progesterone, Placentally derived Growth Hormone, Prolactin, Cortisol) are elevated in the 2nd trimester and naturally increase insulin resistance to maintain fetal growth.
*   <font color="red">Maternal metabolic adaptations</font> to pregnancy include fasting hypoglycemia, postprandial hyperglycemia, and hyperinsulinemia.
*   <font color="red">Proinflammatory cytokines</font> (TNF-alpha, IL-6, IGF-1) evoke insulin resistance during pregnancy, contributing to the pathogenesis of GDM.
*   <font color="red">Maternal hyperglycemia</font> leads to the transfer of excess glucose to the fetus, which stimulates the fetal pancreas to secrete its own insulin.
*   <font color="red">Fetal hyperinsulinemia</font> acts as a growth hormone, promoting fat deposition and excessive anabolism, leading to <font color="red">Fetal Macrosomia</font>.
*   <font color="red">Aerobic metabolism of glucose</font> consumes oxygen; extreme maternal hyperglycemia can lead to fetal hypoxemia because the baby uses excessive oxygen to process high glucose levels.
*   <font color="red">Placental development</font> is usually fully established by the 24-28th week, which is why screening is most effective at this interval to detect placentally-derived glucose elevation.

### SCREENING AND DIAGNOSTIC CRITERIA (IADPSG)

*   <font color="red">Universal screening</font> for GDM is recommended for all pregnant women, regardless of risk, because the Filipino race itself is a high-risk factor.
*   <font color="red">First prenatal visit screening</font> aims to categorize patients: FBS $\leq$ 92 mg% is normal, 92-125 mg% is GDM, and $\geq$ 126 mg% is overt DM.
*   <font color="red">Low-risk patients</font> should undergo a 75-g OGTT at 24-28 weeks gestation.
*   <font color="red">High-risk patients</font> (obese, family history) should receive an immediate 75-g OGTT at the first visit, even if the initial FBS is normal.
*   <font color="red">75-g OGTT Procedure (IADPSG)</font> requires 6-8 hours of fasting, blood collection at baseline, 1 hour, and 2 hours after drinking the glucose solution.
*   <font color="red">IADPSG Diagnostic Thresholds</font> for GDM (75-g OGTT) require only **ONE** value to be met or exceeded:
    1.  Fasting Blood Sugar (FBS) $\geq$ 92 mg/dL
    2.  1-hour postprandial $\geq$ 180 mg/dL
    3.  2-hour postprandial $\geq$ 153 mg/dL
*   <font color="red">Overt Diabetes</font> in pregnancy is diagnosed if any of the following are met: FBS $\geq$ 126 mg/dL, HbA1c $\geq$ 6.5%, or Random Plasma Glucose $\geq$ 200 mg/dL.
*   <font color="red">75-g OGTT at 32 weeks</font> should be repeated if signs of DM appear, such as polyhydramnios, macrosomia, polyphagia, polydipsia, or polyuria.

### MANAGEMENT AND GLYCOSE TARGETS

*   <font color="red">Medical Nutrition Therapy (MNT)</font> is the first-line management for 2 weeks; it was formerly known as Diet Restriction Management.
*   <font color="red">Pinggang Pinoy</font> and small, frequent meals are encouraged to decrease the incidence of glucose spikes.
*   <font color="red">MNT Dietary Composition</font> (ACOG) suggests 33–40% carbohydrates, ~20% protein, and ~40% fat. Note that low carbohydrate intake may lead to IUGR.
*   <font color="red">Insulin</font> is the "Gold Standard" for pharmacological management and is initiated if target glucose levels are not achieved through MNT and exercise.
*   <font color="red">Insulin safety</font> in pregnancy is high because it does **NOT** cross the placenta.
*   <font color="red">Self-Monitored Capillary Blood Glucose (CBG) Targets</font>:
    *   Fasting $\leq$ 95 mg/dL
    *   1-hour postprandial $\leq$ 140 mg/dL
    *   2-hour postprandial $\leq$ 120 mg/dL
*   <font color="red">Metformin</font> is considered a second-line option for pharmacological management in selected cases.
*   <font color="red">Folic Acid</font> supplementation should be 5 mg/day (lecturer) or 400 $\mu$g/day (book) given periconceptionally to reduce the risk of neural tube defects.
*   <font color="red">Weight Gain Guidelines</font> are based on BMI: Obese (BMI $\ge$ 30) should only gain 5-9 kg total (0.21 kg/week in 2nd/3rd trimesters).

### ANTENATAL SURVEILLANCE AND COMPLICATIONS

*   <font color="red">Congenital Heart Defects</font> are the most common congenital anomaly affected by high maternal blood sugar before conception.
*   <font color="red">Congenital Anomaly Scan (CAS) with fetal ECHO</font> is specifically indicated at 20-24 weeks for diabetic pregnancies to monitor for cardiac and CNS anomalies.
*   <font color="red">Fetal Kick Counting</font> should begin at 26-28 weeks every night; a normal count is 10 movements in 2 hours.
*   <font color="red">Biophysical Profile (BPS)</font> should be performed every 2 weeks starting at 28-37 weeks, increasing to twice a week if the patient is on insulin.
*   <font color="red">Aspirin</font> (60-150 mg/day) is given from 12-16 weeks until delivery as prophylaxis for preeclampsia.
*   <font color="red">Shoulder Dystocia</font> and birth trauma (e.g., Clavicular fracture) are major labor complications due to fetal macrosomia and enlargement of the fetal trunk.
*   <font color="red">Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS)</font> in neonates occurs because elevated sugar levels delay the formation of lung surfactant.
*   <font color="red">Neonatal metabolic complications</font> include hypoglycemia, hypocalcemia, and hypomagnesemia.
*   <font color="red">Neonatal polycythemia</font> (increased hematocrit) is caused by hyperglycemia-induced increases in blood cell formation.
*   <font color="red">Hydramnios / Polyhydramnios</font> is caused by hyperglycemia increasing osmolality, which leads to a transfer of fluids and increased amniotic fluid.

### DELIVERY AND POSTNATAL CARE

*   <font color="red">Timing of delivery for well-controlled GDM</font> is usually planned at 39 to 39 6/7 weeks.
*   <font color="red">Timing of delivery for insulin-treated GDM</font> is recommended at 38-39 weeks.
*   <font color="red">Poorly controlled GDM</font> requires earlier delivery at 37-38 weeks.
*   <font color="red">Elective Cesarean Section</font> should be considered if the estimated fetal weight is $\geq$ 4500 g to prevent brachial plexus injury.
*   <font color="red">Postpartum glucose metabolism assessment</font> involves a repeat 75-g, 2-hr OGTT at 6-12 weeks after delivery.
*   <font color="red">Postpartum classification</font> uses non-pregnant thresholds: DM if FBS $\geq$ 126 mg/dL or 2-hr $\geq$ 200 mg/dL.
*   <font color="red">GDM recurrence</font> risk is significant, with a 50-75% likelihood of developing Type 2 DM within 15-25 years.

### DIFFERENTIATING SIMILAR ENTITIES IN EXAMS

1.  <font color="red">GDM vs. Overt DM (AOG)</font>: GDM is recognized later in pregnancy (typically after 20 weeks/2nd trimester), while Overt DM is discovered in the 1st trimester (before 20 weeks).
2.  <font color="red">GDM vs. Overt DM (FBS criteria)</font>: Overt DM requires an FBS $\geq$ 126 mg/dL; GDM is diagnosed with an FBS between 92-125 mg/dL.
3.  <font color="red">IADPSG vs. ADA Screening</font>: IADPSG requires only **ONE** abnormal value on the 75-g OGTT, whereas ADA criteria traditionally require **TWO** abnormal values.
4.  <font color="red">GDM FBS vs. Non-pregnant FBS</font>: The cutoff for fasting glucose in pregnancy is lower ($\geq$ 92 mg/dL) compared to the non-pregnant cutoff ($\geq$ 100 mg/dL/126 mg/dL).
5.  <font color="red">Macrosomia vs. LGA</font>: Macrosomia usually refers to an absolute birth weight (e.g., >4000g), while Large for Gestational Age (LGA) refers to a weight above the 90th percentile for a specific AOG.
6.  <font color="red">Fetal Kick Counting (Normal vs. Abnormal)</font>: 10 movements within 2 hours is normal; fewer movements require further biophysical evaluation.
7.  <font color="red">Insulin vs. Glucose Placental Transfer</font>: Glucose crosses the placenta easily (facilitated diffusion), causing fetal hyperglycemia; <font color="red">Insulin</font> does **NOT** cross the placenta.
8.  <font color="red">Maternal Hypoglycemia vs. Neonatal Hypoglycemia</font>: Maternal hypoglycemia occurs due to the continuous glucose draw by the fetus; Neonatal hypoglycemia occurs post-delivery because the baby still has high insulin levels but the maternal glucose supply is cut off.
9.  <font color="red">Insulin Regimens (Parkland vs. UAB)</font>: Parkland uses a split-dose mix (Breakfast: 2/3 total dose; Dinner: 1/3 total dose), while UAB uses a Basal-Bolus approach (50% long-acting at bedtime; 50% split rapid-acting before meals).
10. <font color="red">Hypertension in Pregnancy Types</font>: Pregnancy-induced hypertension and preeclampsia are both associated with GDM due to shared vascular damage mechanisms.
11. <font color="red">Polyhydramnios vs. Polyphagia</font>: Polyhydramnios is an objective sign of excess amniotic fluid; Polyphagia is a subjective symptom of excessive hunger.
12. <font color="red">Postpartum screening timing</font>: Persistent overt diabetes is checked at 1-3 days post-delivery; GDM re-classification is performed at 6-12 weeks postpartum.
13. <font color="red">CAS vs. Fetal ECHO</font>: The Congenital Anomaly Scan (CAS) is a general head-to-toe survey; the Fetal ECHO is a specialized view looking specifically for cardiac defects (the #1 anomaly).
14. <font color="red">MODY 1-6 (Obese vs. Autosomal Dominant)</font>: One form of Maturity-Onset Diabetes of the Young is common in obese adolescents, while the other is a rare autosomal dominant condition in thin young adults.
15. <font color="red">Glycolysis vs. Hypoxemia</font>: Excessive glucose metabolism (Glycolysis) in the fetus consumes oxygen; if disproportionate, it leads to <font color="red">fetal hypoxemia</font> and potential stillbirth.

### ENDOCRINE PHYSIOLOGY BULLET POINTS

* Maternal <font color="red">endocrine physiology</font> is constantly changed across pregnancy, partially due to the development of the maternal-fetal unit in the placenta which acts as a temporary gland.
* First trimester <font color="red">hCG</font> acts as a strong stimulator for TSH receptors, resulting in increased thyroid function and a mirroring decrease in TSH levels.
* Gestational <font color="red">thyrotoxicosis</font> is the clinical condition where TSH is decreased because of high hCG levels; TSH typically increases back to normal after 12 weeks as hCG decreases.
* Total <font color="red">thyroxine (T4)</font> increases early in pregnancy due to increased liver synthesis of thyroxin-binding globulin (TBG) stimulated by high placental estradiol.
* Free <font color="red">thyroxine (fT4)</font> should be maintained at the upper limit of the nonpregnant reference range to be considered normal for healthy pregnant women.
* Maternal <font color="red">thyroid hormones</font> pass through the placenta in small amounts as early as 6 weeks and are critical for embryogenesis and fetal brain development.
* Biologically inactive <font color="red">rT3</font> is produced by type III deiodinase in the placenta, which inactivates maternal T4; this activity increases in the second half of pregnancy.
* Maternal <font color="red">Thyrotropin-releasing hormone (TRH)</font> can cross the placenta and is also synthesized by the placenta to stimulate fetal pituitary thyroid function.
* Maternal <font color="red">TSH</font> does not cross the placenta and has no direct effect on the fetus.
* Fetal <font color="red">thyroid gland</font> starts functioning after 12 weeks of gestation but remains dependent on maternal thyroxine for 30% of its needs at term.
* Thyroxine-binding <font color="red">globulin (TBG)</font> levels are affected by maternal nutrition; TBG binds fT4, decreasing its bioavailability.
* The <font color="red">Pituitary Gland</font> is considered the "Master Gland," while the thyroid is the central gland in metabolic regulation.
* Regulatory <font color="red">positive feedback</font> in the HPO axis occurs when a mid-cycle surge in estrogen stimulates LH release, triggering ovulation.

### HYPERTHYROIDISM AND THYROID STORM

* Most common <font color="red">cause of thyrotoxicosis</font> in pregnancy is Graves' Disease, an autoimmune disorder featuring TSH-receptor antibodies.
* Clinical <font color="red">hyperthyroidism</font> diagnosis is suggested by excessive tachycardia, thyromegaly, exophthalmos, and weight loss despite adequate intake.
* Adverse <font color="red">pregnancy outcomes</font> linked to poorly controlled hyperthyroidism include miscarriage, preterm birth, preeclampsia, and heart failure.
* Offspring of <font color="red">hyperthyroid mothers</font> face higher risks of epilepsy and autism spectrum disorders.
* Growth <font color="red">restriction</font> occurs in hypermetabolic mothers because the mother consumes massive energy for her own metabolic processes, leaving insufficient nutrients/oxygen for the fetus.
* Fetal <font color="red">goiter</font> can occur even if the mother is euthyroid because testing only captures maternal levels and not fetal thyroid status.
* Subclinical <font color="red">hyperthyroidism</font> is characterized by low TSH and normal T4; it generally requires no antithyroid treatment due to potential fetal risks.
* Rare but life-threatening <font color="red">Thyroid Storm</font> is a hypermetabolic state that can lead to pulmonary hypertension and heart failure (cardiomyopathy).
* Propylthiouracil <font color="red">(PTU)</font> is the preferred thionamide for the first trimester because it has less placental transfer and inhibits T4 to T3 conversion, despite the FDA warning for hepatotoxicity.
* Methimazole <font color="red">(MMI)</font> is preferred after 16 weeks of gestation; its use in the first trimester is avoided due to rare embryopathy like aplasia cutis or choanal atresia.
* Switching from <font color="red">PTU to MMI</font> is done at a 20:1 dose ratio.
* Thyroidectomy <font color="red">in pregnancy</font> is only recommended in the 2nd trimester if medical therapy is toxic or fails (e.g., doses >450mg/day PTU).
* Radioactive <font color="red">iodine (RAI)</font> is strictly contraindicated in pregnancy as it can destroy the fetal thyroid gland.
* Management <font color="red">of thyroid storm</font> (Parkland Hospital protocol) involves loading PTU (1000 mg), followed by iodine (e.g., Sodium iodide/Lugol's) after 1-2 hours, and potential dexamethasone.
* Gestational <font color="red">Transient Thyrotoxicosis (GTT)</font> is caused by hCG stimulating TSH receptors; it requires no antithyroid drug treatment and normalizes by mid-pregnancy.
* Molar <font color="red">pregnancy</font> causes T4 elevation in 25-65% of cases because the excess hCG overstimulates TSH receptors; normalization follows molar evacuation.

### HYPOTHYROIDISM AND IODINE

* Hashimoto <font color="red">thyroiditis</font> is the most common cause of hypothyroidism in pregnancy, evidenced by anti-TPO antibodies.
* Overt <font color="red">hypothyroidism</font> is confirmed by an abnormally high TSH and an abnormally low T4.
* Severe <font color="red">maternal hypothyroidism</font> is associated with infertility, higher miscarriage rates, and a twofold greater risk of severe preeclampsia.
* Metabolic <font color="red">syndrome</font> is found in 36% of adult Filipinos with hypothyroidism, significantly increasing the presence of DM and hypertension.
* Levothyroxine <font color="red">dosing</font> for overt hypothyroidism is 1-2μg/kg/d (approx 100μg/day), with higher requirements starting as early as 5 weeks' gestation.
* Monitoring <font color="red">of TSH</font> during hypothyroidism treatment should occur every 4 weeks in the first half of pregnancy and at least once in the third trimester.
* Isolated <font color="red">maternal hypothyroxemia</font> features normal TSH but low free T4; current guidelines recommend against routine treatment.
* Euthyroid <font color="red">autoimmune thyroid disease</font> (positive anti-TPO with normal TSH/T4) is associated with a 2-5x increased risk of early pregnancy loss and preterm birth.
* Iodine <font color="red">requirements</font> are higher in pregnancy (220-250 μg/day) due to increased thyroid hormone production, renal losses, and fetal needs.
* Severe <font color="red">iodine deficiency</font> is associated with endemic cretinism; the ATA advises against exceeding 500 μg/day (twice the recommended intake).
* Early <font color="red">thyroxine replacement</font> is critical for newborns with Congenital Hypothyroidism to prevent neurological damage.

### THYROIDITIS, NODULES, AND CANCER

* Postpartum <font color="red">thyroiditis</font> occurs in 5-10% of women within the first year after childbirth; it presents initially as a thyrotoxic phase followed by a hypothyroid phase.
* Thyrotoxic <font color="red">phase</font> of postpartum thyroiditis (1-4 months PP) is caused by hormone release from glandular disruption and is treated with beta-blockers if symptoms are severe.
* Hypothyroid <font color="red">phase</font> of postpartum thyroiditis (4-8 months PP) is more symptomatic and requires levothyroxine (25-75 μg/day) for 6-12 months.
* Palpable <font color="red">thyroid nodules</font> found in pregnancy require Fine-needle aspiration (FNA) under ultrasound guidance as pregnancy does not affect cytological diagnosis.
* Solitary <font color="red">thyroid nodules</font> are 90-95% benign; biopsy is recommended for nodules >5 mm persistent at 3 months post-delivery.
* Surgery <font color="red">for thyroid cancer</font> is deferred until postpartum if the cancer is non-aggressive or diagnosed in the third trimester; surgery is performed in the 1st/2nd trimester for aggressive cases.
* Papillary <font color="red">thyroid cancer</font> is the most common thyroid cancer; it is usually slow-growing and non-invasive.

### PARATHYROID DISORDERS

* Calcium <font color="red">homeostasis</font> during pregnancy is maintained by Parathyroid hormone (PTH) and 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D (calcitriol).
* Elemental <font color="red">calcium</font> requirement is 1.5-2 g daily from the 20th week of gestation until the end of pregnancy (WHO).
* Primary <font color="red">hyperparathyroidism (PHPT)</font> is mostly caused by a single parathyroid adenoma; in the Philippines, it is predominantly diagnosed when overtly symptomatic.
* Hypercalcemic <font color="red">crisis</font> (Ca >14 mg/dL) is a postpartum risk of hyperparathyroidism characterized by nausea, vomiting, and mental status changes.
* Maternal <font color="red">hyperparathyroidism</font> suppresses the fetal parathyroid gland, leading to a 15-25% incidence of severe neonatal hypocalcemia/tetany after birth.
* Surgical <font color="red">removal</font> of a symptomatic parathyroid adenoma is the preferred treatment, ideally performed in the 2nd trimester.
* Emergency <font color="red">treatment</font> for severe hypercalcemia involves IV normal saline diuresis, furosemide, and calcitonin to decrease skeletal calcium release.
* Hypoparathyroidism <font color="red">symptoms</font> include neuromuscular manifestations like numbness, tingling, and muscle cramps.

### ADRENAL AND DISORDERS

* Cushing's <font color="red">syndrome</font> symptoms like hypertension and DM overlap with pregnancy findings, making late detection common.
* Active <font color="red">Cushing's syndrome</font> carries a 66% risk of preterm birth and a 52% risk of cesarean delivery.
* Adrenalectomy <font color="red">surgery</font> remains the definitive cure for Cushing's syndrome caused by an adrenal adenoma.
* Addison <font color="red">disease</font> (Adrenal Insufficiency) requires increased corticosteroid replacement (stress doses) during labor and delivery to match the normal adrenal response.

### PITUITARY DISORDERS

* Pituitary <font color="red">gland</font> enlarges by approximately one-third during normal pregnancy due to estrogen-induced lactotrophic hyperplasia.
* Sheehan <font color="red">syndrome</font> is postpartum pituitary necrosis caused by severe hemorrhage/hypovolemic shock; the earliest sign is failure of lactation.
* Prolactinoma <font color="red">is the</font> most common pituitary tumor in women of childbearing age; complications include amenorrhea, galactorrhea, and visual field defects.
* Microadenomas <font color="red">(pituitary)</font> are ≤10 mm, while Macroadenomas are >10 mm.
* Visual <font color="red">field</font> testing (bitemporal hemianopia) is used to detect tumor expansion impinging on the optic chiasm.
* Bromocriptine <font color="red">is a</font> dopamine-receptor agonist used to restore ovulation in hyperprolactinemic women and to treat symptomatic tumor enlargement during pregnancy.
* MRI <font color="red">imaging</font> is considered safe for pregnant women needing visualization of pituitary tumors.
* Acromegaly <font color="red">diagnosis</font> is confirmed by elevated IGF-1 and lack of GH suppression after a glucose load (GH <1 ug/L is normal).
* Octreotide <font color="red">and pegvisomant</font> are somatostatin and GH analogues used to treat pregnant women with acromegaly.
* Lymphocytic <font color="red">hypophysitis</font> is a rare autoimmune pituitary disorder that presents close to delivery with headaches and visual disturbances.
* Desmopressin <font color="red">(dDAVP)</font> is the standard treatment for Central Diabetes Insipidus and is considered safe for the fetus and lactating mothers.
* Water <font color="red">deprivation test</font> is used to differentiate DI types; nothing by mouth is advised during the test, with hourly measurement of urine tests/body weight.

### COMPARISON AND DIFFERENTIATION FOR EXAMS

* <font color="red">PTU vs. Methimazole</font>: PTU is used in the 1st trimester due to lower placental transfer but has hepatotoxicity risk; MMI is used in 2nd/3rd trimester due to higher potency but is avoided in the 1st trimester due to aplasia cutis risk.
* <font color="red">TRH vs. TSH</font>: TRH is produced in the placenta and crosses to the fetus; TSH does not cross the placenta.
* <font color="red">Thyroid Storm vs. Hyperemesis Gravidarum</font>: Both feature tachycardia and vomiting, but Thyroid Storm is a life-threatening hypermetabolic state with decompensation (heart failure), while HG is often transient and TSH/T4 normalize by mid-pregnancy.
* <font color="red">Graves' Disease vs. Gestational Thyrotoxicosis (GTT)</font>: Graves' usually has a pre-pregnancy history, thyromegaly, or exophthalmos and requires thionamides; GTT is transient, hCG-mediated, requires no antithyroid medications, and improves after 12 weeks.
* <font color="red">Overt Hypothyroidism vs. Subclinical Hypothyroidism</font>: Overt has high TSH and low fT4 and must be treated with levothyroxine (100μg); Subclinical has high TSH but normal fT4 and is treated selectively (e.g., if antibodies present or TSH >10).
* <font color="red">Central vs. Nephrogenic DI</font>: Central DI responds to Desmopressin (dDAVP) with increased urine osmolality; Nephrogenic DI is resistant to Desmopressin and involves the renal tubules.
* <font color="red">Diabetes Insipidus vs. Primary Polydipsia</font>: Both feature polyuria/polydipsia, but primary polydipsia is psychogenic and shows a higher response in urine osmolality during Desmopressin testing compared to organic DI.
* <font color="red">Sheehan Syndrome vs. Prolactinoma</font>: Sheehan syndrome presents with lactation failure and breast atrophy (low prolactin); Prolactinoma features galactorrhea and secondary amenorrhea (high prolactin).
* <font color="red">Cushing's Syndrome vs. Normal Pregnancy</font>: Both feature weight gain and striae; however, Cushing's is distinguished by more severe hypertension, hyperglycemia, and suppressed ACTH with high UFC.
* <font color="red">Cushing's Syndrome: Pregnant vs. Non-pregnant</font>: In pregnancy, it is mostly an adrenal adenoma; in non-pregnant adults, 60% is caused by ACTH-dependent pituitary-dependent adrenal hyperplasia.
* <font color="red">Neonatal Hyperparathyroidism vs. Hypoparathyroidism</font>: Maternal hyperparathyroidism suppresses the fetal gland, leading to transient neonatal hypoparathyroidism (severe hypocalcemia) after birth.
* <font color="red">Bitemporal Hemianopia vs. Normal Vision</font>: A patient with bitemporal hemianopia (seen in pituitary tumors) can see the medial/central portion but has lost peripheral vision.
* <font color="red">dDAVP vs. Insulin</font>: dDAVP is safe for the fetus in DI treatment; insulin for DM is also safe but requires closer management for maternal-fetal metabolic side effects.
* <font color="red">Prolactinoma: Micro vs. Macro</font>: Microadenomas are ≤10mm and rarely enlarge symptomatically in pregnancy (2.4%); Macroadenomas are >10mm and have a 21% risk of symptomatic enlargement.
* <font color="red">Postpartum Thyroiditis Phases</font>: The Thyrotoxic phase occurs 1-4 months PP (treated with Beta-blockers); the Hypothyroid phase occurs 4-8 months PP (treated with Levothyroxine).
* <font color="red">TSH-hCG mirroring</font>: In early pregnancy, as hCG rises (peaks at 12 wks), TSH falls; as hCG falls after 12 wks, TSH rises back to baseline.

### COMPREHENSIVE BULLET POINTS (REPRESENTATIVE FOR FLASHCARDS)

- In <font color="red">Mixed Connective Tissue Disease (MCTD)</font>, the Alarcon-Segovia Criteria is used for diagnosis, and high titers of <font color="red">anti-U1RNP</font> are characteristic findings.
- Patients with <font color="red">Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)</font> have placentas that are typically smaller with vascular lesions like <font color="red">decidual vasculopathy</font>, thrombosis, and infarction.
- <font color="red">Neonatal Lupus Erythematosus (NLE)</font> is caused by the transplacental passage of maternal <font color="red">anti-SSA (Ro) or anti-SSB (La)</font> autoantibodies.
- The most common manifestation of <font color="red">Neonatal Lupus (NLE)</font> is a photosensitive skin rash, which usually resolves within 3 to 6 months as maternal antibodies clear.
- A permanent and serious complication of <font color="red">Neonatal Lupus (NLE)</font> is <font color="red">congenital heart block (CHB)</font>, though the lecturer notes this was not seen in the specific Filipino study reviewed.
- <font color="red">Hydroxychloroquine (HCQ)</font> is the only maternal treatment for SLE that has been shown to lower the risk of <font color="red">Neonatal Lupus</font>.
- For pregnant women with <font color="red">Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)</font>, <font color="red">non-fluorinated corticosteroids</font> (Prednisone, Hydrocortisone) are preferred because they are inactivated by the placenta.
- <font color="red">Aspirin</font> at 81mg/day should be initiated at 12 weeks of gestation in all <font color="red">SLE</font> patients to decrease the occurrence of preeclampsia.
- <font color="red">Lupus flares</font> in pregnancy are often difficult to distinguish from preeclampsia; however, <font color="red">decreased complement levels (C3, C4)</font> and increased anti-dsDNA titers strongly suggest a flare.
- The <font color="red">Lupus Anticoagulant (LAC)</font> is the only antiphospholipid antibody consistently associated with adverse pregnancy outcomes despite being named an "anticoagulant" due to its in vitro effects.
- <font color="red">Annexin V</font> is a natural anticoagulant expressed by the syncytiotrophoblast that may be targeted by pathogenetic antibodies in <font color="red">Antiphospholipid Syndrome (APS)</font>.
- <font color="red">Catastrophic Antiphospholipid Antibody Syndrome (CAPS)</font>, also known as Asherson Syndrome, involves a "cytokine storm" affecting three or more organ systems and has a high mortality rate.
- <font color="red">Heparin</font> (UFH or LMWH) is the mainstay for thrombosis prevention in <font color="red">Antiphospholipid Syndrome (APS)</font> because it prevents cellular damage by binding to beta-2-glycoprotein-I.
- <font color="red">Warfarin</font> is strictly avoided during pregnancy in <font color="red">Antiphospholipid Syndrome (APS)</font> management because it is a Vitamin K antagonist that is teratogenic and causes fetal bleeding.
- Symptom improvement in <font color="red">Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA)</font> during pregnancy is correlated with higher serum levels of <font color="red">pregnancy-associated alpha2-glycoprotein (PAG)</font>.
- In <font color="red">Systemic Sclerosis</font>, <font color="red">Labetalol</font> must be avoided for hypertension management because it can cause <font color="red">peripheral vasospasm</font>.
- <font color="red">Captopril (ACE inhibitor)</font> is the treatment of choice if a <font color="red">Scleroderma Renal Crisis (SRC)</font> is suspected, though typically used post-natally unless life-threatening.
- <font color="red">Polyarteritis Nodosa (PAN)</font> is a necrotizing vasculitis of small and medium-sized arteries that may present peripartum with tender <font color="red">subcutaneous nodules</font> and myositis.
- <font color="red">Marfan Syndrome</font> risk is highest during the <font color="red">third trimester and postpartum</font> due to cardiovascular changes like increased stroke volume and hormonal wall changes.
- In <font color="red">Ehlers-Danlos Syndrome (EDS)</font>, patients are at high risk for <font color="red">postpartum hemorrhage (PPH)</font> and should receive prompt episiotomies to prevent irregular perineal tears.
- <font color="red">Osteogenesis Imperfecta (OI)</font> patients may experience a fracture rate increase in the third trimester due to <font color="red">pregnancy-induced bone loss</font> and transient decreases in bone mineral density.

### DIFFERENTIATING SIMILAR ENTITIES (FOR EXAMS)

- <font color="red">Lupus Flare vs. Preeclampsia</font>: In <font color="red">Lupus Flare</font>, complement levels (C3/C4) are <font color="red">decreased</font> and anti-dsDNA is increased; in Preeclampsia, complements are usually normal and anti-dsDNA is negative.
- <font color="red">Lupus Flare vs. Preeclampsia</font>: <font color="red">Urinary cellular casts</font> and hematuria are highly characteristic of an <font color="red">SLE renal flare</font>, whereas they are absent in pure preeclampsia.
- <font color="red">Lupus Flare vs. Preeclampsia</font>: <font color="red">Uric acid</font> is typically <font color="red">elevated in preeclampsia</font> but remains normal in an SLE flare.
- <font color="red">Chronic Arterial vs. Venous Insufficiency</font>: <font color="red">Arterial insufficiency</font> presents with <font color="red">decreased/absent pulses</font> and pale skin on elevation; <font color="red">Venous insufficiency</font> has normal pulses and brown skin pigmentation.
- <font color="red">Arterial vs. Venous Ulcers</font>: <font color="red">Arterial ulcers</font> occur on toes or trauma points; <font color="red">Venous ulcers</font> occur at the <font color="red">medial ankle</font>.
- <font color="red">SLE vs. RA</font>: SLE is multisystemic with hallmark <font color="red">malar rash and nephritis</font>; RA is primarily an <font color="red">inflammatory synovitis</font> of peripheral joints.
- <font color="red">APS Lab Markers</font>: <font color="red">Lupus Anticoagulant (LAC)</font> is the most predictive of pregnancy loss, while <font color="red">Anticardiolipin (ACA)</font> is directed specifically against mitochondrial/platelet membranes.
- <font color="red">OI Type I vs. Type II</font>: <font color="red">Type I</font> is the mildest form and compatible with successful pregnancy; <font color="red">Type II</font> is typically lethal in the neonatal period or in utero.
- <font color="red">EDS vs. Marfan</font>: Both involve connective tissue, but <font color="red">Marfan</font> is primarily a fibrillin-1 defect with <font color="red">aortic risks</font>, while <font color="red">EDS</font> is a collagen defect with <font color="red">skin/joint fragility risks</font>.
- <font color="red">Heparin vs. Aspirin</font>: <font color="red">Heparin</font> inhibits the coagulation cascade (common pathway); <font color="red">Aspirin</font> inhibits <font color="red">platelet aggregation</font> by blocking Thromboxane A2.
- <font color="red">Systemic Sclerosis (Limited vs. Diffuse)</font>: <font color="red">Limited (CREST)</font> is more benign and slow; <font color="red">Diffuse</font> features rapid skin and GI fibrosis.
- <font color="red">Neonatal Lupus vs. SLE</font>: <font color="red">Neonatal Lupus</font> is a passive transfer of antibodies that <font color="red">resolves</font> (except heart block); <font color="red">SLE</font> is an endogenous autoimmune disease of the patient.
- <font color="red">Prednisone vs. Dexamethasone</font>: <font color="red">Prednisone</font> is preferred for maternal SLE treatment (placenta inactivates it); <font color="red">Dexamethasone</font> is used only if the goal is to treat the fetus (crosses placenta).
- <font color="red">Labetalol in CTDs</font>: Preferred in preeclampsia but <font color="red">contraindicated in Scleroderma</font> due to potential to worsen Raynaud's/vasospasm.
- <font color="red">Thrombosis (APS)</font>: <font color="red">Arterial thrombosis</font> often presents as stroke or MI; <font color="red">Venous thrombosis</font> typically presents as DVT or PE.
